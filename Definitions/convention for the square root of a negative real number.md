The square root of a negative real number has two values: $i\sqrt{r}$ and $-i\sqrt{r}$. We need a convention that helps us avoid ambiguity any time a $\sqrt{\cdot}$ lands on a negative real. The convention works as follows:
1. For the square root when the argument is a negative real: for $r>0, \sqrt{-r}$ means the principal square root $i \sqrt{r}$ (the one with positive imaginary part). 
2. The "second root" is just the negative of the principal root, $-i \sqrt{r}$.


# Examples

$\sqrt{-7} = i\sqrt{7}$ and $-i\sqrt{7}$ because $(i \sqrt{7})^2=i^2 7= -7$ and also $(-i \sqrt{7})^2=i^2 7=-7$. $i\sqrt{7}$ is the principal root of $-7$.


**_Facts:_**
- Later, in full complex analysis, this same idea grows into the notion of a _principal branch_: for multivalued functions (like $\sqrt{z}, \log z$, or $z^\alpha$ ), you consistently pick one branch so that the function is well defined.
