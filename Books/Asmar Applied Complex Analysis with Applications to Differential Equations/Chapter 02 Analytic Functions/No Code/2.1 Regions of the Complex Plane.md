# 2.1 Regions of the Complex Plane

In the previous chapter, we defined some elementary functions of a complex variable. An important part of a function is its domain of definition. In calculus, functions were usually defined over intervals. In dealing with functions of a complex variable, intervals will be replaced by subsets of the complex plane. The picture is no longer as simple as the one on the real line. For this reason, it is necessary to understand basic properties of subsets of the complex plane, which will assist us in our analytical study of functions of a complex variable.

##### problem 1

A neighborhood of a point $z_0$ is an open disk centered at $z_0$:

$$
B_r(z_0)=\{z: |z-z_0|<r\},
\qquad r>0.
$$

The strict inequality matters because it excludes the points on the circle $|z-z_0|=r$. So a neighborhood contains only points strictly less than distance $r$ from the center, making it the analog of an open interval rather than a closed disk.

##### problem 2


Let $z \in S$. If some neighborhood of $z$ is wholly contained in $S$, then $z$ is an interior point of $S$. If no neighborhood of $z$ is wholly contained in $S$, then every neighborhood of $z$ contains a point not in $S$. Since every neighborhood of $z$ contains $z$ itself, and $z \in S$, every neighborhood of $z$ also contains at least one point of $S$. Hence every neighborhood of $z$ contains a point in $S$ and a point not in $S$, so $z$ is a boundary point. Therefore every point of $S$ is either an interior point or a boundary point.

##### problem 3

A deleted neighborhood of $z_0$ is a neighborhood with the center removed:

$$
B_r'(z_0)=\{z: 0<|z-z_0|<r\}.
$$

An ordinary neighborhood includes the center $z_0$, since $|z_0-z_0|=0<r$. A deleted neighborhood excludes that one point but keeps all the other points of the neighborhood.

One very useful definition in this book is that of a neighborhood of a point. It is the analog of an open interval in one dimension.


> [!definition] open set
> Let $r>0$ be a positive real number and $z_{0}$ a point in the plane. The $r$-neighborhood of $z_{0}$ is the set of all complex numbers $z$ satisfying
> 
> $$
> \left|z-z_{0}\right|<r .
> $$
> 
> We denote this set by $B_{r}\left(z_{0}\right)$.


By interpreting the absolute value as a distance, we see from (1) that $B_{r}\left(z_{0}\right)$ is the disk centered at $z_{0}$ with radius $r>0$. The fact that the inequality in (1) is strict tells us that we should not include the points on the circle $\left|z-z_{0}\right|=r$ as part of the $r$-neighborhood of $z_{0}$. This is indicated in Figure 1, where we used a dashed line to plot the circle $\left|z-z_{0}\right|=r$.

Sometimes we will not specify the value of $r$ and will simply refer to $B_{r}\left(z_{0}\right)$ as a neighborhood of $z_{0}$.

A neighborhood of $z_{0}$ from which we have deleted the center $z_{0}$ is called a deleted neighborhood or punctured neighborhood of $z_{0}$ and is sometimes denoted by $B_{r}^{\prime}\left(z_{0}\right)$. Thus

$$
B_{r}^{\prime}\left(z_{0}\right)=\left\{z: 0<\left|z-z_{0}\right|<r\right\} .
$$

> [!definition] interior and boundary points
> Let $S$ be a subset of the complex numbers. A point $z_{0}$ in $S$ is called an interior point of $S$ if we can find a neighborhood of $z_{0}$ that is wholly contained in $S$. A point $z$ in the complex plane is called a boundary point of $S$ if every neighborhood of $z$ contains at least one point in $S$ and at least one point not in $S$. The set of all boundary points of $S$ is called the boundary of $S$.

From the definitions, every point in $S$ is either an interior point or a boundary point. If a point is an interior point of $S$, then it cannot be a boundary point of $S$. Also, while an interior point of $S$ is necessarily a point in $S$, a boundary point of $S$ need not be in $S$.

##### problem a


> [!verbalization]
> Let $z$ be a point in the open ball $B_r\left(z_0\right)$. Then the distance from $z$ to $z_0$ is strictly less than $r$. Define $\delta$ to be $r$ minus the distance from $z$ to $z_0$. Since that distance is less than $r$, we have that $\delta$ is positive. Now choose $\delta^{\prime}$ strictly between 0 and $\delta$. We claim that the open ball of radius $\delta^{\prime}$ centered at $z$ is wholly contained in $B_r\left(z_0\right)$. Indeed, suppose $w$ lies in $B_{\delta^{\prime}}(z)$, so that the distance from $w$ to $z$ is less than $\delta^{\prime}$. By the triangle inequality, the distance from $w$ to $z_0$ is at most the distance from $w$ to $z$ plus the distance from $z$ to $z_0$; since the former is less than $\delta^{\prime}$, the total is strictly less than $\delta^{\prime}$ plus the distance from $z$ to $z_0$, which in turn is strictly less than $\delta$ plus the distance from $z$ to $z_0$. By the definition of $\delta$, this last expression equals $r$ minus the distance from $z$ to $z_0$, plus the distance from $z$ to $z_0$, which simplifies to $r$. Hence $w$ lies in $B_r\left(z_0\right)$. Therefore $B_{\delta^{\prime}}(z)$ is wholly contained in $B_r\left(z_0\right)$, so $z$ is an interior point of $B_r\left(z_0\right)$. Since $z$ was arbitrary, every point of $B_r\left(z_0\right)$ is an interior point.

$$
\left|z-z_0\right|<r .
$$


Set

$$
\delta=r-\left|z-z_0\right| .
$$


Since $\left|z-z_0\right|<r$, we have $\delta>0$. Choose $\delta^{\prime}$ such that

$$
0<\delta^{\prime}<\delta
$$


We claim that

$$
B_{\delta^{\prime}}(z) \subset B_r\left(z_0\right) .
$$


Indeed, if $w \in B_{\delta^{\prime}}(z)$, then $|w-z|<\delta^{\prime}$, so by the triangle inequality,


$$\left|w-z_0\right| \leq|w-z|+\left|z-z_0\right|$$

But

$$\begin{aligned} & \left|w-z\right|<\delta^{\prime} \\ \Rightarrow & \left|w-z\right|+\left|z-z_0\right|<\delta^{\prime}+\left|z-z_0\right|\end{aligned}$$


so

$$\left|w-z_0\right| < \delta^{\prime}+\left|z-z_0\right|$$


But 


$$\displaystyle  \begin{array}{{>{\displaystyle}l}}
0\ < \ \delta '< \delta \\
\Longrightarrow \ \delta '\ +\ |\ z\ -\ z_{0} |\ < \ \delta +\ |\ z\ -\ z_{0} |

\Longrightarrow |w\ -z_{0} |\ < \ r
\end{array}$$

##### problem b

> [!verbalization]
> First let $z$ satisfy the equation that the distance from $z$ to $z_0$ equals $r$ - that is, $z$ lies exactly on the circle of radius $r$ around $z_0$. We show that $z$ is a boundary point of $B_r\left(z_0\right)$. Let $\varepsilon$ be an arbitrary positive number; our goal is to find a point of $B_r\left(z_0\right)$ and a point outside $B_r\left(z_0\right)$, both within distance $\varepsilon$ of $z$. Choose $\eta$ to be the smaller of $\varepsilon / 2$ and $r / 2$; this ensures $\eta$ is positive, strictly less than $r$, and at most $\varepsilon / 2$. The idea is now geometric: $z$ sits on the circle, and we will nudge it slightly inward along the radial line through $z_0$ and $z$ to produce a point $w_1$ inside the ball, and slightly outward along the same line to produce a point $w_2$ outside the ball, each at distance exactly $\eta$ from $z$.
> 
> To construct $w_1$, we parameterize points on the radial line as $z_0+t\left(z-z_0\right)$, where $t$ is a scalar. When $t$ is zero, we are at $z_0$; when $t$ is one, we are at $z$; and for $t$ between zero and one, we are somewhere along the segment, at distance $t \cdot r$ from $z_0$. We want $w_1$ to sit at distance $r-\eta$ from $z_0$ - that is, $\eta$ units short of the boundary - so we need $t \cdot r$ to equal $r-\eta$, which gives $t$ equal to $1-\eta / r$. Accordingly, define $w_1$ as $z_0$ plus $(1-\eta / r)$ times the vector from $z_0$ to $z$. This scaling factor is positive because $\eta$ is less than $r$. Then the distance from $w_1$ to $z_0$ is $(1-\eta / r)$ times the distance from $z$ to $z_0$, which is $(1-\eta / r) \cdot r$, or $r-\eta$, which is strictly less than $r$. Hence $w_1$ lies in $B_r\left(z_0\right)$. The distance from $w_1$ to $z$ is $\eta / r$ times the distance from $z$ to $z_0$, which equals $\eta$, which is at most $\varepsilon / 2$ and therefore strictly less than $\varepsilon$. Thus every neighborhood of $z$ contains a point of $B_r\left(z_0\right)$.
> 
> The construction of $w_2$ follows the same logic, but now we want to travel past $z$ by $\eta$ units along the same radial direction, landing at distance $r+\eta$ from $z_0$. Setting $t \cdot r$ equal to $r+\eta$ gives $t$ equal to $1+\eta / r$. Accordingly, define $w_2$ as $z_0$ plus $(1+\eta / r)$ times the vector from $z_0$ to $z$. The distance from $w_2$ to $z_0$ is $(1+\eta / r) \cdot r$, which equals $r+\eta$, strictly greater than $r$, so $w_2$ does not lie in $B_r\left(z_0\right)$. The distance from $w_2$ to $z$ is again $\eta$, which is strictly less than $\varepsilon$. Hence every neighborhood of $z$ contains both a point in $B_r\left(z_0\right)$ and a point not in $B_r\left(z_0\right)$, so $z$ is a boundary point. 
> 
> Conversely, suppose $z$ is not on the circle. If the distance from $z$ to $z_0$ is strictly less than $r$, then by part (a), $z$ is an interior point of $B_r\left(z_0\right)$, and in particular it is not a boundary point. If the distance from $z$ to $z_0$ is strictly greater than $r$, set $\rho$ equal to that distance minus $r$, which is positive. We claim that the open ball of radius $\rho$ around $z$ lies entirely outside $B_r\left(z_0\right)$. Indeed, for any $w$ in $B_\rho(z)$, the reverse triangle inequality says that the distance from $w$ to $z_0$ is at least the absolute difference between the distance from $z$ to $z_0$ and the distance from $w$ to $z$; since the latter is less than $\rho$, this lower bound is strictly greater than the distance from $z$ to $z_0$ minus $\rho$, which by the definition of $\rho$ equals $r$. Hence $w$ lies outside $B_r\left(z_0\right)$. Therefore $z$ has a neighborhood containing no points of $B_r\left(z_0\right)$, so $z$ cannot be a boundary point. Combining the three cases, the boundary of $B_r\left(z_0\right)$ is exactly the circle where the distance from $z$ to $z_0$ equals $r$.


First let $z$ satisfy

$$
\left|z-z_0\right|=r
$$


We show that $z$ is a boundary point of $B_r\left(z_0\right)$. Let $\varepsilon>0$. Choose

$$
\eta=\min \left\{\frac{\varepsilon}{2}, \frac{r}{2}\right\} .
$$


Then $0<\eta<r$ and $\eta \leq \varepsilon / 2$.
Consider points on the radial line through $z_0$ and $z$, which can be written in the form

$$
z_0+t\left(z-z_0\right)
$$

where $t$ is a real scalar. When $t=0$, this gives $z_0$; when $t=1$, this gives $z$; and when $0<t<1$, the point lies between $z_0$ and $z$.

We first construct a point inside $B_r\left(z_0\right)$ that lies within distance $\varepsilon$ of $z$. We want a point on this line whose distance from $z_0$ is $r-\eta$. Since

$$
\left|z_0+t\left(z-z_0\right)-z_0\right|=\left|t\left(z-z_0\right)\right|=t\left|z-z_0\right|=t r
$$

we need $t r=r-\eta$, so


$$
t=1-\frac{\eta}{r}
$$


Thus define

$$
w_1=z_0+\left(1-\frac{\eta}{r}\right)\left(z-z_0\right)
$$


Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
\left|w_1-z_0\right| & =\left|\left(1-\frac{\eta}{r}\right)\left(z-z_0\right)\right| \\
& =\left(1-\frac{\eta}{r}\right)\left|z-z_0\right| \\
& =\left(1-\frac{\eta}{r}\right) r \\
& =r-\eta \\
& <r
\end{aligned}
$$


so $w_1 \in B_r\left(z_0\right)$. Also,

$$
\begin{aligned}
w_1-z & =z_0+\left(1-\frac{\eta}{r}\right)\left(z-z_0\right)-z \\
& =\left(1-\frac{\eta}{r}\right)\left(z-z_0\right)-\left(z-z_0\right) \\
& =-\frac{\eta}{r}\left(z-z_0\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

hence

$$
\begin{aligned}
\left|w_1-z\right| & =\frac{\eta}{r}\left|z-z_0\right| \\
& =\frac{\eta}{r} r \\
& =\eta \\
& \leq \frac{\varepsilon}{2} \\
& <\varepsilon
\end{aligned}
$$


Thus every neighborhood of $z$ contains a point of $B_r\left(z_0\right)$.


Now we construct a point outside $B_r\left(z_0\right)$ that also lies within distance $\varepsilon$ of $z$. We want a point on the same radial line whose distance from $z_0$ is $r+\eta$. Again writing points on the line as $z_0+t\left(z-z_0\right)$, we need $t r=r+\eta$, so

$$
t=1+\frac{\eta}{r} .
$$


Thus define

$$
w_2=z_0+\left(1+\frac{\eta}{r}\right)\left(z-z_0\right) .
$$


Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
\left|w_2-z_0\right| & =\left|\left(1+\frac{\eta}{r}\right)\left(z-z_0\right)\right| \\
& =\left(1+\frac{\eta}{r}\right)\left|z-z_0\right| \\
& =\left(1+\frac{\eta}{r}\right) r \\
& =r+\eta \\
& >r
\end{aligned}
$$

so $w_2 \notin B_r\left(z_0\right)$. Also,

$$
\begin{aligned}
w_2-z & =z_0+\left(1+\frac{\eta}{r}\right)\left(z-z_0\right)-z \\
& =\left(1+\frac{\eta}{r}\right)\left(z-z_0\right)-\left(z-z_0\right) \\
& =\frac{\eta}{r}\left(z-z_0\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

and therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
\left|w_2-z\right| & =\frac{\eta}{r}\left|z-z_0\right| \\
& =\frac{\eta}{r} r \\
& =\eta \\
& \leq \frac{\varepsilon}{2} \\
& <\varepsilon
\end{aligned}
$$


Hence every neighborhood of $z$ contains a point in $B_r\left(z_0\right)$ and a point not in $B_r\left(z_0\right)$, so $z$ is a boundary point.

Conversely, if $\left|z-z_0\right|<r$, then part (a) shows that $z$ is an interior point of $B_r\left(z_0\right)$, so $z$ is not a boundary point. If $\left|z-z_0\right|>r$, set

$$
\rho=\left|z-z_0\right|-r>0 .
$$


We claim that $B_\rho(z)$ lies entirely outside $B_r\left(z_0\right)$. Indeed, if $w \in B_\rho(z)$, then $\mid w- z \mid<\rho$, so by the reverse triangle inequality,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\left|w-z_0\right| & \geq| | z-z_0|-|w-z|| \\
& >\left|z-z_0\right|-\rho \\
& =\left|z-z_0\right|-\left(\left|z-z_0\right|-r\right) \\
& =r .
\end{aligned}
$$


So $w \notin B_r\left(z_0\right)$. Thus $z$ has a neighborhood containing no points of $B_r\left(z_0\right)$, so $z$ cannot be a boundary point.

Therefore the boundary of $B_r\left(z_0\right)$ is exactly the circle

$$
\left|z-z_0\right|=r .
$$

##### problem 1

A set $S \subset \mathbb{C}$ is open if every point of $S$ is an interior point of $S$. That means that for each $z \in S$ there exists some radius $r>0$ such that the neighborhood $B_r(z)$ is wholly contained in $S$.

The radius may depend on the point because points near the boundary usually require smaller neighborhoods than points farther inside the set. Openness only requires some positive radius for each point, not one uniform radius that works for all points.

##### problem 2

The empty set $\emptyset$ is open because the definition is vacuously true. There are no points in $\emptyset$, so there is no point for which the openness condition could fail.

##### problem 3

An open set is a set that contains none of its boundary points. A closed set is a set that contains all of its boundary points.

So boundary points distinguish the two notions: openness excludes them, while closedness includes them.

##### problem 4

A set can be open, closed, both, or neither.

The open disk $B_r(z_0)$ is open. The closed disk

$$
\{z: |z-z_0|\le r\}
$$

is closed. The sets $\mathbb{C}$ and $\emptyset$ are both open and closed because each has empty boundary. A set such as

$$
\{z: |z-z_0|\le r,\ \operatorname{Im} z>0\}
$$

is neither open nor closed, because it contains some boundary points but not all of them.

##### problem 5

The open disk $B_r(z_0)$ is open because every point in it is an interior point.

The closed disk

$$
\{z: |z-z_0|\le r\}
$$

is closed because it contains its entire boundary, namely the circle $|z-z_0|=r$.

The set

$$
\{z: |z-z_0|>r\}
$$

is open because if a point lies strictly outside the circle of radius $r$, then a sufficiently small neighborhood around that point also stays outside that circle.

##### problem 6

To say that $z$ is an element of a set $S$ means that $z$ belongs to $S$. We write

$$
z\in S.
$$

If $z$ does not belong to $S$, we write $z\notin S$.

##### problem 7

The union of $A$ and $B$ is the set of points that lie in at least one of the two sets:

$$
A\cup B=\{z: z\in A \text{ or } z\in B\}.
$$

The intersection of $A$ and $B$ is the set of points that lie in both sets:

$$
A\cap B=\{z: z\in A \text{ and } z\in B\}.
$$

The sets $A$ and $B$ are disjoint if they have no common points, that is,

$$
A\cap B=\emptyset.
$$

##### problem 8

The set difference $A\backslash B$ is the set of points that belong to $A$ but not to $B$:

$$
A\backslash B=\{z: z\in A \text{ and } z\notin B\}.
$$

The relation $A\subset B$ means that every element of $A$ is also an element of $B$. In symbols,

$$
z\in A \Rightarrow z\in B.
$$

We come now to an important definition.

> [!definition] open set
> A subset $S$ of the complex numbers is called open if every point in $S$ is an interior point of $S$.

Thus $S$ is open if around each point $z$ in $S$ you can find a neighborhood $B_{r}(z)$ that is wholly contained in $S$. The radius of $B_{r}(z)$ depends on $z$. Here are some useful examples to keep in mind.

- The empty set, denoted as usual by $\emptyset$, is open. Because there are no points in $\emptyset$, the definition of open sets is vacuously satisfied.
- The set of all complex numbers $\mathbb{C}$ is open.
- Any $r$-neighborhood, $B_{r}\left(z_{0}\right)$, is open. We just verified in Example 1(a) that every point in $B_{r}\left(z_{0}\right)$ is an interior point.
- The set of all $z$ such that $\left|z-z_{0}\right|>r$ is open. This set is called a neighborhood of $\infty$. To justify this terminology, see Exercise 23.

An $r$-neighborhood, $B_{r}\left(z_{0}\right)$, is more commonly called an open disk of radius $r$, centered at $z_{0}$.

You can show that a set is open if and only if it contains none of its boundary points (Exercise 18). Sets that contain all of their boundary points are called closed. The complex plane $\mathbb{C}$ and the empty set $\emptyset$ are closed since they trivially contain their empty sets of boundary points. The disk

$$
\left\{z:\left|z-z_{0}\right| \leq r\right\}
$$

is closed because it contains all its boundary points consisting of the circle $\left|z-z_{0}\right|=r$ (Figure 3). We will refer to such a disk as the closed disk of radius $r$, centered at $z_{0}$.

Some sets are neither open nor closed. For example, the set

$$
\left\{z:\left|z-z_{0}\right| \leq r ; \operatorname{Im} z>0\right\}
$$

contains the boundary points on the upper semicircle, but it does not contain the points on the $x$-axis. Hence, this set is neither open nor closed (Figure 4).

# 2.1.2 Connected Sets

> [!review]
> 1. What is the role of connectedness in extending the calculus result that a function with zero derivative is constant?
> 2. What does it mean for a subset of the complex plane to be polygonally connected, and why is the requirement that the polygonal line be finite important?
> 3. What two properties define a region, and why is each one needed independently?
> 4. What is the difference between a region, a domain, and a domain of definition?
> 5. How can you tell whether an open set is connected without having to construct polygonal paths, and why must both subsets in the decomposition be open?

##### problem 1

Connectedness is what lets the calculus statement "zero derivative implies constant" extend from an interval to a more general set. If the domain is not connected, a function can be constant on each connected piece and still take different constants on different pieces.

That is exactly what happens for a function such as

$$
f(x)=
\begin{cases}
1, & 0<x<1,\\
-1, & 2<x<3,
\end{cases}
$$

whose derivative is zero everywhere on its domain, but which is not constant because the domain has two disconnected pieces.

##### problem 2

A subset $S$ of the complex plane is polygonally connected if any two points of $S$ can be joined by a polygonal line lying wholly in $S$ and consisting of finitely many line segments joined end to end.

The requirement that the polygonal line be finite matters because the definition is meant to describe an actual path built from a finite chain of straight segments. Without finiteness, one could allow infinitely complicated broken lines, which is not the notion being used here.

##### problem 3

A region is defined by two properties: it must be open and connected.

Each condition is needed independently. A closed disk is connected but not open, so it is not a region. The union of two disjoint open disks is open but not connected, so it is also not a region.

##### problem 4

In this book, a region is a nonempty open connected subset of $\mathbb{C}$. The term domain is often used by other authors as a synonym for region, but this book warns that it will use the word domain mainly in its usual functional sense.

A domain of definition is simply the set on which a function is defined. It need not be open or connected. So every region can serve as a domain of definition, but not every domain of definition is a region.

##### problem 5

For a nonempty open set $S$, you can test connectedness by checking whether $S$ can be written as the disjoint union of two nonempty open subsets. If that is impossible, then $S$ is connected and hence a region.

Both subsets in the decomposition must be open because the characterization is specifically about splitting an open set into two separated open pieces. If one allowed arbitrary subsets, then many connected sets could be decomposed in trivial ways that do not reflect disconnectedness.




+++++


A basic result from calculus states that if $f^{\prime}(x)=0$ for all $x$ in $(a, b)$, then $f$ is a constant. This result is not true if the domain of definition of the function is not connected. For example, consider the function

$$
f(x)= \begin{cases}1 & \text { if } 0<x<1 \\ -1 & \text { if } 2<x<3\end{cases}
$$



whose domain of definition is $(0,1) \cup(2,3)$. We have $f^{\prime}(x)=0$ for all $x$ in $(0,1) \cup(2,3)$, but clearly $f$ is not constant. This example shows you how crucial connectedness is in calculus. For subsets of the plane, one way to define connectedness is as follows.

> [!definition] polygonally connected (or connected)
> A subset $S$ of the complex plane is called polygonally connected or connected if any two points in $S$ can be joined by a polygonal line consisting of a finite number of line segments joined end to end and wholly contained in $S$ (Figure 6).


> [!figure] Figure 6
> 
> ![|center|432](../images/af4f9593-dec7-4bc0-8d78-d25500419ec9-06_508_562_1818_97.jpg)
> Joining two points in a connected region by a polygonal line.
> 


The following definition combines two important properties.


> [!definition] region (or domain)
 > A nonempty subset $S$ of the complex plane is called a region (or domain) if it is open and connected.



> [!warning]
> Some authors use the term domain instead of region and use the term region to denote a subset of the complex plane that may be open or closed. In this book, it is important to keep in mind that regions are always open. The term domain will be used only in connection with its usual meaning as in "domain of definition" of a function.




Connected open sets or regions have the following useful characterization, which is intuitively clear. Its proof is left as an exercise.


> [!proposition] Proposition 1: Characterization of Regions
> A nonempty open subset $S$ of the complex plane is a region if and only if it cannot be written as the disjoint union of two nonempty open subsets. Equivalently, if $S$ is a region (nonempty connected open subset of $\mathbb{C}$ ) and $S=A \cup B$, where $A$ and $B$ are open and disjoint, then either $A=\emptyset$ or $B=\emptyset$.

Here are some useful examples of regions.

- An open disk $B_{r}\left(z_{0}\right)$ is a region.
- A punctured disk centered at $z_{0}, B_{r}^{\prime}\left(z_{0}\right)=\left\{z: 0<\left|z-z_{0}\right|<r\right\}$, is a region.
- An open annulus centered at $z_{0}$,

$$
A_{r_{1}, r_{2}}\left(z_{0}\right)=\left\{z: r_{1}<\left|z-z_{0}\right|<r_{2}\right\}
$$

is a region (Figure 5).



> [!figure] Figure 5
> 
> ![400](../images/af4f9593-dec7-4bc0-8d78-d25500419ec9-05_455_500_1415_104.jpg)
> An open annulus $A_{r_{1}, r_{2}}\left(z_{0}\right)$ is a region. Note how two arbitrary points $A$ and $B$ in $A_{r_{1}, r_{2}}\left(z_{0}\right)$ can be joined by a polygonal line contained in the annulus.


- The open upper half-plane $\{z: \operatorname{Im} z>0\}$ is a region.
- The complex plane is a region.

Here are sets that are not regions.

- A closed disk is not a region because it is not open.
- The union of two disjoint open disks (for example, $B_{1}(0) \cup B_{\frac{1}{2}}(2 i)$ ) is not a region because it is not connected.
- An interval $(a, b)$ is not a region because it is not an open subset of the complex plane.

> [!review]
> 1. Why is it necessary that $\Omega$ be open in order to define partial derivatives on it?
> 2. Why is it necessary that $\Omega$ be connected in order to conclude that a function with both partial derivatives zero is constant?
> 3. Does the one-dimensional result - that a function with zero derivative is constant still hold for functions of two variables, and if so, under what conditions?

##### problem 1

It is necessary that $\Omega$ be open because the definition of a partial derivative uses nearby points such as $(x+h,y)$ and $(x,y+h)$. To form the limits

$$
\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}
=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{u(x+h,y)-u(x,y)}{h},
\qquad
\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}
=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{u(x,y+h)-u(x,y)}{h},
$$

those nearby points must remain in $\Omega$ for all sufficiently small $h$. Openness guarantees that.

##### problem 2

Connectedness is necessary because if $\Omega$ is not connected, then a function can be constant on each connected component and still take different constants on different components. In that case both partial derivatives may be zero everywhere without forcing one single constant value on all of $\Omega$.

##### problem 3

Yes. The one-dimensional result does extend to functions of two variables, provided the function is defined on a nonempty open connected set $\Omega$ and both partial derivatives vanish everywhere on $\Omega$.

Under those conditions, if

$$
u_x(x,y)=0
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
u_y(x,y)=0
$$

for all $(x,y)\in\Omega$, then $u$ is constant on $\Omega$.







+++++


We end the section with an application of connectedness to functions of two variables. This is our generalization to two dimensions of the fact that a function with zero derivative is constant.

Suppose that $u(x, y)$ is a real-valued function of $(x, y)$ defined on a nonempty open set $\Omega$. If we fix $y$, we can think of $u$ as a function of $x$ alone and take its derivative with respect to $x$ and is denoted by $\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}$ or sometimes $u_x$.




$$
\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}=\lim _{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{u(x+h, y)-u(x, y)}{h} .
$$

Similarly, we can fix $x$, think of $u(x, y)$ as a function of $y$, and take its partial derivative with respect to $y$. Thus

$$
\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}=\lim _{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{u(x, y+h)-u(x, y)}{h} .
$$


> [!exercise]
> The limit in (2) involves the values of $u$ at the point ( $x+ h, y)$. Show that this point belongs to $\Omega$ if $h$ is sufficiently small, because $\Omega$ is open. It is in this sense that we will interpret expressions involving limits.

Since $(x,y)\in\Omega$ and $\Omega$ is open, there exists $r>0$ such that the disk

$$
B_r(x+iy)
$$

is wholly contained in $\Omega$.

Now

$$
\left|(x+h+iy)-(x+iy)\right|=|h|.
$$

Therefore, if $|h|<r$, then the point $x+h+iy$, that is, the point $(x+h,y)$, belongs to the disk $B_r(x+iy)$. Hence

$$
(x+h,y)\in\Omega
$$

for all sufficiently small $h$. This is why the limit defining $\partial u/\partial x$ is meaningful on an open set.

> [!theorem] Theorem 1
> Suppose that $u$ is a real-valued function defined over a region $\Omega$ such that $u_{x}(x, y)=0$ and $u_{y}(x, y)=0$ for all $(x, y)$ in $\Omega$. Then $u(x, y)$ is constant for all $(x, y)$ in $\Omega$.



> [!exercise] Title
> Prove Theorem 1

Pick a point $(x_0,y_0)\in\Omega$, and let

$$
A=\{(x,y)\in\Omega:u(x,y)=u(x_0,y_0)\}.
$$

We will show that $A$ is nonempty, open, and closed in $\Omega$. Since $\Omega$ is connected, that will force $A=\Omega$, which means that $u$ is constant on $\Omega$.

The set $A$ is nonempty because $(x_0,y_0)\in A$.

Now let $(a,b)\in A$. Because $\Omega$ is open, there exists $r>0$ such that the disk centered at $a+ib$ with radius $r$ lies in $\Omega$. We claim that $u$ is constant on this disk.

Take any point $(x,y)$ in that disk. Since the whole disk lies in $\Omega$, the horizontal segment from $(a,b)$ to $(x,b)$ and the vertical segment from $(x,b)$ to $(x,y)$ both lie in the disk.

Along the horizontal segment we keep $y=b$ fixed. Define

$$
\phi(t)=u(t,b).
$$

Then

$$
\phi'(t)=u_x(t,b)=0
$$

for all $t$ on that segment. By the one-variable calculus result, $\phi$ is constant there, so

$$
u(x,b)=u(a,b).
$$

Along the vertical segment we keep $x$ fixed and define

$$
\psi(t)=u(x,t).
$$

Then

$$
\psi'(t)=u_y(x,t)=0
$$

for all $t$ on that segment, so $\psi$ is constant there and

$$
u(x,y)=u(x,b).
$$

Combining the two equalities gives

$$
u(x,y)=u(x,b)=u(a,b)=u(x_0,y_0).
$$

Thus every point of that disk belongs to $A$, so $A$ is open in $\Omega$.

Now let $(a,b)\in\Omega\setminus A$. Using the same argument on a small disk contained in $\Omega$ and centered at $(a,b)$, we see that $u$ is constant on that disk. Since $(a,b)\notin A$, the constant value on the disk is not $u(x_0,y_0)$. Hence every point of that disk also lies in $\Omega\setminus A$. Therefore $\Omega\setminus A$ is open in $\Omega$.

So $A$ is both open and closed in $\Omega$, and it is nonempty. Because $\Omega$ is connected, the only nonempty subset of $\Omega$ that is both open and closed is $\Omega$ itself. Hence

$$
A=\Omega.
$$

Therefore

$$
u(x,y)=u(x_0,y_0)
$$

for every $(x,y)\in\Omega$, so $u$ is constant on $\Omega$.

##### problem 17

We prove both directions.

First suppose that $S$ is open. Let $z$ be a boundary point of $\mathbb{C}\backslash S$. We claim that

$$
z\in \mathbb{C}\backslash S.
$$

If $z\in S$, then because $S$ is open there is a neighborhood of $z$ wholly contained in $S$. But then that neighborhood contains no point of $\mathbb{C}\backslash S$, which is impossible if $z$ is a boundary point of $\mathbb{C}\backslash S$. Hence every boundary point of $\mathbb{C}\backslash S$ belongs to $\mathbb{C}\backslash S$, so $\mathbb{C}\backslash S$ is closed.

Conversely, suppose that $\mathbb{C}\backslash S$ is closed. Let $z\in S$. Then

$$
z\notin \mathbb{C}\backslash S.
$$

Since a closed set contains all of its boundary points, $z$ cannot be a boundary point of $\mathbb{C}\backslash S$. Therefore there exists a neighborhood of $z$ containing no point of $\mathbb{C}\backslash S$. That neighborhood is wholly contained in $S$, so $z$ is an interior point of $S$. Since every point of $S$ is interior, $S$ is open.

Therefore

$$
S \text{ is open }
\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad
\mathbb{C}\backslash S \text{ is closed.}
$$

##### problem 18

Again we prove both directions.

Suppose first that $S$ is open. Then every point of $S$ is an interior point. But an interior point cannot be a boundary point. Hence $S$ contains none of its boundary points.

Conversely, suppose that $S$ contains none of its boundary points. Let $z\in S$. Every point of $S$ is either an interior point or a boundary point. Since $z\in S$ and $S$ contains no boundary points, $z$ must be an interior point. Since every point of $S$ is interior, $S$ is open.

Hence

$$
S \text{ is open }
\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad
S \text{ contains none of its boundary points.}
$$

##### problem 20(b)

An infinite intersection of open sets need not be open. Consider

$$

\qquad n=1,2,3,\dots
$$

$$
\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty}A_n
=\left\{z:|z|<\frac{1}{n}\text{ for all }n\right\}.

|z|=0<\frac{1}{n}
\qquad\text{for all }n,

To show that $A\cup B$ is a region, we must show that it is open, nonempty, and connected.

$$
A\cap B\ne\emptyset,
$$

choose a point

$$

$$

There are four cases.

If

$$
z\in A
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
w\in A,
$$

then, because $A$ is connected, there is a polygonal line in $A$ joining $z$ to $w$. Since

$$
A\subset A\cup B,
$$

this polygonal line also lies in $A\cup B$.

If

$$
z\in B
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
w\in B,
$$

the same argument using the connectedness of $B$ shows that $z$ and $w$ can be joined by a polygonal line in $B$, hence in $A\cup B$.

If

$$
z\in A
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
w\in B,

##### problem 22

The point of this problem is that the condition

$$
u_y(x,y)=0

only says that $u$ is locally constant in the vertical direction. It does not force the values on two different vertical pieces of $\Omega$ to agree if the vertical slice of $\Omega$ is disconnected.

##### problem 22(a)

Consider

$$
u(x,y)=
\begin{cases}
0, & x>0,\\
\operatorname{sgn} y, & x\le 0.
\end{cases}
$$

We first show that

$$
u_y(x,y)=0
\qquad\text{for all }(x,y)\in\Omega.
$$

Take $(x_0,y_0)\in\Omega$.

If $x_0>0$, then for all $(x_0,y)$ sufficiently near $(x_0,y_0)$ we still have $x_0>0$, so

$$
u(x_0,y)=0.
$$

Hence, as a function of $y$, $u(x_0,y)$ is constant near $y_0$, and therefore

$$
u_y(x_0,y_0)=0.
$$

Now suppose $x_0\le 0$. Because $(x_0,y_0)\in\Omega$ and the slit in Figure 7 removes the negative-$x$ direction near the $x$-axis, such a point cannot lie on the $x$-axis. Thus

$$
y_0\ne 0.
$$

Choose $\delta>0$ so small that

$$
y_0+h
$$

has the same sign as $y_0$ whenever $|h|<\delta$. Then for such $h$,

$$
\operatorname{sgn}(y_0+h)=\operatorname{sgn}(y_0),
$$

and hence

$$
u(x_0,y_0+h)=u(x_0,y_0).
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
u_y(x_0,y_0)
&=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{u(x_0,y_0+h)-u(x_0,y_0)}{h}\\
&=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{0}{h}\\
&=0.
\end{aligned}
$$

So indeed

$$
u_y(x,y)=0
\qquad\text{throughout }\Omega.
$$

However, $u$ is not a function of $x$ alone. For some negative $a$, the vertical line $x=a$ meets $\Omega$ in two pieces, one with $y>0$ and one with $y<0$. Pick $(a,y_1)$ and $(a,y_2)$ in those two pieces with

$$
y_1>0,
\qquad
y_2<0.
$$

Then

$$
u(a,y_1)=1,
\qquad
u(a,y_2)=-1.
$$

These two points have the same $x$-coordinate but different values of $u$, so $u$ cannot be written in the form

$$
u(x,y)=\phi(x).
$$

##### problem 22(b)

As written, the branch

$$
e^{-1/x^2}\operatorname{sgn} y
$$

is undefined at $x=0$. The intended example is obtained by using its natural extension at $x=0$, namely

$$
u(x,y)=
\begin{cases}
0, & x>0,\\
e^{-1/x^2}\operatorname{sgn} y, & x<0,\\
0, & x=0.
\end{cases}
$$

We now show that this function has the required properties.

First,

$$
u_y(x,y)=0
\qquad\text{for all }(x,y)\in\Omega.
$$

If $x_0>0$, then $u(x_0,y)=0$ near $(x_0,y_0)$, so $u_y(x_0,y_0)=0$.

If $x_0<0$, then near $(x_0,y_0)$ the sign of $y$ is constant, so

$$
u(x_0,y)=e^{-1/x_0^2}\operatorname{sgn} y
$$

is constant as a function of $y$ near $y_0$. Hence

$$
u_y(x_0,y_0)=0.
$$

If $x_0=0$, then by the extension above,

$$
u(0,y)=0
$$

for every $(0,y)\in\Omega$, so again

$$
u_y(0,y_0)=0.
$$

Thus

$$
u_y=0
\qquad\text{throughout }\Omega.
$$

Next we show that the partial derivative with respect to $x$ exists everywhere.

For $x_0>0$, the function is identically $0$ in a neighborhood, so

$$
u_x(x_0,y_0)=0.
$$

For $x_0<0$, the factor $\operatorname{sgn} y$ is locally constant, so differentiating the $x$-dependence gives

$$
\begin{aligned}
u_x(x_0,y_0)
&=\frac{d}{dx}\left(e^{-1/x^2}\operatorname{sgn} y_0\right)\Bigg|_{x=x_0}\\
&=\left(e^{-1/x_0^2}\frac{2}{x_0^3}\right)\operatorname{sgn} y_0.
\end{aligned}
$$

The only nontrivial case is $x_0=0$. Since $u(0,y_0)=0$, we compute

$$
\begin{aligned}
u_x(0,y_0)
&=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{u(h,y_0)-u(0,y_0)}{h}\\
&=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{u(h,y_0)}{h}.
\end{aligned}
$$

If $h>0$, then $u(h,y_0)=0$, so the quotient is $0$.

If $h<0$, then

$$
u(h,y_0)=e^{-1/h^2}\operatorname{sgn} y_0,
$$

so

$$
\left|\frac{u(h,y_0)}{h}\right|
=\frac{e^{-1/h^2}}{|h|}.
$$

Set

$$
t=\frac{1}{|h|}.
$$

Then $t\to\infty$ as $h\to 0$, and

$$
\frac{e^{-1/h^2}}{|h|}
=te^{-t^2}\to 0.
$$

Therefore

$$
u_x(0,y_0)=0.
$$

So $u_x$ exists everywhere on $\Omega$.

Finally, $u$ is still not a function of $x$ alone. Indeed, for any negative $a$ for which both upper and lower points of the slice lie in $\Omega$, pick $y_1>0$ and $y_2<0$ with $(a,y_1),(a,y_2)\in\Omega$. Then

$$
u(a,y_1)=e^{-1/a^2},
\qquad
u(a,y_2)=-e^{-1/a^2},
$$

so the same $x$-value gives two different function values.

##### problem 22(c)

A sufficient condition is that every vertical slice of $\Omega$ be connected. More precisely, require that for each real number $x$, the set

$$
\Omega_x=\{y:(x,y)\in\Omega\}
$$

be either empty or an interval.

Equivalently: whenever $(x,y_1)$ and $(x,y_2)$ both belong to $\Omega$, the whole vertical line segment between them also lies in $\Omega$.

Under this condition, if

$$
u_y=0
\qquad\text{on }\Omega,
$$

then $u$ depends only on $x$.

To prove this, fix $x_0$ and suppose

$$
(x_0,y_1),(x_0,y_2)\in\Omega,
\qquad y_1<y_2.
$$

By the hypothesis on $\Omega$, the whole segment

$$
\{(x_0,y):y_1\le y\le y_2\}
$$

lies in $\Omega$. Define

$$
g(y)=u(x_0,y),
\qquad y_1\le y\le y_2.
$$

Then

$$
g'(y)=u_y(x_0,y)=0
\qquad\text{for }y_1<y<y_2.
$$

By the mean value theorem,

$$
\frac{g(y_2)-g(y_1)}{y_2-y_1}=g'(\xi)
$$

for some $\xi\in(y_1,y_2)$. Since $g'(\xi)=0$, we get

$$
g(y_2)-g(y_1)=0,
$$

so

$$
u(x_0,y_2)=u(x_0,y_1).
$$

Thus for a fixed $x_0$, all points of $\Omega$ with that $x$-coordinate have the same $u$-value. We may therefore define

$$
\phi(x_0)=u(x_0,y)
$$

for any $y$ such that $(x_0,y)\in\Omega$.

This is well defined, and for every $(x,y)\in\Omega$ we have

$$
u(x,y)=\phi(x).
$$

So under the condition that each vertical slice of $\Omega$ is connected, the identity $u_y=0$ does imply that $u$ depends only on $x$.

For parts (a)-(e), keep the geometric construction in mind. The stereographic projection sends a point $z^\star$ on the sphere to the point $z$ in the plane obtained by drawing the line through the north pole $N$ and $z^\star$ and extending it to the plane. ##### problem a

Let the parallel of latitude be the intersection of the sphere with a horizontal plane

$$
x_3=c,
\qquad -1<c<1.
$$

We now show that every point of this parallel projects to a point in the plane having the same modulus. Let

$$
z^\star=(x_1,x_2,c)
$$

be any point on that parallel. From part (h),

$$
x=\frac{x_1}{1-x_3},
\qquad
y=\frac{x_2}{1-x_3}.
$$

Since here $x_3=c$, this becomes

$$
x=\frac{x_1}{1-c},
\qquad
y=\frac{x_2}{1-c}.
$$

Hence

$$
\begin{aligned}
x^2+y^2
&=\frac{x_1^2}{(1-c)^2}+\frac{x_2^2}{(1-c)^2}\\
&=\frac{x_1^2+x_2^2}{(1-c)^2}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Because $z^\star$ lies on the unit sphere,

$$
x_1^2+x_2^2+x_3^2=1.
$$

Substitute $x_3=c$:

$$
x_1^2+x_2^2+c^2=1,
$$

so

$$
x_1^2+x_2^2=1-c^2.
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
x^2+y^2
&=\frac{1-c^2}{(1-c)^2}\\
&=\frac{(1-c)(1+c)}{(1-c)^2}\\
&=\frac{1+c}{1-c}.
\end{aligned}
$$

This quantity is constant, depending only on $c$. So the image under $P$ is the circle

$$
|z|^2=\frac{1+c}{1-c},
\qquad\text{equivalently}\qquad
|z|=\sqrt{\frac{1+c}{1-c}}.
$$

Thus a parallel of latitude maps to a circle centered at the origin.

This formula also explains the geometric cases:

$$
c=0 \Rightarrow |z|=1,
$$

so the equator maps to the unit circle;

$$
c>0 \Rightarrow \frac{1+c}{1-c}>1,
$$

so parallels in the northern hemisphere map outside the unit circle; and

$$
c<0 \Rightarrow \frac{1+c}{1-c}<1,
$$

so parallels in the southern hemisphere map inside the unit circle.

##### problem b

Consider the set

$$

for some fixed $R>0$. The boundary circle

$$
|z|=R

So the points on the sphere mapped to

$$
\{z:|z|>R\}
$$

are exactly the points in a neighborhood of the north pole on the sphere. Since the north pole corresponds to the point at infinity, this explains the terminology "neighborhood of infinity."


##### problem c

A great circle through the poles is the intersection of the sphere with a plane through the vertical axis. That plane also meets the complex plane in a line through the origin.

Now if $z^{\star}$ lies on that great circle and $z^{\star}\neq N$, then $N$, $z^{\star}$, and its image

$$
z=P\left(z^{\star}\right)
$$

all lie on the same line, hence all lie in that same vertical plane. Therefore $z$ lies on the intersection of that plane with

$$
x_3=0,
$$

which is a line through $0$.

So the image of the great circle minus the north pole is a straight line through the origin. Since the missing point is

$$
P(N)=\infty,
$$

the image of the full great circle in the extended complex plane is that line together with the point at infinity.


##### problem d

A circle on the sphere passing through $N$ but not through the south pole is the intersection of the sphere with some plane that passes through $N$ but not through the center of the sphere.

That plane intersects the complex plane in a straight line. For any point $z^{\star}$ on the circle with $z^{\star}\neq N$, the line through $N$ and $z^{\star}$ lies entirely in that plane, so its intersection

$$
z=P\left(z^{\star}\right)
$$

with the complex plane lies on that straight line.

Thus the image of the circle minus the north pole is contained in a straight line.

Conversely, let $z$ be any point on that line in the complex plane. Since both $N$ and $z$ lie in the same plane, the line through $N$ and $z$ lies in that plane. That line meets the sphere in exactly one point other than $N$, and because the plane cuts the sphere in the given circle, that second intersection point lies on the circle. So every point of the line is hit.

Therefore the image of the circle minus the north pole is exactly a straight line.

Since the plane does not pass through the center, this line does not pass through the origin. Indeed, if it did pass through the origin, then the plane would contain $N$ and the origin, hence the entire vertical axis, and therefore also the south pole, contrary to assumption.

So the image of the circle minus $N$ is a line not passing through $0$, and the image of the full circle in the extended complex plane is that line together with the point

$$
\infty=P(N).
$$



##### problem e

Geometrically, if $z^\star$ moves toward the north pole, then the line through $N$ and $z^\star$ becomes nearly tangent to the sphere at $N$. A line of that kind meets the complex plane farther and farther from the origin. So

$$
z^\star\to N
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
P(z^\star)\to\infty.
$$

Conversely, if the projected point $P(z^\star)$ goes farther and farther away in the plane, then the line from $N$ to $P(z^\star)$ becomes almost horizontal near $N$. The second intersection point with the sphere must then lie arbitrarily close to $N$. Thus

$$
P(z^\star)\to\infty
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
z^\star\to N.
$$

Therefore

$$
z^\star\to N
\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad
P(z^\star)\to\infty.
$$

This can also be checked algebraically from part (g), because

x_3=1-\frac{2}{x^2+y^2+1}.

we get

so

For (f), the line through

\qquad\text{and}\qquad
z=(x,y,0)
$$

has direction vector

$$
(x,y,-1).
$$

Since $z^\star=(x_1,x_2,x_3)$ lies on this line, the symmetric equation of the line is

$$
\frac{x_1-0}{x-0}
=\frac{x_2-0}{y-0}
=\frac{x_3-1}{0-1}.
$$


##### problem g


For (g), let the common value in part (f) be $t$. Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
x_1&=tx,\\
x_2&=ty,\\
x_3-1&=-t,
\end{aligned}
$$

so

$$
x_3=1-t.
$$

Because $z^\star$ lies on the unit sphere,

$$
x_1^2+x_2^2+x_3^2=1.
$$

Substitute the expressions above:

$$
\begin{aligned}
(tx)^2+(ty)^2+(1-t)^2&=1\\
t^2x^2+t^2y^2+1-2t+t^2&=1\\
t^2(x^2+y^2+1)-2t&=0\\
t\bigl(t(x^2+y^2+1)-2\bigr)&=0.
\end{aligned}
$$

The root $t=0$ gives the north pole, so for points on the sphere other than $N$ we must have

$$
t=\frac{2}{x^2+y^2+1}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
x_1&=tx=\frac{2x}{x^2+y^2+1},\\
x_2&=ty=\frac{2y}{x^2+y^2+1},\\
x_3&=1-t=1-\frac{2}{x^2+y^2+1}.
\end{aligned}
$$


##### problem h

For (h), go back to the relations from part (f):

$$
x_1=tx,\qquad x_2=ty,\qquad x_3=1-t.
$$

From the last equation,

$$
t=1-x_3.
$$

Substitute into the first two:

$$
\begin{aligned}
x_1&=(1-x_3)x,\\
x_2&=(1-x_3)y.
\end{aligned}
$$

Since $z^\star\neq N$, we have $x_3\neq 1$, and therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
x&=\frac{x_1}{1-x_3},\\
y&=\frac{x_2}{1-x_3}.
\end{aligned}
$$

In the following Mathematica demonstration, $(x,y,0)$ is the point in the plane, $N=(0,0,1)$ is the north pole, and the orange point is the projected point $z^\star$ on the sphere. As $(x,y)$ moves farther from the origin, the projected point moves toward the north pole.
