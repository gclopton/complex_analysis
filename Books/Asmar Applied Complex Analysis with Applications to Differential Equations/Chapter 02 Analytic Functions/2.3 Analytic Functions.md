##### problem 1

A complex-valued function $f$ is differentiable at a point $z_0$ if the limit
$$
f'(z_0)=\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{f(z)-f(z_0)}{z-z_0}
$$
exists. The definition looks formally identical to the real-variable one, but it demands more because in the complex plane $z$ may approach $z_0$ from infinitely many directions, not just from the left or the right. Thus the same difference quotient must approach the same value along every possible approach to $z_0$.

##### problem 2

A function is analytic on an open set $S$ if, under the textbook's definition, $f'(z)$ exists and is continuous for every $z\in S$. So formally this is stronger than merely saying that $f$ is differentiable at each point of $S$, because the definition explicitly includes continuity of the derivative. The definition is tied to an open set because analyticity is a local neighborhood property: it is meant to describe behavior throughout a region around each point, not just at one isolated point.

##### problem 3

To say that $f$ is analytic at a point $z_0$ means that there exists some open set containing $z_0$ on which $f$ is analytic. It does not mean only that $f'(z_0)$ exists. This matters because complex analysis studies local structure on neighborhoods, and differentiability at one point alone is too weak to capture the phenomena associated with analytic functions.

##### problem 4

A function is entire if it is analytic on all of $\mathbb{C}$. Equivalently, it is analytic at every complex number.

##### problem 5

The continuity requirement is not actually needed once one knows the deeper theory. If $f'(z)$ exists at every point of an open set, then $f'(z)$ is automatically continuous there. The theorem that establishes this is Goursat's theorem. So the continuity clause is theoretically redundant. It is included in the definition at this stage because it makes some early statements and proofs easier to formulate before Goursat's theorem has been proved.

##### problem 6

Complex differentiability is much stronger than real differentiability because the limit defining $f'(z_0)$ must be independent of the direction of approach in the plane. In real calculus there are essentially only two one-dimensional approaches, from the left and from the right. In complex calculus one can approach along the real axis, the imaginary axis, lines of arbitrary slope, curves, or any other path in $\mathbb{C}$. To show that a complex limit does not exist, one can use the earlier path-comparison technique: approach the same point along two different directions or curves and show that the resulting limits are different.

For a real-valued function $f(x)$ defined on an open interval containing the point $x_{0}$, we defined the derivative at $x_{0}$ to be $f^{\prime}\left(x_{0}\right)=\lim _{x \rightarrow x_{0}} \frac{f(x)-f\left(x_{0}\right)}{x-x_{0}}$, when the limit exists. Our definition for the derivative of a complex function $f(z)$ is a natural extension of the real case.



> [!definition] Complex Derivative
> Let $f(z)$ be defined on a neighborhood of $z_{0}$. If
> 
> $$
> \lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}} \frac{f(z)-f\left(z_{0}\right)}{z-z_{0}}
> $$
> 
> exists, then $f$ is said to be differentiable at the point $z_{0}$, and the number
> 
> $$
> f^{\prime}\left(z_{0}\right)=\lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}} \frac{f(z)-f\left(z_{0}\right)}{z-z_{0}}
> $$
> 
> is called the derivative of $f$ at $z_{0}$.



We can also use the Leibniz notation for the derivative, $\frac{d f}{d z}\left(z_{0}\right)$, or $\left.\frac{d f}{d z}\right|_{z=z_{0}}$. We can recast the difference quotient and define the derivative as

$$
f^{\prime}\left(z_{0}\right)=\lim _{\Delta z \rightarrow 0} \frac{f\left(z_{0}+\Delta z\right)-f\left(z_{0}\right)}{\Delta z}
$$

While this extension looks similar to the definition of a derivative for functions defined on the real line, we are asking a lot more in the complex case. In the real case, $x$ can only approach $x_{0}$ from either the right or the left. In the complex case, $z$ can approach $z_{0}$ from any number of directions. For the derivative to exist, we are requiring that the limit exists no matter how we approach $z_{0}$ in (1).

We now introduce a fundamental definition in the theory of complex variables.


> [!definition] Analytic Function
> A function $f(z)$ defined on an open set $S$ is said to be analytic on $S$ if $f^{\prime}(z)$ exists and is continuous for all $z$ in $S$. We say $f(z)$ is analytic at a point if $f(z)$ is analytic on some open set containing that point. We say $f(z)$ is entire if $f(z)$ is analytic on $\mathbb{C}$.

It is important to note that while differentiability is defined at a specific point, analyticity is defined on an open set. Even when we say "analytic at a point," we really mean analytic on some open set containing this point.

In Section 3.9, we will prove a theoretical result, called Goursat's theorem. It asserts that the mere existence of $f^{\prime}(z)$ in an open set implies that $f^{\prime}(z)$ is continuous. Thus our requirement that $f^{\prime}(z)$ be continuous in the definition of analytic functions is redundant; we have included it to make some initial proofs easier to understand.





> [!exercise] Exercise 1: Some simple entire functions
> Show the following functions are entire (analytic at every point in the complex plane), and find their derivatives.
> (a) $f(z)=2+4 i$.
> (b) $f(z)=(3-i) z$.
> (c) $f(z)=z^{2}$.




##### problem a

Let
$$
f(z)=2+4i.
$$
To find the derivative, use the definition:
$$
\begin{aligned}
f'(z_0)
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{f(z_0+\Delta z)-f(z_0)}{\Delta z}\\
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{(2+4i)-(2+4i)}{\Delta z}\\
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{0}{\Delta z}\\
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}0\\
&=0.
\end{aligned}
$$
This computation does not depend on the choice of $z_0$. Therefore $f'(z)$ exists for every $z\in\mathbb{C}$ and
$$
f'(z)=0.
$$
Hence $f$ is entire.

##### problem b

Let
$$
f(z)=(3-i)z.
$$
Again use the definition:
$$
\begin{aligned}
f'(z_0)
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{f(z_0+\Delta z)-f(z_0)}{\Delta z}\\
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{(3-i)(z_0+\Delta z)-(3-i)z_0}{\Delta z}\\
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{(3-i)z_0+(3-i)\Delta z-(3-i)z_0}{\Delta z}\\
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{(3-i)\Delta z}{\Delta z}\\
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}(3-i)\\
&=3-i.
\end{aligned}
$$
So $f'(z)$ exists for every $z\in\mathbb{C}$ and
$$
f'(z)=3-i.
$$
Hence $f$ is entire.

##### problem c

Let
$$
f(z)=z^2.
$$
Using the definition,
$$
\begin{aligned}
f'(z_0)
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{f(z_0+\Delta z)-f(z_0)}{\Delta z}\\
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{(z_0+\Delta z)^2-z_0^2}{\Delta z}\\
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{z_0^2+2z_0\Delta z+(\Delta z)^2-z_0^2}{\Delta z}\\
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{2z_0\Delta z+(\Delta z)^2}{\Delta z}\\
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\left(2z_0+\Delta z\right)\\
&=2z_0+0\\
&=2z_0.
\end{aligned}
$$
Since $z_0$ was arbitrary, $f'(z)$ exists for every $z\in\mathbb{C}$ and
$$
f'(z)=2z.
$$
Hence $f$ is entire.

##### problem 1

For a constant function
$$
f(z)=c,
$$
the derivative from the limit definition is
$$
\begin{aligned}
f'(z_0)
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{f(z_0+\Delta z)-f(z_0)}{\Delta z}\\
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{c-c}{\Delta z}\\
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{0}{\Delta z}\\
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}0\\
&=0.
\end{aligned}
$$
So
$$
f'(z)=0.
$$

For the identity function
$$
f(z)=z,
$$
we get
$$
\begin{aligned}
f'(z_0)
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{f(z_0+\Delta z)-f(z_0)}{\Delta z}\\
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{(z_0+\Delta z)-z_0}{\Delta z}\\
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{\Delta z}{\Delta z}\\
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}1\\
&=1.
\end{aligned}
$$
So
$$
f'(z)=1.
$$

##### problem 2

If a complex function is differentiable at $z_0$, then it is continuous at $z_0$.

To prove this, assume that $f'(z_0)$ exists. Then
$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{f(z)-f(z_0)}{z-z_0}=f'(z_0).
$$
For $z\ne z_0$, write
$$
\begin{aligned}
f(z)-f(z_0)
&=\frac{f(z)-f(z_0)}{z-z_0}(z-z_0).
\end{aligned}
$$
Now let $z\to z_0$. Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lim_{z\to z_0}\bigl(f(z)-f(z_0)\bigr)
&=
\left(\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{f(z)-f(z_0)}{z-z_0}\right)
\left(\lim_{z\to z_0}(z-z_0)\right)\\
&=
f'(z_0)\cdot 0\\
&=
0.
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence
$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)=f(z_0),
$$
so $f$ is continuous at $z_0$.

##### problem 3

If $f$ and $g$ are analytic on an open set $S$, then both
$$
c_1f+c_2g
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
fg
$$
are analytic on $S$.

For the linear combination, let
$$
h(z)=c_1f(z)+c_2g(z).
$$
Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
h'(z)
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{h(z+\Delta z)-h(z)}{\Delta z}\\
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{c_1f(z+\Delta z)+c_2g(z+\Delta z)-c_1f(z)-c_2g(z)}{\Delta z}\\
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\left[c_1\frac{f(z+\Delta z)-f(z)}{\Delta z}+c_2\frac{g(z+\Delta z)-g(z)}{\Delta z}\right]\\
&=c_1\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{f(z+\Delta z)-f(z)}{\Delta z}+c_2\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{g(z+\Delta z)-g(z)}{\Delta z}\\
&=c_1f'(z)+c_2g'(z).
\end{aligned}
$$
Since $f'$ and $g'$ are continuous on $S$, the function
$$
h'(z)=c_1f'(z)+c_2g'(z)
$$
is continuous on $S$. Therefore $h$ is analytic on $S$.

For the product, let
$$
h(z)=f(z)g(z).
$$
Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
h'(z)
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{f(z+\Delta z)g(z+\Delta z)-f(z)g(z)}{\Delta z}\\
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{f(z+\Delta z)g(z+\Delta z)-f(z)g(z+\Delta z)+f(z)g(z+\Delta z)-f(z)g(z)}{\Delta z}\\
&=\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\left[g(z+\Delta z)\frac{f(z+\Delta z)-f(z)}{\Delta z}+f(z)\frac{g(z+\Delta z)-g(z)}{\Delta z}\right]\\
&=\left(\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}g(z+\Delta z)\right)\left(\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{f(z+\Delta z)-f(z)}{\Delta z}\right)+f(z)\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{g(z+\Delta z)-g(z)}{\Delta z}\\
&=g(z)f'(z)+f(z)g'(z).
\end{aligned}
$$
Since $f,g,f',g'$ are continuous on $S$, the derivative
$$
h'(z)=f'(z)g(z)+f(z)g'(z)
$$
is continuous on $S$. Hence $fg$ is analytic on $S$.

##### problem 4

The quotient
$$
\frac{f}{g}
$$
is analytic at points of $S$ where
$$
g(z)\ne 0.
$$
Thus $\dfrac{f}{g}$ is analytic on the open set
$$
S\setminus\{z\in S:g(z)=0\}.
$$
We must exclude the zeros of $g$ because the quotient is not even defined there.

To find the derivative, let
$$
h(z)=\frac{f(z)}{g(z)}.
$$
Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
f(z)
&=h(z)g(z).
\end{aligned}
$$
Differentiate both sides:
$$
\begin{aligned}
f'(z)
&=h'(z)g(z)+h(z)g'(z).
\end{aligned}
$$
Now solve for $h'(z)$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
h'(z)g(z)
&=f'(z)-h(z)g'(z)\\
&=f'(z)-\frac{f(z)}{g(z)}g'(z)\\
&=\frac{f'(z)g(z)-f(z)g'(z)}{g(z)}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Therefore
$$
\begin{aligned}
h'(z)
&=\frac{f'(z)g(z)-f(z)g'(z)}{g(z)^2}.
\end{aligned}
$$
So
$$
\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)'(z)=\frac{f'(z)g(z)-f(z)g'(z)}{g(z)^2},
\qquad g(z)\ne 0.
$$

##### problem 5

If $g$ is analytic on an open set $S$, and $f$ is analytic on an open set containing $g(S)$, then the composition
$$
f\circ g
$$
is analytic on $S$, and the chain rule says
$$
(f\circ g)'(z)=f'(g(z))\,g'(z).
$$

The condition on $g$ must hold for $z\in S$, while the condition on $f$ must hold at the points
$$
w=g(z)
$$
that arise as values of $g$. In other words, $f$ must be analytic on a neighborhood of every point in the image of $g$.

##### problem 6

The distinction is necessary because analyticity is a neighborhood property, while differentiability at a single point is only a pointwise property. The derivative formulas in Theorem 2 are meant to hold throughout an open set, and the conclusion is that the resulting function is again analytic on that open set. If we only assumed differentiability at one isolated point, we could at best conclude a pointwise derivative formula at that point, not analyticity on any region.

So the algebraic formulas themselves may still be true at a single point where all relevant derivatives exist, but the theorem is stronger than that: it asserts preservation of analyticity on an open set. That conclusion would fail if we replaced the hypothesis by differentiability at merely an isolated point, because there would be no open set on which analyticity had been established.

Because the definition of the derivative in the complex case is modeled after the definition of the derivative in the real case, it should not surprise you that many of the properties of derivatives that you are familiar with from calculus hold for analytic functions.



> [!theorem] Theorem 2: Properties of Analytic Functions
> Suppose that $f$ and $g$ are analytic on an open set $S$ and $c_{1}, c_{2}$ are complex constants. Then $c_{1} f+c_{2} g$ and $f g$ are analytic on $S$ with
> 
> $$
> \begin{aligned}
> \left(c_{1} f+c_{2} g\right)^{\prime}(z) & =c_{1} f^{\prime}(z)+c_{2} g^{\prime}(z) \text { and } \\
> (f g)^{\prime}(z) & =f^{\prime}(z) g(z)+f(z) g^{\prime}(z) .
> \end{aligned}
> $$
> 
> Also, $\frac{f}{g}$ is analytic on $S$ minus the points where $g=0$, and
> 
> $$
> \left(\frac{f}{g}\right)^{\prime}(z)=\frac{f^{\prime}(z) g(z)-f(z) g^{\prime}(z)}{(g(z))^{2}} \quad(g(z) \neq 0) .
> $$
> 
> If $g$ is analytic at $z_{0}$ and $f$ is analytic at $g\left(z_{0}\right)$, then the composition $(f \circ g)(z)$ is analytic at $z_{0}$ and we have the chain rule
> 
> $$
> (f \circ g)^{\prime}\left(z_{0}\right)=f^{\prime}\left(g\left(z_{0}\right)\right) g^{\prime}\left(z_{0}\right) .
> $$
> 


**Proof** 





> [!exercise] Exercise 2: Analyticity of $z^{n}$ for $n=1,2, \ldots$
> Use (1) to show that for $n=1,2, \ldots$
> 
> $$
> \frac{d}{d z} z^{n}=n z^{n-1} .
> $$
> 
> Conclude that $f(z)=z^{n}$ is entire.


**Solution:** We prove by induction on $n$ that
$$
\frac{d}{dz}z^n=nz^{n-1}.
$$

For $n=1$, we have
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{d}{dz}z^1
&=\frac{d}{dz}z\\
&=1\\
&=1\cdot z^0.
\end{aligned}
$$
So the formula is true when $n=1$.

Now assume it is true for some $n\ge 1$, that is, assume
$$
\frac{d}{dz}z^n=nz^{n-1}.
$$
We prove it for $n+1$. Since
$$
z^{n+1}=z^n\cdot z,
$$
the product rule gives
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{d}{dz}z^{n+1}
&=\frac{d}{dz}(z^n\cdot z)\\
&=\left(\frac{d}{dz}z^n\right)z+z^n\left(\frac{d}{dz}z\right)\\
&=(nz^{n-1})z+z^n(1)\\
&=nz^n+z^n\\
&=(n+1)z^n.
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the formula holds for $n+1$.

Therefore, by induction,
$$
\frac{d}{dz}z^n=nz^{n-1}
$$
for every integer $n=1,2,\dots$.

To conclude that
$$
f(z)=z^n
$$
is entire, note that the derivative exists for every complex number $z$, namely
$$
f'(z)=nz^{n-1}.
$$
Since this is true for every $z\in\mathbb{C}$, the function $z^n$ is analytic on all of $\mathbb{C}$. Hence $z^n$ is entire.

> [!exercise] Exercise 3: Analyticity of a rational function
> Find the derivative of
> 
> $$
> f(z)=\frac{(z+1)(z+i)^{2}}{z+1-3 i}
> $$
> 
> and determine where $f(z)$ is analytic.


**Solution:** Let
$$
f(z)=\frac{(z+1)(z+i)^2}{z+1-3i}.
$$
Set
$$
N(z)=(z+1)(z+i)^2,
\qquad
D(z)=z+1-3i.
$$
Then
$$
f(z)=\frac{N(z)}{D(z)}.
$$

We first compute $N'(z)$. Using the product rule,
$$
\begin{aligned}
N'(z)
&=\frac{d}{dz}\bigl((z+1)(z+i)^2\bigr)\\
&=\left(\frac{d}{dz}(z+1)\right)(z+i)^2+(z+1)\frac{d}{dz}(z+i)^2\\
&=1\cdot (z+i)^2+(z+1)\cdot 2(z+i)\\
&=(z+i)^2+2(z+1)(z+i).
\end{aligned}
$$
Factor out $(z+i)$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
N'(z)
&=(z+i)\bigl((z+i)+2(z+1)\bigr)\\
&=(z+i)(z+i+2z+2)\\
&=(z+i)(3z+2+i).
\end{aligned}
$$

Also,
$$
\begin{aligned}
D'(z)
&=\frac{d}{dz}(z+1-3i)\\
&=1.
\end{aligned}
$$

Now apply the quotient rule:
$$
\begin{aligned}
f'(z)
&=\frac{N'(z)D(z)-N(z)D'(z)}{D(z)^2}\\
&=\frac{(z+i)(3z+2+i)(z+1-3i)-(z+1)(z+i)^2}{(z+1-3i)^2}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Factor out $(z+i)$ in the numerator:
$$
\begin{aligned}
f'(z)
&=\frac{(z+i)\bigl((3z+2+i)(z+1-3i)-(z+1)(z+i)\bigr)}{(z+1-3i)^2}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Now simplify the bracket. First,
$$
\begin{aligned}
(3z+2+i)(z+1-3i)
&=3z(z+1-3i)+(2+i)(z+1-3i)\\
&=3z^2+3z-9iz+(2+i)z+(2+i)(1-3i)\\
&=3z^2+(3-9i+2+i)z+(2-6i+i-3i^2)\\
&=3z^2+(5-8i)z+(2-5i+3)\\
&=3z^2+(5-8i)z+(5-5i).
\end{aligned}
$$
Next,
$$
\begin{aligned}
(z+1)(z+i)
&=z^2+iz+z+i\\
&=z^2+(1+i)z+i.
\end{aligned}
$$
Therefore,
$$
\begin{aligned}
(3z+2+i)(z+1-3i)-(z+1)(z+i)
&=\bigl(3z^2+(5-8i)z+(5-5i)\bigr)-\bigl(z^2+(1+i)z+i\bigr)\\
&=2z^2+\bigl((5-8i)-(1+i)\bigr)z+\bigl((5-5i)-i\bigr)\\
&=2z^2+(4-9i)z+(5-6i).
\end{aligned}
$$
So
$$
\boxed{
f'(z)=\frac{(z+i)\bigl(2z^2+(4-9i)z+(5-6i)\bigr)}{(z+1-3i)^2}.
}
$$

Now determine where $f$ is analytic. The numerator
$$
(z+1)(z+i)^2
$$
is a polynomial, so it is entire. The denominator
$$
z+1-3i
$$
vanishes when
$$
\begin{aligned}
z+1-3i&=0\\
z&=-1+3i.
\end{aligned}
$$
Therefore $f(z)$ is analytic for all
$$
z\in \mathbb{C}\setminus\{-1+3i\}.
$$

> [!Review]
> 1. Why is every polynomial an entire function?
> 2. Where is a rational function $\mathrm{p}(\mathrm{z}) / \mathrm{q}(\mathrm{z})$ analytic, and why?
> 3. Not every complex-valued function is analytic. Give examples of complex-valued functions that fail to be analytic everywhere, and explain intuitively why they might fail.




##### problem 1

Every polynomial is entire because a polynomial is a finite linear combination of powers of $z$:
$$
p(z)=a_nz^n+a_{n-1}z^{n-1}+\cdots+a_1z+a_0.
$$
Each power $z^k$ is entire, and constants are entire. Since sums of analytic functions and constant multiples of analytic functions are analytic, it follows that $p(z)$ is analytic for every $z\in\mathbb{C}$. Therefore every polynomial is entire.

##### problem 2

A rational function
$$
\frac{p(z)}{q(z)}
$$
is analytic at all points where
$$
q(z)\ne 0.
$$
This is because polynomials are entire, and the quotient of two analytic functions is analytic wherever the denominator does not vanish. The only points that must be excluded are the zeros of the denominator polynomial $q(z)$, since the function is not defined there.

##### problem 3

Examples of complex-valued functions that are not analytic everywhere include
$$
\bar z,\qquad \operatorname{Re}z,\qquad \operatorname{Im}z,\qquad |z|.
$$
Intuitively, these fail because they do not depend on $z$ in the rigid way that analytic functions do. They involve conjugation, separate real and imaginary parts, or modulus, and these operations generally make the difference quotient depend on the direction from which $z$ approaches the point. Since complex differentiability requires the same limit from every direction in the plane, such functions typically fail to be analytic except possibly in very restricted situations.

By using linear combinations of powers of $z$ and appealing to the result of Example 2 and the linearity of the derivative (5), we conclude that a polynomial is an entire function. By appealing now to the quotient rule, as we did in Example 3, we see that a rational function is analytic at all $z$ where $g(z) \neq 0$.




> [!theorem] Theorem 3 Polynomials and Rational Functions
> Let $n$ be a nonnegative integer. A polynomial of degree $n, p(z)=a_{n} z^{n}+ a_{n-1} z^{n-1}+\cdots+a_{1} z+a_{0}$, is entire. Its derivative is given by
> 
> $$
> p^{\prime}(z)=n a_{n} z^{n-1}+(n-1) a_{n-1} z^{n-1}+\cdots+a_{1} z .
> $$
> 
> A rational function $f(z)=p(z) / q(z)$, where $p(z)$ and $q(z)$ are polynomials, is analytic at all points $z$ where $q(z) \neq 0$. Its derivative is given by
> 
> $$
> f^{\prime}(z)=\frac{p^{\prime}(z) q(z)-p(z) q^{\prime}(z)}{q(z)^{2}}
> $$
> 


Typically, any function that algebraically manipulates $z$ will be differentiable; however, not every complex-valued function is analytic. Functions like $\operatorname{Re} z, \operatorname{Im} z, \bar{z}$, and $|z|$ will have at best limited differentiability.





> [!exercise] Exercise 4: Functions that are nowhere analytic
> Show that the functions
> (a) $f(z)=\bar{z}$ and
> (b) $f(z)=\operatorname{Re} z$
> are not analytic at any point.




##### problem 4.a


**Solution:** Let
$$
f(z)=\bar z.
$$
We show that $f$ is not differentiable at any point $z_0\in\mathbb{C}$.

Let
$$
z_0=x_0+iy_0.
$$
Then
$$
f(z_0)=\overline{z_0}.
$$
If the derivative existed at $z_0$, it would have to equal
$$
\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{f(z_0+\Delta z)-f(z_0)}{\Delta z}
=
\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{\overline{z_0+\Delta z}-\overline{z_0}}{\Delta z}
=
\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{\overline{\Delta z}}{\Delta z}.
$$
So we examine this limit along two different approaches.

First, let $\Delta z=\Delta x$, where $\Delta x$ is real and $\Delta x\to 0$. Then
$$
\overline{\Delta z}=\overline{\Delta x}=\Delta x,
$$
and therefore
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\overline{\Delta z}}{\Delta z}
&=\frac{\Delta x}{\Delta x}\\
&=1.
\end{aligned}
$$
So along the real direction,
$$
\frac{\overline{\Delta z}}{\Delta z}\to 1.
$$

Next, let $\Delta z=i\Delta y$, where $\Delta y$ is real and $\Delta y\to 0$. Then
$$
\overline{\Delta z}=\overline{i\Delta y}=-i\Delta y,
$$
and therefore
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\overline{\Delta z}}{\Delta z}
&=\frac{-i\Delta y}{i\Delta y}\\
&=-1.
\end{aligned}
$$
So along the imaginary direction,
$$
\frac{\overline{\Delta z}}{\Delta z}\to -1.
$$

Since the two directional limits are different,
$$
\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{\overline{\Delta z}}{\Delta z}
$$
does not exist. Therefore $f'(z_0)$ does not exist.

Because $z_0$ was arbitrary, $\bar z$ is not differentiable at any point of $\mathbb{C}$. Hence $f(z)=\bar z$ is nowhere analytic.

##### problem 4.b


**Solution:** Let
$$
f(z)=\operatorname{Re} z.
$$
We show that $f$ is not differentiable at any point $z_0\in\mathbb{C}$.

Let
$$
z_0=x_0+iy_0.
$$
Then
$$
f(z_0)=\operatorname{Re}(z_0)=x_0.
$$
If the derivative existed at $z_0$, then
$$
\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{f(z_0+\Delta z)-f(z_0)}{\Delta z}
$$
would have to exist.

Now examine this quotient along two different approaches.

First, let
$$
\Delta z=\Delta x,
$$
where $\Delta x$ is real and $\Delta x\to 0$. Then
$$
z_0+\Delta z=(x_0+\Delta x)+iy_0,
$$
so
$$
\operatorname{Re}(z_0+\Delta z)=x_0+\Delta x.
$$
Therefore
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{f(z_0+\Delta z)-f(z_0)}{\Delta z}
&=\frac{(x_0+\Delta x)-x_0}{\Delta x}\\
&=\frac{\Delta x}{\Delta x}\\
&=1.
\end{aligned}
$$
So along the real direction,
$$
\frac{f(z_0+\Delta z)-f(z_0)}{\Delta z}\to 1.
$$

Next, let
$$
\Delta z=i\Delta y,
$$
where $\Delta y$ is real and $\Delta y\to 0$. Then
$$
z_0+\Delta z=x_0+i(y_0+\Delta y),
$$
so
$$
\operatorname{Re}(z_0+\Delta z)=x_0.
$$
Therefore
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{f(z_0+\Delta z)-f(z_0)}{\Delta z}
&=\frac{x_0-x_0}{i\Delta y}\\
&=\frac{0}{i\Delta y}\\
&=0.
\end{aligned}
$$
So along the imaginary direction,
$$
\frac{f(z_0+\Delta z)-f(z_0)}{\Delta z}\to 0.
$$

Since the two directional limits are different, the derivative at $z_0$ does not exist.

Because $z_0$ was arbitrary, $\operatorname{Re} z$ is not differentiable at any point of $\mathbb{C}$. Hence $f(z)=\operatorname{Re} z$ is nowhere analytic.

> 1. If you know that a composition $h(z)=f(g(z))$ is analytic, that $f$ is analytic with nonvanishing derivative, and that $g$ is continuous, can you conclude that $g$ is analytic? If so, what is $\mathrm{g}^{\prime}(\mathrm{z})$ ?
> 2. Why is this result useful for establishing the analyticity of inverse functions like logarithms and powers?



##### problem 1

Yes. If
$$
h(z)=f(g(z))
$$
is analytic, $f$ is analytic at $g(z)$ with
$$
f'(g(z))\ne 0,
$$
and $g$ is continuous, then Theorem 4 implies that $g$ is analytic. Moreover,
$$
g'(z)=\frac{h'(z)}{f'(g(z))}.
$$

##### problem 2

This result is useful for inverse functions because inverse functions naturally arise from compositions that equal the identity. If $g$ is a local inverse of $f$, then
$$
f(g(z))=z.
$$
So in the notation of Theorem 4,
$$
h(z)=z,
$$
which is certainly analytic, and
$$
h'(z)=1.
$$
Therefore, provided $f$ is analytic and
$$
f'(g(z))\ne 0,
$$
we obtain
$$
g'(z)=\frac{1}{f'(g(z))}.
$$
This is exactly the kind of formula needed to prove that inverse functions such as logarithms and powers are analytic on appropriate regions.

> [!theorem] Theorem 4: Analyticity of Composed Functions
> Suppose that $h(z)=f(g(z))$ is analytic on a region $\Omega$, and $f$ is analytic at $g(z)$ with $f^{\prime}(g(z)) \neq 0$ for all $z$ in $\Omega$. Suppose further that $g(z)$ is continuous on $\Omega$ (Figure 2). Then $g$ is analytic on $\Omega$ and

> g^{\prime}(z)=\frac{h^{\prime}(z)}{f^{\prime}(g(z))}
> $$
>

> [!exercise] Exercise 5: Analyticity of $n$th roots
> Show that the principal branch of the $n$th root, $g(z)=z^{1 / n}(n=1,2, \ldots)$, is analytic in the region $\Omega$ that consists of all $z \neq 0$ and not on the negative real axis. Also show that for $z$ in $\Omega$, we have
> 
> $$
> g^{\prime}(z)=\frac{1}{n} z^{(1-n) / n}
> $$
> 



**Solution:** Let
$$
g(z)=z^{1/n}
$$
denote the principal branch of the $n$th root, defined on the region
$$
\Omega=\mathbb{C}\setminus(-\infty,0].
$$
For $z\in\Omega$, the principal branch is the unique value of the $n$th root obtained from the principal argument. We show that $g$ is analytic on $\Omega$ and compute its derivative.

Define
$$
f(w)=w^n.
$$
Then $f$ is a polynomial, so it is entire. Also define
$$
h(z)=f(g(z)).
$$
Since $g(z)$ is an $n$th root of $z$, we have
$$
\begin{aligned}
h(z)
&=f(g(z))\\
&=(g(z))^n\\
&=z.
\end{aligned}
$$
So
$$
h(z)=z,
$$
which is analytic on $\Omega$.

Now compute the derivative of $f$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
f'(w)
&=\frac{d}{dw}w^n\\
&=nw^{n-1}.
\end{aligned}
$$
We must check that
$$
f'(g(z))\ne 0
$$
for $z\in\Omega$. Since $z\in\Omega$, we have
$$
z\ne 0.
$$
Therefore
$$
g(z)\ne 0,
$$
because if $g(z)=0$, then
$$
(g(z))^n=0,
$$
which would imply
$$
z=0,
$$
a contradiction. Hence
$$
\begin{aligned}
f'(g(z))
&=n(g(z))^{\,n-1}\\
&\ne 0.
\end{aligned}
$$

Also, the principal branch $g(z)=z^{1/n}$ is continuous on $\Omega$. Therefore all the hypotheses of Theorem 4 are satisfied:
$$
h(z)=f(g(z))=z
$$
is analytic on $\Omega$, $f$ is analytic with
$$
f'(g(z))\ne 0,
$$
and $g$ is continuous on $\Omega$.

So Theorem 4 implies that $g$ is analytic on $\Omega$ and
$$
\begin{aligned}
g'(z)
&=\frac{h'(z)}{f'(g(z))}\\
&=\frac{1}{n(g(z))^{\,n-1}}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Now substitute
$$
g(z)=z^{1/n}.
$$
Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
g'(z)
&=\frac{1}{n\left(z^{1/n}\right)^{n-1}}\\
&=\frac{1}{n z^{(n-1)/n}}\\
&=\frac{1}{n}z^{-(n-1)/n}\\
&=\frac{1}{n}z^{(1-n)/n}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Therefore, for $z\in\Omega$,
$$
\boxed{
g'(z)=\frac{1}{n}z^{(1-n)/n}.
}
$$
Thus the principal branch of the $n$th root is analytic on
$$
\Omega=\mathbb{C}\setminus(-\infty,0].
$$

## 2.3.2 Appendix: Proofs Related to Differentiation



> [!review]
> Show how a function can be locally approximated by an affine linear function at a point if it is differentiable at that point. Show whether or not the converse is true.


**Solution:** Suppose first that $f$ is differentiable at $z_0$. Then
$$
f'(z_0)=\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{f(z)-f(z_0)}{z-z_0}.
$$
Define
$$
\epsilon(z)=\frac{f(z)-f(z_0)}{z-z_0}-f'(z_0)
$$
for $z\ne z_0$. Since the difference quotient tends to $f'(z_0)$, we have
$$
\epsilon(z)\to 0
\qquad\text{as }z\to z_0.
$$
Now solve for $f(z)$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\epsilon(z)
&=\frac{f(z)-f(z_0)}{z-z_0}-f'(z_0)\\
\epsilon(z)+f'(z_0)
&=\frac{f(z)-f(z_0)}{z-z_0}\\
\bigl(\epsilon(z)+f'(z_0)\bigr)(z-z_0)
&=f(z)-f(z_0)\\
f(z)
&=f(z_0)+f'(z_0)(z-z_0)+\epsilon(z)(z-z_0).
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus, near $z_0$, the function is an affine linear function
$$
f(z_0)+f'(z_0)(z-z_0)
$$
plus an error term
$$
\epsilon(z)(z-z_0)
$$
with $\epsilon(z)\to 0$. This shows that a differentiable function is locally approximated by an affine linear function.

Now for the converse. Suppose that
$$
f(z)=f(z_0)+A(z-z_0)+\epsilon(z)(z-z_0),
$$
where
$$
\epsilon(z)\to 0
\qquad\text{as }z\to z_0.
$$
Then for $z\ne z_0$,
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{f(z)-f(z_0)}{z-z_0}
&=\frac{A(z-z_0)+\epsilon(z)(z-z_0)}{z-z_0}\\
&=A+\epsilon(z).
\end{aligned}
$$
Now let $z\to z_0$. Since $\epsilon(z)\to 0$, we get
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{f(z)-f(z_0)}{z-z_0}
&=\lim_{z\to z_0}\bigl(A+\epsilon(z)\bigr)\\
&=A+0\\
&=A.
\end{aligned}
$$
Therefore $f$ is differentiable at $z_0$, and
$$
f'(z_0)=A.
$$

So the converse is true: a function is differentiable at $z_0$ if and only if it admits a local affine linear approximation of the form
$$
f(z)=f(z_0)+A(z-z_0)+\epsilon(z)(z-z_0),
\qquad
\epsilon(z)\to 0.
$$

Suppose that $f(z)$ has a derivative at $z_{0}$ and let

$$
\epsilon(z)=\frac{f(z)-f\left(z_{0}\right)}{z-z_{0}}-f^{\prime}\left(z_{0}\right)
$$

Then $\epsilon(z) \rightarrow 0$ as $z \rightarrow z_{0}$, because the difference quotient in (14) tends to $f^{\prime}\left(z_{0}\right)$. Solving for $f(z)$ in (14) we obtain

$$
f(z)=\overbrace{f\left(z_{0}\right)+f^{\prime}\left(z_{0}\right)\left(z-z_{0}\right)}^{\text {linear function of } z}+\epsilon(z)\left(z-z_{0}\right) .
$$

This expression shows that, near a point where $f$ is differentiable, $f(z)$ is approximately a linear function. The converse is also true. We summarize these results as follows.



> [!theorem] Theorem 5: Differentiability
> A function $f(z)$ is differentiable at $z_{0}$ if and only if it can be written in the form
> 
> $$
> f(z)=f\left(z_{0}\right)+A\left(z-z_{0}\right)+\epsilon(z)\left(z-z_{0}\right)
> $$
> 
> where $A=f^{\prime}\left(z_{0}\right)$ and $\epsilon(z) \rightarrow 0$ as $z \rightarrow z_{0}$.







> [!exercise] Exercise 6: Proof of the Chain Rules
> Suppose $g$ is analytic at $z_{0}$ and $f$ is analytic at $g\left(z_{0}\right)$. Show that
> 
> $$
> (f \circ g)^{\prime}\left(z_{0}\right)=f^{\prime}\left(g\left(z_{0}\right)\right) g^{\prime}\left(z_{0}\right)
> $$
> 



**Solution:** Since $g$ is analytic at $z_0$ and $f$ is analytic at $g(z_0)$, both derivatives
$$
g'(z_0)
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
f'(g(z_0))
$$
exist.

We must show that
$$
(f\circ g)'(z_0)=f'(g(z_0))\,g'(z_0).
$$

Set
$$
w_0=g(z_0).
$$
Since $f$ is differentiable at $w_0$, Theorem 5 gives
$$
f(w)=f(w_0)+f'(w_0)(w-w_0)+\epsilon_1(w)(w-w_0),
$$
where
$$
\epsilon_1(w)\to 0
\qquad\text{as }w\to w_0.
$$
Now substitute
$$
w=g(z).
$$
Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
f(g(z))
&=f(g(z_0))+f'(g(z_0))\bigl(g(z)-g(z_0)\bigr)+\epsilon_1(g(z))\bigl(g(z)-g(z_0)\bigr).
\end{aligned}
$$
Therefore
$$
\begin{aligned}
f(g(z))-f(g(z_0))
&=f'(g(z_0))\bigl(g(z)-g(z_0)\bigr)+\epsilon_1(g(z))\bigl(g(z)-g(z_0)\bigr).
\end{aligned}
$$
Divide by $z-z_0$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{f(g(z))-f(g(z_0))}{z-z_0}
&=f'(g(z_0))\frac{g(z)-g(z_0)}{z-z_0}
  +\epsilon_1(g(z))\frac{g(z)-g(z_0)}{z-z_0}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Now let $z\to z_0$. Since $g$ is continuous at $z_0$, we have
$$
g(z)\to g(z_0)=w_0.
$$
Hence
$$
\epsilon_1(g(z))\to 0.
$$
Also, because $g$ is differentiable at $z_0$,
$$
\frac{g(z)-g(z_0)}{z-z_0}\to g'(z_0).
$$
So
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{f(g(z))-f(g(z_0))}{z-z_0}
&=f'(g(z_0))\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{g(z)-g(z_0)}{z-z_0}
  +\lim_{z\to z_0}\left[\epsilon_1(g(z))\frac{g(z)-g(z_0)}{z-z_0}\right]\\
&=f'(g(z_0))\,g'(z_0)+0\cdot g'(z_0)\\
&=f'(g(z_0))\,g'(z_0).
\end{aligned}
$$
Therefore
$$
(f\circ g)'(z_0)=f'(g(z_0))\,g'(z_0).
$$

> [!exercise] Exercise 7: Proof of Theorem 4
> Suppose that $h(z)=f(g(z))$ is analytic on a region $\Omega$, and $f$ is analytic at $g(z)$ with $f^{\prime}(g(z)) \neq 0$ for all $z$ in $\Omega$. Suppose further that $g(z)$ is continuous on $\Omega$ (Figure 2). Then $g$ is analytic on $\Omega$ and
> 
> $$
> g^{\prime}(z)=\frac{h^{\prime}(z)}{f^{\prime}(g(z))}
> $$


**Solution:** We are given that
$$
h(z)=f(g(z))
$$
is analytic on a region $\Omega$, that $f$ is analytic at $g(z)$ with
$$
f'(g(z))\ne 0
$$
for all $z\in\Omega$, and that $g$ is continuous on $\Omega$. We must show that $g$ is analytic on $\Omega$ and that
$$
g'(z)=\frac{h'(z)}{f'(g(z))}.
$$

Fix a point $z_0\in\Omega$, and set
$$
w_0=g(z_0).
$$
Since $f$ is differentiable at $w_0$, Theorem 5 gives
$$
f(w)=f(w_0)+f'(w_0)(w-w_0)+\epsilon_1(w)(w-w_0),
$$
where
$$
\epsilon_1(w)\to 0
\qquad\text{as }w\to w_0.
$$
Now substitute
$$
w=g(z).
$$
Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
h(z)
&=f(g(z))\\
&=f(g(z_0))+f'(g(z_0))\bigl(g(z)-g(z_0)\bigr)+\epsilon_1(g(z))\bigl(g(z)-g(z_0)\bigr).
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence
$$
\begin{aligned}
h(z)-h(z_0)
&=f'(g(z_0))\bigl(g(z)-g(z_0)\bigr)+\epsilon_1(g(z))\bigl(g(z)-g(z_0)\bigr).
\end{aligned}
$$
Factor out $g(z)-g(z_0)$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
h(z)-h(z_0)
&=\bigl(f'(g(z_0))+\epsilon_1(g(z))\bigr)\bigl(g(z)-g(z_0)\bigr).
\end{aligned}
$$
Since $g$ is continuous at $z_0$, we have
$$
g(z)\to g(z_0)=w_0
\qquad\text{as }z\to z_0.
$$
Therefore
$$
\epsilon_1(g(z))\to 0.
$$
So
$$
f'(g(z_0))+\epsilon_1(g(z))\to f'(g(z_0)).
$$
Because
$$
f'(g(z_0))\ne 0,
$$
the quantity
$$
f'(g(z_0))+\epsilon_1(g(z))
$$
is nonzero for all $z$ sufficiently close to $z_0$. Thus for $z\ne z_0$ and close to $z_0$ we may solve for $g(z)-g(z_0)$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
g(z)-g(z_0)
&=\frac{h(z)-h(z_0)}{f'(g(z_0))+\epsilon_1(g(z))}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Now divide by $z-z_0$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{g(z)-g(z_0)}{z-z_0}
&=
\frac{h(z)-h(z_0)}{z-z_0}\cdot
\frac{1}{f'(g(z_0))+\epsilon_1(g(z))}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Let $z\to z_0$. Then
$$
\frac{h(z)-h(z_0)}{z-z_0}\to h'(z_0),
$$
because $h$ is analytic, and
$$
\frac{1}{f'(g(z_0))+\epsilon_1(g(z))}\to \frac{1}{f'(g(z_0))}.
$$
Therefore
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{g(z)-g(z_0)}{z-z_0}
&=
\left(\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{h(z)-h(z_0)}{z-z_0}\right)
\left(\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{1}{f'(g(z_0))+\epsilon_1(g(z))}\right)\\
&=
h'(z_0)\cdot \frac{1}{f'(g(z_0))}\\
&=
\frac{h'(z_0)}{f'(g(z_0))}.
\end{aligned}
$$
So $g'(z_0)$ exists and
$$
g'(z_0)=\frac{h'(z_0)}{f'(g(z_0))}.
$$

Since $z_0$ was arbitrary, $g$ is analytic on $\Omega$, and for every $z\in\Omega$,
$$
\boxed{
g'(z)=\frac{h'(z)}{f'(g(z))}.
}
$$

**Solution:**

##### problem 1

Let
$$
f(z)=3(z-1)^2+2(z-1).
$$
We first determine where $f$ is analytic. The function
$$
z\mapsto z-1
$$
is analytic on $\mathbb{C}$ because it is the sum of the identity function $z$ and the constant function $-1$. Therefore
$$
(z-1)^2=(z-1)(z-1)
$$
is analytic on $\mathbb{C}$ by the product rule. Then
$$
3(z-1)^2
$$
is analytic on $\mathbb{C}$ because multiplying an analytic function by a constant preserves analyticity. Also
$$
2(z-1)
$$
is analytic on $\mathbb{C}$ for the same reason. Therefore
$$
f(z)=3(z-1)^2+2(z-1)
$$
is analytic on $\mathbb{C}$ as the sum of two analytic functions. Hence $f$ is entire.

Differentiate term by term:
$$
\begin{aligned}
f'(z)
&=3\cdot 2(z-1)\cdot 1+2\cdot 1\\
&=6(z-1)+2\\
&=6z-6+2\\
&=6z-4.
\end{aligned}
$$

So $f$ is analytic on $\mathbb{C}$, and
$$
f'(z)=6z-4.
$$

##### problem 2

Let
$$
f(z)=z^3+\frac{z}{1+i}.
$$
We determine the set of analyticity term by term. The identity function
$$
z\mapsto z
$$
is analytic on $\mathbb{C}$. Therefore
$$
z^2=zz
$$
is analytic on $\mathbb{C}$, and then
$$
z^3=z\cdot z^2
$$
is analytic on $\mathbb{C}$ by another application of the product rule. Since
$$
\frac{1}{1+i}
$$
is a constant, the function
$$
\frac{z}{1+i}
$$
is analytic on $\mathbb{C}$. Therefore
$$
f(z)=z^3+\frac{z}{1+i}
$$
is analytic on $\mathbb{C}$ as a sum of analytic functions. Hence $f$ is entire.

Differentiate:
$$
\begin{aligned}
f'(z)
&=\frac{d}{dz}(z^3)+\frac{d}{dz}\left(\frac{z}{1+i}\right)\\
&=3z^2+\frac{1}{1+i}.
\end{aligned}
$$
If we rationalize the constant,
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{1}{1+i}
&=\frac{1}{1+i}\cdot\frac{1-i}{1-i}\\
&=\frac{1-i}{(1+i)(1-i)}\\
&=\frac{1-i}{1-i^2}\\
&=\frac{1-i}{1-(-1)}\\
&=\frac{1-i}{2}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Therefore
$$
f'(z)=3z^2+\frac{1-i}{2}.
$$

So $f$ is analytic on $\mathbb{C}$, and
$$
f'(z)=3z^2+\frac{1-i}{2}.
$$

##### problem 3

Let
$$
f(z)=\operatorname{Im}z.
$$
Write $z=x+iy$. Then
$$
f(z)=y.
$$
To test differentiability at an arbitrary point $z_0$, use the difference quotient:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{f(z)-f(z_0)}{z-z_0}
&=\frac{\operatorname{Im}z-\operatorname{Im}z_0}{z-z_0}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Let
$$
\Delta z=z-z_0.
$$
Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{f(z)-f(z_0)}{z-z_0}
&=\frac{\operatorname{Im}(z_0+\Delta z)-\operatorname{Im}z_0}{\Delta z}\\
&=\frac{\operatorname{Im}\Delta z}{\Delta z}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Now approach $\Delta z\to 0$ along the real axis:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\Delta z=h,\qquad h\in\mathbb{R}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\operatorname{Im}\Delta z}{\Delta z}
&=\frac{\operatorname{Im}h}{h}\\
&=\frac{0}{h}\\
&=0.
\end{aligned}
$$
Next approach $\Delta z\to 0$ along the imaginary axis:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\Delta z=ih,\qquad h\in\mathbb{R}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\operatorname{Im}\Delta z}{\Delta z}
&=\frac{\operatorname{Im}(ih)}{ih}\\
&=\frac{h}{ih}\\
&=\frac{1}{i}\\
&=-i.
\end{aligned}
$$
The two limiting values are different, so the limit does not exist. Therefore $f$ is not differentiable at $z_0$. Since $z_0$ was arbitrary, $f$ is analytic nowhere on $\mathbb{C}$.

So $f$ is analytic at no point of $\mathbb{C}$, and $f'(z)$ does not exist anywhere.

##### problem 4

Let
$$
f(z)=\left(\frac{z-2+i}{z-1+i}\right)^2.
$$
We first determine where the quotient is defined. The denominator is
$$
z-1+i.
$$
This vanishes exactly when
$$
z-1+i=0.
$$
Solving for $z$, we get
$$
\begin{aligned}
z-1+i&=0\\
z&=1-i.
\end{aligned}
$$
So
$$
\frac{z-2+i}{z-1+i}
$$
is defined for all
$$
z\ne 1-i.
$$
The numerator
$$
z-2+i
$$
is analytic on $\mathbb{C}$, and the denominator
$$
z-1+i
$$
is analytic on $\mathbb{C}$. Therefore the quotient is analytic wherever the denominator is nonzero, namely on
$$
\mathbb{C}\setminus\{1-i\}.
$$
Since squaring preserves analyticity, the function
$$
f(z)=\left(\frac{z-2+i}{z-1+i}\right)^2
$$
is analytic on
$$
\mathbb{C}\setminus\{1-i\}.
$$

Now differentiate. Let
$$
u(z)=\frac{z-2+i}{z-1+i}.
$$
Then
$$
f(z)=(u(z))^2,
$$
so by the chain rule,
$$
f'(z)=2u(z)u'(z).
$$
Compute $u'(z)$ by the quotient rule:
$$
\begin{aligned}
u'(z)
&=\frac{(z-1+i)\cdot 1-(z-2+i)\cdot 1}{(z-1+i)^2}\\
&=\frac{z-1+i-z+2-i}{(z-1+i)^2}\\
&=\frac{1}{(z-1+i)^2}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence
$$
\begin{aligned}
f'(z)
&=2\left(\frac{z-2+i}{z-1+i}\right)\left(\frac{1}{(z-1+i)^2}\right)\\
&=\frac{2(z-2+i)}{(z-1+i)^3}.
\end{aligned}
$$

So $f$ is analytic on $\mathbb{C}\setminus\{1-i\}$, and
$$
f'(z)=\frac{2(z-2+i)}{(z-1+i)^3}.
$$

##### problem 5

Let
$$
f(z)=\frac{1}{z^3+1}.
$$
We first find where the denominator is zero. We solve
$$
z^3+1=0.
$$
This is equivalent to
$$
z^3=-1.
$$
Write
$$
-1=e^{i(\pi+2\pi k)},
\qquad k\in\mathbb{Z}.
$$
Then the cube roots are
$$
z=e^{i(\pi+2\pi k)/3},
\qquad k=0,1,2.
$$
For $k=0$, we get
$$
\begin{aligned}
z
&=e^{i\pi/3}\\
&=\cos\frac{\pi}{3}+i\sin\frac{\pi}{3}\\
&=\frac12+i\frac{\sqrt3}{2}\\
&=\frac{1+i\sqrt3}{2}.
\end{aligned}
$$
For $k=1$, we get
$$
\begin{aligned}
z
&=e^{i\pi}\\
&=\cos\pi+i\sin\pi\\
&=-1.
\end{aligned}
$$
For $k=2$, we get
$$
\begin{aligned}
z
&=e^{i5\pi/3}\\
&=\cos\frac{5\pi}{3}+i\sin\frac{5\pi}{3}\\
&=\frac12-i\frac{\sqrt3}{2}\\
&=\frac{1-i\sqrt3}{2}.
\end{aligned}
$$
So the denominator vanishes exactly at
$$
-1,\qquad \frac{1+i\sqrt3}{2},\qquad \frac{1-i\sqrt3}{2}.
$$
The numerator $1$ is analytic on $\mathbb{C}$, and the denominator $z^3+1$ is analytic on $\mathbb{C}$. Therefore the quotient is analytic wherever the denominator is nonzero.
Therefore $f$ is analytic on
$$
\mathbb{C}\setminus\left\{-1,\frac{1+i\sqrt{3}}{2},\frac{1-i\sqrt{3}}{2}\right\}.
$$

Differentiate:
$$
\begin{aligned}
f'(z)
&=\frac{d}{dz}\left((z^3+1)^{-1}\right)\\
&=-1\cdot (z^3+1)^{-2}\cdot \frac{d}{dz}(z^3+1)\\
&=-(z^3+1)^{-2}\cdot 3z^2\\
&=-\frac{3z^2}{(z^3+1)^2}.
\end{aligned}
$$

So $f$ is analytic on
$$
\mathbb{C}\setminus\left\{-1,\frac{1+i\sqrt{3}}{2},\frac{1-i\sqrt{3}}{2}\right\},
$$
and
$$
f'(z)=-\frac{3z^2}{(z^3+1)^2}.
$$

##### problem 6

Let
$$
f(z)=8\bar{z}+i.
$$
To test differentiability at an arbitrary point $z_0$, compute the difference quotient:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{f(z)-f(z_0)}{z-z_0}
&=\frac{(8\bar{z}+i)-(8\overline{z_0}+i)}{z-z_0}\\
&=\frac{8(\bar{z}-\overline{z_0})}{z-z_0}\\
&=8\frac{\overline{z-z_0}}{z-z_0}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Let
$$
\Delta z=z-z_0.
$$
Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{f(z)-f(z_0)}{z-z_0}

\end{aligned}

Now approach $\Delta z\to 0$ along the real axis:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\Delta z=h,\qquad h\in\mathbb{R}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
8\frac{\overline{\Delta z}}{\Delta z}
&=8\frac{\overline{h}}{h}\\
&=8\frac{h}{h}\\
&=8.
\end{aligned}
$$
Next approach $\Delta z\to 0$ along the imaginary axis:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\Delta z=ih,\qquad h\in\mathbb{R}.
\end{aligned}

Then

\begin{aligned}
8\frac{\overline{\Delta z}}{\Delta z}
&=8\frac{\overline{ih}}{ih}\\
&=8\frac{-ih}{ih}\\
&=-8.
\end{aligned}

The two limiting values are different, so the derivative does not exist at $z_0$. Since $z_0$ was arbitrary, $f$ is analytic nowhere on $\mathbb{C}$.

Therefore $f$ is analytic at no point of $\mathbb{C}$, and $f'(z)$ does not exist anywhere.

##### problem 7

Let
$$
f(z)=\frac{1}{z^2-(1-2i)z-3-i}.
$$
This is a rational function, so we first find where the denominator vanishes. Let
$$
Q(z)=z^2-(1-2i)z-3-i.
$$
Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
Q(z)
&=z^2-(1-2i)z-3-i\\
&=0.
\end{aligned}
$$
Using the quadratic formula,
$$
\begin{aligned}
z
&=\frac{(1-2i)\pm\sqrt{(1-2i)^2-4(1)(-3-i)}}{2}\\
&=\frac{(1-2i)\pm\sqrt{(1-2i)^2+4(3+i)}}{2}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Now
$$
\begin{aligned}
(1-2i)^2+4(3+i)
&=(1-4i+4i^2)+(12+4i)\\
&=(1-4i-4)+(12+4i)\\
&=9.
\end{aligned}
$$
So
$$
\begin{aligned}
z
&=\frac{(1-2i)\pm 3}{2}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence the two roots are
$$
\begin{aligned}
z_1
&=\frac{1-2i+3}{2}\\
&=\frac{4-2i}{2}\\
&=2-i,
\end{aligned}
$$
and
$$	
\begin{aligned}
z_2
&=\frac{1-2i-3}{2}\\
&=\frac{-2-2i}{2}\\
&=-1-i.
\end{aligned}
$$
So
$$
Q(z)=0
$$
exactly when
$$
z=2-i
\qquad\text{or}\qquad
z=-1-i.
$$
The numerator $1$ is analytic on $\mathbb{C}$, and the denominator $Q(z)$ is analytic on $\mathbb{C}$. Therefore the quotient
$$
\frac{1}{Q(z)}
$$
is analytic wherever $Q(z)\ne 0$, namely on
$$
\mathbb{C}\setminus\{2-i,-1-i\}.
$$

Differentiate:
$$
\begin{aligned}
f'(z)
&=\frac{d}{dz}\left(Q(z)^{-1}\right)\\
&=-Q(z)^{-2}Q'(z).
\end{aligned}

Since
$$
\begin{aligned}

&=\frac{d}{dz}\left(z^2-(1-2i)z-3-i\right)\\
&=2z-(1-2i)\\
&=2z-1+2i,
\end{aligned}
$$
we get
$$
f'(z)=-\frac{2z-1+2i}{\left(z^2-(1-2i)z-3-i\right)^2}.

So $f$ is analytic on $\mathbb{C}\setminus\{2-i,-1-i\}$, and

f'(z)=-\frac{2z-1+2i}{\left(z^2-(1-2i)z-3-i\right)^2}.

##### problem 9

Let
$$
f(z)=z^{2/3},
$$
where we use the principal branch of the power. By definition,
$$
z^{2/3}=e^{(2/3)\operatorname{Log} z},
$$
where $\operatorname{Log} z$ is the principal logarithm. The principal logarithm is analytic on
$$
\mathbb{C}\setminus(-\infty,0],
$$
and the exponential function is entire. Therefore the composition
$$
z\mapsto e^{(2/3)\operatorname{Log} z}
$$
is analytic on
$$
\mathbb{C}\setminus(-\infty,0].
$$
Now differentiate:
$$
\begin{aligned}
f'(z)
&=\frac{d}{dz}\left(e^{(2/3)\operatorname{Log} z}\right)\\
&=e^{(2/3)\operatorname{Log} z}\cdot \frac{d}{dz}\left(\frac{2}{3}\operatorname{Log} z\right)\\
&=e^{(2/3)\operatorname{Log} z}\cdot \frac{2}{3}\cdot \frac{1}{z}\\
&=z^{2/3}\cdot \frac{2}{3}\cdot \frac{1}{z}\\
&=\frac{2}{3}z^{2/3-1}\\
&=\frac{2}{3}z^{-1/3}.
\end{aligned}
$$

So $f$ is analytic on $\mathbb{C}\setminus(-\infty,0]$, and
$$
f'(z)=\frac{2}{3}z^{-1/3}.
$$

The function
$$
w\mapsto w
$$
is analytic everywhere, so $f$ is analytic at every point satisfying
$$
|z|<1.

At such a point,

then in a small neighborhood of $z$ we still have

> [!exercise] Exercise 11
> 18. (a) Show that the derivative of $f(z)=|z|^{2}$ exists at $z=0$ and does not exist at all other points. **(Hint: Proceed as in Example 4.)**
> (b) At which points is $f$ analytic?



**Solution:**

##### problem 18.a

Let
$$
f(z)=|z|^2.
$$
Since
$$
|z|^2=z\bar z,
$$
we compute the derivative at an arbitrary point $z_0$ from the difference quotient
$$
\frac{f(z)-f(z_0)}{z-z_0}.
$$
Let
$$
\Delta z=z-z_0.
$$
Then
$$
z=z_0+\Delta z.
$$
Therefore
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{f(z)-f(z_0)}{z-z_0}
&=\frac{|z|^2-|z_0|^2}{z-z_0}\\
&=\frac{|z_0+\Delta z|^2-|z_0|^2}{\Delta z}\\
&=\frac{(z_0+\Delta z)(\overline{z_0+\Delta z})-z_0\overline{z_0}}{\Delta z}\\
&=\frac{(z_0+\Delta z)(\overline{z_0}+\overline{\Delta z})-z_0\overline{z_0}}{\Delta z}\\
&=\frac{z_0\overline{z_0}+z_0\overline{\Delta z}+\Delta z\,\overline{z_0}+\Delta z\,\overline{\Delta z}-z_0\overline{z_0}}{\Delta z}\\
&=\frac{z_0\overline{\Delta z}+\Delta z\,\overline{z_0}+\Delta z\,\overline{\Delta z}}{\Delta z}\\
&=z_0\frac{\overline{\Delta z}}{\Delta z}+\overline{z_0}+\overline{\Delta z}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Now first consider the case
$$
z_0=0.
$$
Then the formula becomes
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{f(z)-f(0)}{z-0}
&=0\cdot \frac{\overline{\Delta z}}{\Delta z}+\overline{0}+\overline{\Delta z}\\
&=\overline{\Delta z}.
\end{aligned}
$$
As
$$
\Delta z\to 0,
$$
we have
$$
\overline{\Delta z}\to 0.
$$
Therefore
$$
\begin{aligned}
f'(0)
&=\lim_{z\to 0}\frac{f(z)-f(0)}{z}\\
&=0.
\end{aligned}
$$
So the derivative exists at
$$
z=0.
$$

Now consider the case
$$
z_0\ne 0.
$$
We test the limit along two different approaches for $\Delta z\to 0$.

First let
$$
\Delta z=t,
\qquad t\in\mathbb{R}.
$$
Then
$$
\overline{\Delta z}=t
$$
and
$$
\frac{\overline{\Delta z}}{\Delta z}=\frac{t}{t}=1.
$$
Therefore
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{f(z)-f(z_0)}{z-z_0}
&=z_0\frac{\overline{\Delta z}}{\Delta z}+\overline{z_0}+\overline{\Delta z}\\
&=z_0\cdot 1+\overline{z_0}+t\\
&=z_0+\overline{z_0}+t.
\end{aligned}
$$
As
$$
t\to 0,
$$
this tends to
$$
\begin{aligned}
z_0+\overline{z_0}
&=2\operatorname{Re}z_0.
\end{aligned}
$$

Next let
$$
\Delta z=it,
\qquad t\in\mathbb{R}.
$$
Then
$$
\overline{\Delta z}=-it
$$
and
$$
\frac{\overline{\Delta z}}{\Delta z}=\frac{-it}{it}=-1.
$$
Therefore
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{f(z)-f(z_0)}{z-z_0}
&=z_0\frac{\overline{\Delta z}}{\Delta z}+\overline{z_0}+\overline{\Delta z}\\
&=z_0(-1)+\overline{z_0}-it\\
&=-z_0+\overline{z_0}-it.
\end{aligned}
$$
As
$$
t\to 0,
$$
this tends to
$$
\begin{aligned}
-z_0+\overline{z_0}.
\end{aligned}
$$
If we write
$$
z_0=x_0+iy_0,
$$
then
$$
\begin{aligned}
-z_0+\overline{z_0}
&=-(x_0+iy_0)+(x_0-iy_0)\\
&=-2iy_0.
\end{aligned}
$$

So the two directional limits are
$$
2\operatorname{Re}z_0
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
-2i\operatorname{Im}z_0.
$$
For the derivative to exist, these would have to be equal:
$$
2\operatorname{Re}z_0=-2i\operatorname{Im}z_0.
$$
The left-hand side is real and the right-hand side is purely imaginary, so this can happen only if both are zero. Hence we must have
$$
\operatorname{Re}z_0=0
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\operatorname{Im}z_0=0,
$$
that is,
$$
z_0=0.
$$
But in the present case we assumed
$$
z_0\ne 0.
$$
So the two limits are different, and the derivative does not exist.

Therefore the derivative of $f(z)=|z|^2$ exists only at
$$
z=0,
$$
and
$$
f'(0)=0.
$$

##### problem 18.b

A function is analytic at a point only if it is differentiable throughout some open neighborhood of that point. From part `(a)`, the function
$$
f(z)=|z|^2
$$
is differentiable only at the single point
$$
z=0.
$$
There is no open neighborhood of $0$ on which $f$ is differentiable at every point, because every neighborhood of $0$ contains points
$$
z\ne 0,
$$
and at those points the derivative does not exist.

Therefore $f$ is analytic at no point of $\mathbb{C}$. In other words, $f$ is nowhere analytic.



> [!exercise] Exercise 12
> 19. Show that $f(z)=|z|$ is nowhere differentiable. **(Hint: Compute the limit in (1) by letting $z=\alpha z_{0}$ with $\alpha>0$; then $\alpha<0$.)**



**Solution:** Let
$$
f(z)=|z|.
$$
We will show that $f$ is not differentiable at any point $z_0\in\mathbb{C}$.

First consider the point
$$
z_0=0.
$$
Since
$$
f(0)=|0|=0,
$$
the difference quotient is
$$
\frac{f(z)-f(0)}{z-0}=\frac{|z|}{z}.
$$
If we let
$$
z=t,\qquad t>0,
$$
then
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{|z|}{z}
&=\frac{|t|}{t}\\
&=\frac{t}{t}\\
&=1.
\end{aligned}
$$
If instead we let
$$
z=it,\qquad t>0,
$$
then
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{|z|}{z}
&=\frac{|it|}{it}\\
&=\frac{t}{it}\\
&=\frac{1}{i}\\
&=-i.
\end{aligned}
$$
These two limiting values are different, so the derivative does not exist at
$$
z_0=0.
$$

Now let
$$
z_0\ne 0.
$$
Then
$$
\frac{f(z)-f(z_0)}{z-z_0}=\frac{|z|-|z_0|}{z-z_0}.
$$
We examine this quotient along two different approaches to $z_0$.

For the first approach, let
$$
z=\alpha z_0,
\qquad
\alpha>0,
\qquad
\alpha\to 1.
$$
Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{|z|-|z_0|}{z-z_0}
&=\frac{|\alpha z_0|-|z_0|}{\alpha z_0-z_0}\\
&=\frac{\alpha|z_0|-|z_0|}{(\alpha-1)z_0}\\
&=\frac{(\alpha-1)|z_0|}{(\alpha-1)z_0}\\
&=\frac{|z_0|}{z_0}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence along this approach,
$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{|z|-|z_0|}{z-z_0}=\frac{|z_0|}{z_0}.
$$

For the second approach, let
$$
z=(1+it)z_0,
\qquad
t\in\mathbb{R},
\qquad
t\to 0.
$$
Then
$$
z\to z_0
$$
because
$$
1+it\to 1.
$$
Now compute
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{|z|-|z_0|}{z-z_0}
&=\frac{|(1+it)z_0|-|z_0|}{(1+it)z_0-z_0}\\
&=\frac{|1+it|\cdot |z_0|-|z_0|}{it\,z_0}\\
&=\frac{\sqrt{1+t^2}\,|z_0|-|z_0|}{it\,z_0}\\
&=\frac{|z_0|(\sqrt{1+t^2}-1)}{it\,z_0}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Rationalize the factor
$$
\sqrt{1+t^2}-1
$$
by multiplying by
$$
\sqrt{1+t^2}+1.
$$
Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{|z|-|z_0|}{z-z_0}
&=\frac{|z_0|(\sqrt{1+t^2}-1)}{it\,z_0}\cdot
\frac{\sqrt{1+t^2}+1}{\sqrt{1+t^2}+1}\\
&=\frac{|z_0|((1+t^2)-1)}{it\,z_0(\sqrt{1+t^2}+1)}\\
&=\frac{|z_0|t^2}{it\,z_0(\sqrt{1+t^2}+1)}\\
&=\frac{|z_0|t}{iz_0(\sqrt{1+t^2}+1)}.
\end{aligned}
$$
As
$$
t\to 0,
$$
the numerator tends to $0$ and the denominator tends to
$$
2iz_0\ne 0.
$$
Therefore
$$
\lim_{t\to 0}\frac{|z_0|t}{iz_0(\sqrt{1+t^2}+1)}=0,
$$
so along this second approach,
$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{|z|-|z_0|}{z-z_0}=0.
$$

Thus for
$$
z_0\ne 0
$$
the difference quotient has two different limiting values,
$$
\frac{|z_0|}{z_0}
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
0.
$$
Moreover,
$$
\left|\frac{|z_0|}{z_0}\right|=\frac{|z_0|}{|z_0|}=1,
$$
so
$$
\frac{|z_0|}{z_0}\ne 0.
$$
Hence the derivative does not exist at any nonzero point.

Since the derivative does not exist at
$$
z_0=0
$$
and also does not exist at any point
$$
z_0\ne 0,
$$
we conclude that
$$
f(z)=|z|
$$
is nowhere differentiable.

$$
Now
$$
f'(g(z))=q(g(z))^{q-1},
$$
and since
$$
q>0,
$$
this can vanish only if
$$
g(z)=0.
$$
But if
$$
g(z)=0,
$$
then
$$
h(z)=(g(z))^q=0,
$$
so
$$
z^p=0.
$$
For the branch domains under consideration, we are working on a domain where the chosen branch of $\log z$ is defined, so in particular
$$
z\ne 0.
$$
Therefore
$$
g(z)\ne 0,
$$
and hence
$$
f'(g(z))\ne 0.
$$

All the hypotheses of Theorem 4 are now satisfied. Therefore
$$
g'(z)=\frac{h'(z)}{f'(g(z))}.
$$
Compute each term:
$$
\begin{aligned}
h'(z)
&=\frac{d}{dz}(z^p)\\
&=pz^{p-1},
\end{aligned}
$$
and
$$
\begin{aligned}
f'(g(z))
&=q(g(z))^{q-1}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus
$$
\begin{aligned}
g'(z)
&=\frac{pz^{p-1}}{q(g(z))^{q-1}}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Now use the identity
$$
(g(z))^q=z^p.
$$
Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
g'(z)
&=\frac{pz^{p-1}}{q(g(z))^{q-1}}\cdot \frac{g(z)}{g(z)}\\
&=\frac{pz^{p-1}g(z)}{q(g(z))^q}\\
&=\frac{pz^{p-1}g(z)}{qz^p}\\
&=\frac{p\,g(z)}{qz}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Since
$$
g(z)=z^{p/q},
$$
we obtain
$$
\boxed{
\frac{d}{dz}z^{p/q}=\frac{p}{qz}z^{p/q}.
}
$$

This uses the same chosen branch of
$$
z^{p/q}
$$
on both sides, exactly as required.

##### problem 23.a

Let
$$
h(z)=\frac{1}{f(z)},
$$
and suppose that
$$
f(z_0)\ne 0.
$$
We compute the derivative of $h$ at $z_0$ from the definition:
$$
\begin{aligned}
h'(z_0)
&=\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{h(z)-h(z_0)}{z-z_0}\\
&=\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{\frac{1}{f(z)}-\frac{1}{f(z_0)}}{z-z_0}.
\end{aligned}

Now combine the two fractions in the numerator:
$$
\begin{aligned}

&=\frac{1}{(f(z_0))^2}.
\end{aligned}

Also,
$$
\frac{f(z)-f(z_0)}{z-z_0}\to f'(z_0).
$$
Therefore
$$
\begin{aligned}
h'(z_0)
&=-\left(\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{1}{f(z)f(z_0)}\right)

&=-\frac{f'(z_0)}{(f(z_0))^2}.
\end{aligned}

Let
$$
h(z)=f(z)g(z),
$$
and fix a point
$$

We will use Theorem 5 for both $f$ and $g$ at $z_0$.

Since $f$ is differentiable at $z_0$, Theorem 5 gives

\epsilon_1(z)\to 0

$$
Similarly, since $g$ is differentiable at $z_0$, Theorem 5 gives

g(z)=g(z_0)+g'(z_0)(z-z_0)+\epsilon_2(z)(z-z_0),

where

\qquad\text{as }z\to z_0.

Now multiply the two expansions:
$$
\begin{aligned}
h(z)
&=f(z)g(z)\\
&=\Bigl(f(z_0)+f'(z_0)(z-z_0)+\epsilon_1(z)(z-z_0)\Bigr)
\Bigl(g(z_0)+g'(z_0)(z-z_0)+\epsilon_2(z)(z-z_0)\Bigr).
\end{aligned}

Expand the product:
$$
\begin{aligned}
h(z)
&=f(z_0)g(z_0)\\
&\quad +f(z_0)g'(z_0)(z-z_0)\\
&\quad +f(z_0)\epsilon_2(z)(z-z_0)\\

&\quad +\epsilon_1(z)\epsilon_2(z)(z-z_0)^2.
\end{aligned}

Now group the constant term and the terms containing one factor of $(z-z_0)$:

\begin{aligned}
h(z)
&=f(z_0)g(z_0)\\
&\quad +\Bigl(f(z_0)g'(z_0)+f'(z_0)g(z_0)\Bigr)(z-z_0)\\
&\quad +f(z_0)\epsilon_2(z)(z-z_0)\\
&\quad +\epsilon_1(z)g(z_0)(z-z_0)\\
&\quad +f'(z_0)g'(z_0)(z-z_0)^2\\
&\quad +f'(z_0)\epsilon_2(z)(z-z_0)^2\\
&\quad +\epsilon_1(z)g'(z_0)(z-z_0)^2\\
&\quad +\epsilon_1(z)\epsilon_2(z)(z-z_0)^2.
\end{aligned}
$$

Factor one copy of $(z-z_0)$ from the remaining terms:
$$
\begin{aligned}
h(z)
&=f(z_0)g(z_0)\\
&\quad +\Bigl(f(z_0)g'(z_0)+f'(z_0)g(z_0)\Bigr)(z-z_0)\\
&\quad +(z-z_0)\Bigl[f(z_0)\epsilon_2(z)+\epsilon_1(z)g(z_0)\Bigr]\\

$$

(fg)'(z_0)=f'(z_0)g(z_0)+f(z_0)g'(z_0).
}
$$

&=14z(z^2+1)^6.
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence
$$
\begin{aligned}
f'(i)
&=14i(i^2+1)^6\\
&=14i(-1+1)^6\\
&=14i\cdot 0^6\\
&=0.
\end{aligned}
$$
Therefore
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lim_{z\to i}\frac{(z^2+1)^7}{z^6+1}
&=\frac{f'(i)}{g'(i)}\\
&=\frac{0}{6i}\\
&=0.
\end{aligned}
$$
So
$$
\boxed{
\lim_{z\to i}\frac{(z^2+1)^7}{z^6+1}=0.
}
$$

##### problem 28.b
Let
$$
f(z)=z^3+(1-3i)z^2+(i-3)z+2+i
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
g(z)=z-i.
$$
Again we first check that this is a $0/0$ form at
$$
z=i.
$$
Clearly,
$$
\begin{aligned}
g(i)
&=i-i\\
&=0.
\end{aligned}
$$
Now compute $f(i)$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
f(i)
&=i^3+(1-3i)i^2+(i-3)i+2+i\\
&=-i+(1-3i)(-1)+(i^2-3i)+2+i\\
&=-i+(-1+3i)+(-1-3i)+2+i\\
&=\bigl(-1-1+2\bigr)+\bigl(-i+3i-3i+i\bigr)\\
&=0+0\\
&=0.
\end{aligned}
$$
Also,
$$
\begin{aligned}
g'(z)
&=\frac{d}{dz}(z-i)\\
&=1.
\end{aligned}
$$
so
$$
g'(i)=1\ne 0.
$$
Therefore L'Hospital's rule applies:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lim_{z\to i}\frac{z^3+(1-3i)z^2+(i-3)z+2+i}{z-i}
&=\frac{f'(i)}{g'(i)}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Now differentiate:
$$
\begin{aligned}
f'(z)
&=\frac{d}{dz}\left(z^3+(1-3i)z^2+(i-3)z+2+i\right)\\
&=3z^2+2(1-3i)z+(i-3).
\end{aligned}
$$
Evaluate at
$$
z=i.
$$
Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
f'(i)
&=3i^2+2(1-3i)i+(i-3)\\
&=3(-1)+2(i-3i^2)+(i-3)\\
&=-3+2(i+3)+(i-3)\\
&=-3+(2i+6)+(i-3)\\
&=\bigl(-3+6-3\bigr)+\bigl(2i+i\bigr)\\
&=0+3i\\
&=3i.
\end{aligned}
$$
Since
$$
g'(i)=1,
$$
we obtain
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lim_{z\to i}\frac{z^3+(1-3i)z^2+(i-3)z+2+i}{z-i}
&=\frac{f'(i)}{g'(i)}\\
&=\frac{3i}{1}\\
&=3i.
\end{aligned}
$$
So
$$
\boxed{
\lim_{z\to i}\frac{z^3+(1-3i)z^2+(i-3)z+2+i}{z-i}=3i.
}
$$
