

> [!review]
> 1. What does it mean for a complex-valued function to have a limit at a point, and why is this a stronger assertion than the corresponding notion for real-valued functions?
> 2. Why does the definition of a limit not require the function to be defined at the point $z_0$ ?
> 3. What does $|f(z)-L|$ represent geometrically, and why does the equivalence $\lim _{z \rightarrow z_0} f(z)=L \Longleftrightarrow \lim _{z \rightarrow z_0}|f(z)-L|=0$ hold?



##### problem 1
A complex-valued function $f$ has limit $L$ at $z_0$ if for every $\epsilon>0$ there is a $\delta>0$ such that

$$
|f(z)-L|<\epsilon
\qquad \text{whenever} \qquad
0<|z-z_0|<\delta.
$$

This means that once $z$ is taken sufficiently close to $z_0$ in the domain, the value $f(z)$ is forced to lie arbitrarily close to $L$ in the codomain.

This is stronger than the real-variable version because in the complex plane there are infinitely many ways for $z$ to approach $z_0$: along straight lines, curves, spirals, and any other path in the plane. For the limit to exist, all such approaches must drive $f(z)$ to the same number $L$. On the real line, by contrast, there are only two directions of approach, from the left and from the right.


##### problem 2
The definition does not require $f$ to be defined at $z_0$ because a limit describes the behavior of $f(z)$ near $z_0$, not necessarily at the point itself. The condition

$$
0<|z-z_0|<\delta
$$

defines a deleted neighborhood of $z_0$, so the point $z_0$ is explicitly excluded.

This is important because a function may have a perfectly good limit even if it is undefined at $z_0$, or even if its value at $z_0$ is different from the limiting value. Limits concern nearby values; continuity is the extra condition that the function is defined at the point and that its value agrees with the limit.



##### problem 3

$|f(z)-L|$ is the Euclidean distance in the complex plane between the point $f(z)$ and the point $L$. So geometrically it measures how far the function value is from its proposed limit.

The equivalence

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z)=L
\qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad
\lim_{z\to z_0}|f(z)-L|=0
$$

holds because the definition of the limit is stated exactly in terms of this distance. Saying that $f(z)\to L$ means that for every $\epsilon>0$, the distance $|f(z)-L|$ can be made smaller than $\epsilon$ by taking $z$ close enough to $z_0$. But that is precisely the statement that the real-valued quantity $|f(z)-L|$ tends to $0$.




##### problem 1

There are three definitions.

First,

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z)=\infty
$$

means that for every $M>0$ there exists $\delta>0$ such that

$$
0<|z-z_0|<\delta
\qquad \Longrightarrow \qquad
|f(z)|>M.
$$

Second,

$$
\lim_{z\to\infty} f(z)=L
$$

means that for every $\epsilon>0$ there exists $R>0$ such that

$$
|z|>R
\qquad \Longrightarrow \qquad
|f(z)-L|<\epsilon.
$$

Third,

$$
\lim_{z\to\infty} f(z)=\infty
$$

means that for every $M>0$ there exists $R>0$ such that

$$
|z|>R
\qquad \Longrightarrow \qquad
|f(z)|>M.
$$

##### problem 2

In the complex plane, $z\to\infty$ means that $|z|\to\infty$. Unlike the real line, there is no separate $+\infty$ and $-\infty$, because points in $\mathbb{C}$ can leave every bounded set in infinitely many directions.

So the relevant notion is not a direction but unbounded distance from the origin. That is why the complex plane uses a single point at infinity in this limiting sense.

##### problem 3

A limit as $z\to\infty$ can be converted into a limit at $0$ by setting

$$
w=\frac{1}{z}.
$$

Then

$$
z\to\infty
\qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad
w=\frac{1}{z}\to 0.
$$

Hence

$$
\lim_{z\to\infty} f(z)=L
\qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad
\lim_{w\to 0} f\!\left(\frac{1}{w}\right)=L,
$$

and similarly

$$
\lim_{z\to\infty} f(z)=\infty
\qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad
\lim_{w\to 0} f\!\left(\frac{1}{w}\right)=\infty.
$$

This works because large values of $|z|$ correspond exactly to small values of $\left|\frac{1}{z}\right|$.

##### problem 4

These limits reduce to real-valued nonnegative limits by taking moduli:

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z)=\infty
\qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad
\lim_{z\to z_0}|f(z)|=\infty,
$$

$$
\lim_{z\to\infty} f(z)=L
\qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad
\lim_{z\to\infty}|f(z)-L|=0,
$$

and

$$
\lim_{z\to\infty} f(z)=\infty
\qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad
\lim_{z\to\infty}|f(z)|=\infty.
$$

This is useful because it turns complex limits involving infinity into ordinary real-variable statements about sizes.

##### problem 5

Using the reduction to a limit at $0$,

$$
\lim_{z\to\infty}\frac{1}{z}
=
\lim_{w\to 0}\frac{1}{1/w}
=
\lim_{w\to 0} w
=
0.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\lim_{z\to\infty}\frac{1}{z}=0.
$$

##### problem 6

Let $c$ be a constant and let $n$ be a positive integer. Then

$$
\lim_{z\to\infty}\frac{c}{z^n}
=
\lim_{w\to 0}\frac{c}{(1/w)^n}
=
\lim_{w\to 0} c w^n
=
c\lim_{w\to 0} w^n
=
c\cdot 0
=
0.
$$

Hence

$$
\lim_{z\to\infty}\frac{c}{z^n}=0.
$$

When we define the derivative of a complex-valued function in Section 2.3, we will model our definition after the familiar derivative of a real-valued function from calculus. As you recall, such a derivative was defined by taking limits. Therefore, before we study differentiation, we must define limits of complex functions. We will also define continuous functions by appealing to limits.



> [!definition] limit of a complex-valued function
> We say that a complex-valued function $f(z)$ has a limit $L$ as $z$ approaches $z_{0}$, and we write
> 
> $$
> \lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}} f(z)=L \quad \text { or } \quad f(z) \rightarrow L \text { as } z \rightarrow z_{0},
> $$
> 
> if given any $\epsilon>0$, there exists a $\delta>0$ such that
> 
> $$
> |f(z)-L|<\epsilon \quad \text { whenever } 0<\left|z-z_{0}\right|<\delta .
> $$
> 


If the limit of a function exists at a point, then it is unique (Exercise 27). This is referred to as the uniqueness property of limits.


> [!figure] Figure 1
> 
> ![](./images/af4f9593-dec7-4bc0-8d78-d25500419ec9-09_461_1109_1442_746.jpg)
> 
> To say that $f(z) \rightarrow L$ as $z \rightarrow z_{0}$ is a strong assertion; it states that no matter how $z$ approaches $z_{0}$ (and there are many possible ways in the plane), the distance from $f(z)$ to $L$ will tend to zero. By contrast, for limits of functions on the real line, a point $x$ can approach $x_{0}$ from only two directions.


Geometrically, interpreting the absolute value $|f(z)-L|$ as the distance between $f(z)$ and $L$, we see from (1) that the function $f(z)$ has limit $L$ as $z \rightarrow z_{0}$ if and only if the distance from $f(z)$ to $L$ tends to zero as $z$ tends to $z_{0}$. Thus, $\lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}} f(z)=L$ if and only if

$$
\lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}}|f(z)-L|=0
$$

Note that in (1) the value of $f$ at $z_{0}$ is immaterial, and in fact $f$ need not even be defined at $z_{0}$. Note also that the expression $|f(z)-L|$ that appears
in (1) and (2) is real-valued. So even though we will be computing limits of complex-valued functions, we will be working with real quantities.



> [!exercise] Exercise 1: Two simple limits
> Prove that:
> (a) $\lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}} z=z_{0}$; and 
> (b) $\lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}} c=c$, where $c$ is a constant.


##### problem a

We verify the definition directly. Let $\epsilon>0$ be given and choose

$$
\delta=\epsilon.
$$

Then

$$
0<|z-z_0|<\delta
=
\epsilon
\qquad \Longrightarrow \qquad
|z-z_0|<\epsilon.
$$

Since here $f(z)=z$, we have

$$
|f(z)-z_0|
=
|z-z_0|
<
\epsilon.
$$

Therefore the $\epsilon$-$\delta$ condition holds, and so

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} z=z_0.
$$


##### problem b

Let $\epsilon>0$ be given. Since $f(z)=c$ is constant, we may choose any positive $\delta$; for instance,

$$
\delta=1.
$$

Then

$$
0<|z-z_0|<\delta
\qquad \Longrightarrow \qquad
|f(z)-c|
=
|c-c|
=
0
<
\epsilon.
$$

Thus the definition of limit is satisfied, and hence

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} c=c.
$$

> 1. What is the limit of a linear combinations of functions?
> 2. What is the limit of a product of functions?
> 3. What is the limit of a quotient of functions? Are there any restrictions?
> 4. What is the limit of a composition of functions?
> 5. What is a bounded function?

##### problem 1

If $\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z)=A$ and $\lim_{z\to z_0} g(z)=B$, then for any complex constants $c_1,c_2$,

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}\bigl[c_1f(z)+c_2g(z)\bigr]=c_1A+c_2B.
$$

So the limit of a linear combination is the same linear combination of the limits, provided the individual limits exist.

##### problem 2

If $\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z)=A$ and $\lim_{z\to z_0} g(z)=B$, then

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}[f(z)g(z)]=AB.
$$

Thus the limit of a product is the product of the limits, again assuming both limits exist.

##### problem 3

If $\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z)=A$ and $\lim_{z\to z_0} g(z)=B$, then

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{f(z)}{g(z)}=\frac{A}{B},
$$

provided $B\ne 0$.

The restriction is necessary because division by zero is undefined. If the denominator tends to $0$, the quotient need not have a finite limit and must be analyzed separately.

##### problem 4

If

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} g(z)=w_0
\qquad \text{and} \qquad
\lim_{w\to w_0} f(w)=A,
$$

then

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} f(g(z))=A.
$$

So the limit of a composition is obtained by passing first to the limit of the inner function and then taking the limit of the outer function at that resulting point.

##### problem 5

A function $g(z)$ is bounded on a set $S$ if there exists a positive real number $M$ such that

$$
|g(z)|\le M
\qquad \text{for all } z\in S.
$$

So boundedness means the values of the function stay within some fixed distance of the origin throughout the set.

##### problem 6

If $f(z)\to 0$ as $z\to z_0$, then there are two useful ways to force another related function to go to $0$ as well.

First, if $|g(z)|\le |f(z)|$ in a deleted neighborhood of $z_0$, then $g(z)\to 0$ by the squeeze theorem.

Second, if $h(z)$ is bounded in a deleted neighborhood of $z_0$, then

$$
f(z)h(z)\to 0.
$$

This is because multiplying something that tends to zero by a bounded function cannot prevent the product from tending to zero.

##### problem 1

A discontinuity at $z_0$ is removable if the limit

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z)
$$

exists as a finite complex number, but the function either is not defined at $z_0$ or is defined there with the wrong value. Removing the discontinuity means redefining the function at that one point so that

$$
f(z_0)=\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z).
$$

A discontinuity is nonremovable if no such redefinition can make the function continuous at the point.

##### problem 2

You can repair a discontinuity at a single point only when the limit of the function at that point already exists. In other words, there must be some complex number $L$ such that

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z)=L.
$$

Then redefining the function by setting

$$
f(z_0)=L
$$

makes the function continuous at $z_0$. If the limit does not exist, then no choice of a single value at $z_0$ can force continuity there.

##### problem 3

The uniqueness property says that if a limit exists, it must be unique. So to prove that a limit does not exist, it is enough to produce two different candidate limits coming from two different approaches to the same point.

If, for example, one can show that along one path

$$
f(z)\to L
$$

and along another path

$$
f(z)\to L'
\qquad \text{with} \qquad
L\ne L',
$$

then the full limit at the point cannot exist, because an actual limit would have to be the same no matter how the point is approached.

##### problem 4

Concretely, you must find two curves $C$ and $C'$ that both approach $z_0$ and then compute the restricted limits of $f$ along those curves. If

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0,\ z\in C} f(z)=L
$$

but

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0,\ z\in C'} f(z)=L'
\qquad \text{and} \qquad
L\ne L',
$$

then

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z)
$$

does not exist.

Finding two such curves suffices because a genuine complex limit must be independent of path. Once two valid approaches give different answers, the possibility of a single limit is ruled out.

##### problem 5

This method is especially natural in the complex or multivariable setting because there are infinitely many directions and curves along which a point can be approached. Path dependence is therefore a genuine issue.

For a function of one real variable, by contrast, there are only two basic approaches to a point: from the left and from the right. So the analogous test is simply to compare the left-hand and right-hand limits. In complex analysis, the abundance of possible approaches means that checking two carefully chosen curves is a standard way to expose failure of a limit.

Computing more complicated limits by recourse to the $\epsilon \delta$-definition (1) is not always easy. To simplify this task, we will use properties of limits.


> [!theorem] Theorem 1: Operations with Limits
> Suppose $\lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}} f(z)$ and $\lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}} g(z)$ both exist and $c_{1}, c_{2}$ are complex constants, Then
> 
> 
> $$
> \lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}}\left[c_{1} f(z)+c_{2} g(z)\right]=c_{1} \lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}} f(z)+c_{2} \lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}} g(z), \tag{3}
> $$
> 
> $$
> \lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}}[f(z) g(z)]=\lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}} f(z), \lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}} g(z), \tag{4}
> $$
> 
> (5) $\lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}}\left[\frac{f(z)}{g(z)}\right]=\frac{\lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}} f(z)}{\lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}} g(z)}$, provided $\lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}} g(z) \neq 0$.
> 
> If $\lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}} g(z)=w_{0}$ and $\lim _{w \rightarrow w_{0}} f(w)=A$, then
> 
> 
> $$
> \lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}} f(g(z))=A=f\left(\lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}} g(z)\right) \tag{6}
> $$
> 
> 



The function $f(g(z))$ is called the composition of $f$ and $g$ and is also denoted $(f \circ g)(z)$. The proofs of (3)-(6) are similar to the proofs of the corresponding results from calculus. They are left to the exercises. Next, we illustrate these properties with some applications.

> [!theorem] Theorem 2: The Squeeze Theorem
> (i) Suppose that $f(z) \rightarrow 0$ as $z \rightarrow z_0$ and $|g(z)| \leq|f(z)|$ in a deleted neighborhood of $z_0$. Then $g(z) \rightarrow 0$ as $z \rightarrow z_0$.
> (ii) Suppose that $f(z) \rightarrow 0$ as $z \rightarrow z_0$ and $g(z)$ is bounded in a deleted neighborhood of $z_0$. Then $f(z) g(z) \rightarrow 0$ as $z \rightarrow z_0$.





> [!exercise] Exercise 2: Operations on limits
> Suppose that $\lim _{z \rightarrow i} f(z)=2+i$ and $\lim _{z \rightarrow i} g(z)=3-i$. Find
> 
> $$
> L=\lim _{z \rightarrow i}\left[(f(z))^{2}+\frac{(3+i) g(z)}{z}\right]
> $$
> 

Using the limit laws,

$$
\begin{aligned}
L
&=
\lim_{z\to i}\left[(f(z))^2+\frac{(3+i)g(z)}{z}\right] \\
&=
\lim_{z\to i}(f(z))^2+\lim_{z\to i}\frac{(3+i)g(z)}{z} \\
&=
\left(\lim_{z\to i} f(z)\right)^2+(3+i)\lim_{z\to i}\frac{g(z)}{z} \\
&=
(2+i)^2+(3+i)\frac{\lim_{z\to i} g(z)}{\lim_{z\to i} z} \\
&=
(2+i)^2+(3+i)\frac{3-i}{i} \\
&=
\left(2^2+2\cdot 2\cdot i+i^2\right)+\frac{(3+i)(3-i)}{i} \\
&=
\left(4+4i-1\right)+\frac{9-3i+3i-i^2}{i} \\
&=
(3+4i)+\frac{9+1}{i} \\
&=
3+4i+\frac{10}{i} \\
&=
3+4i-10i \\
&=
3-6i.
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore,

$$
L=3-6i.
$$

g(z)=y e^{i/|z|}.

\left|e^{i/|z|}\right|=1.

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
|g(z)|
&=

&=
|y|\left|e^{i/|z|}\right| \\

|y|.
\end{aligned}

Now if $z=x+iy$, then

$$
|z|
=

\ge

|y|.

So

$$
|g(z)|=|y|\le |z|.

$$
|g(z)|\to 0,

1. Does the limit of a complex function reduce to limits of its real and imaginary parts separately?




> [!review]
> 1. Given $f(z)=u(x, y)+i v(x, y)$, what is the relationship between the existence and value of $\lim _{z \rightarrow z_0} f(z)$ and the existence and values of the two real limits $\lim u(x, y)$ and $\lim v(x, y)$ ? Is convergence of $f$ equivalent to simultaneous convergence of its real and imaginary parts, or only implied by it (or only implies it)? If so, prove it.
> 2. If I already know that $u$ and $v$ each approach real limits $a$ and $b$ as $(x, y) \rightarrow\left(x_0, y_0\right)$, am I entitled to conclude that $f$ approaches the single complex number $a+i b-$ and conversely, if I know $f \rightarrow L$, can I automatically read off the limits of its real and imaginary parts from $\operatorname{Re} L$ and $\operatorname{Im} L$ ? If so, prove it.


##### problem 1

The convergence of $f$ is equivalent to the simultaneous convergence of its real and imaginary parts. More precisely, if

$$
f(z)=u(x,y)+iv(x,y)
\qquad \text{and} \qquad
L=a+ib,
$$

then

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z)=L
\qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad
\lim_{z\to z_0} u(x,y)=a
\text{ and }
\lim_{z\to z_0} v(x,y)=b.
$$

To prove the forward implication, assume

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z)=L.
$$

Then

$$
f(z)-L=(u(x,y)-a)+i(v(x,y)-b).
$$

Using

$$
|\operatorname{Re} w|\le |w|
\qquad \text{and} \qquad
|\operatorname{Im} w|\le |w|,
$$

we get

$$
|u(x,y)-a|
=
|\operatorname{Re}(f(z)-L)|
\le
|f(z)-L|
$$

and

$$
|v(x,y)-b|
=
|\operatorname{Im}(f(z)-L)|
\le
|f(z)-L|.
$$

Since $|f(z)-L|\to 0$, both $|u(x,y)-a|$ and $|v(x,y)-b|$ tend to $0$, so

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} u(x,y)=a
\qquad \text{and} \qquad
\lim_{z\to z_0} v(x,y)=b.
$$



##### problem 2

Conversely, assume

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} u(x,y)=a
\qquad \text{and} \qquad
\lim_{z\to z_0} v(x,y)=b.
$$

Then

$$
f(z)-(a+ib)=(u(x,y)-a)+i(v(x,y)-b).
$$

By the inequality

$$
|w|\le |\operatorname{Re} w|+|\operatorname{Im} w|,
$$

we have

$$
|f(z)-(a+ib)|
\le
|u(x,y)-a|+|v(x,y)-b|.
$$

The right-hand side tends to $0+0=0$, so

$$
|f(z)-(a+ib)|\to 0.
$$

Hence

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z)=a+ib.
$$

So yes: if $u\to a$ and $v\to b$, then $f\to a+ib$; and if $f\to L$, then automatically

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} u(x,y)=\operatorname{Re} L,
\qquad
\lim_{z\to z_0} v(x,y)=\operatorname{Im} L.
$$

Thus the limit of a complex-valued function can be read off exactly from the limits of its real and imaginary parts.

\lim_{z\to\infty} f(z)=L
\qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad
\lim_{w\to 0} f\!\left(\frac{1}{w}\right)=L,
$$

and similarly

$$
\lim_{z\to\infty} f(z)=\infty
\qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad
\lim_{w\to 0} f\!\left(\frac{1}{w}\right)=\infty.
$$

This works because large values of $|z|$ correspond exactly to small values of $\left|\frac{1}{z}\right|$.

##### problem 4

These limits reduce to real-valued nonnegative limits by taking moduli:

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z)=\infty
\qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad
\lim_{z\to z_0}|f(z)|=\infty,
$$

$$
\lim_{z\to\infty} f(z)=L
\qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad
\lim_{z\to\infty}|f(z)-L|=0,
$$

and

$$
\lim_{z\to\infty} f(z)=\infty
\qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad
\lim_{z\to\infty}|f(z)|=\infty.
$$

This is useful because it turns complex limits involving infinity into ordinary real-variable statements about sizes.

=
\lim_{w\to 0}\frac{1}{1/w}
=
\lim_{w\to 0} w
=
0.
$$

Therefore,

We have avoided thus far dealing with limits that involve $\infty$. What do we mean by statements such as $\lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}} f(z)=\infty$ or $\lim _{z \rightarrow \infty} f(z)=L$ or even $\lim _{z \rightarrow \infty} f(z)=\infty$ ? We answer these questions and complete our discussion of limits by introducing the following definitions.

> [!definition] Limits Involving Infinity
> (i) We write $\lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}} f(z)=\infty$ to mean that for any $M>0$ there is a $\delta>0$ such that $\left|z-z_{0}\right|<\delta \Rightarrow|f(z)|>M$.
> (ii) We write $\lim _{z \rightarrow \infty} f(z)=L$ to mean that for any $\epsilon>0$ there is an $R>0$ such that $|z|>R \Rightarrow|f(z)-L|<\epsilon$.
> (iii) We write $\lim _{z \rightarrow \infty} f(z)=\infty$ to mean that for any $M>0$ there is an $R>0$ such that $|z|>R \Rightarrow|f(z)|>M$.

Looking at these definitions, we see that $z \rightarrow \infty$ means that the real quantity $|z| \rightarrow \infty$, and similarly $f(z) \rightarrow \infty$ means that $|f(z)| \rightarrow \infty$. Hence

$$
\lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}} f(z)=\infty \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad \lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}}|f(z)|=\infty ;
$$

$$
\lim _{z \rightarrow \infty} f(z)=L \Leftrightarrow \lim _{z \rightarrow \infty}|f(z)-L|=0 ;
$$

$$
\lim _{z \rightarrow \infty} f(z)=\infty \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad \lim _{z \rightarrow \infty}|f(z)|=\infty .
$$

Limits at infinity can also be reduced to limits at $z_{0}=0$ by means of the inversion $1 / z$. The idea is that taking the limit as $z \rightarrow \infty$ of $f(z)$ is the same thing as taking the limit as $z \rightarrow 0$ of $f\left(\frac{1}{z}\right)$. So you can check that

$$
\lim _{z \rightarrow \infty} f(z)=L \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad \lim _{z \rightarrow 0} f\left(\frac{1}{z}\right)=L
$$

and

$$
\lim _{z \rightarrow \infty} f(z)=\infty \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad \lim _{z \rightarrow 0} f\left(\frac{1}{z}\right)=\infty .
$$

These equivalent statements are sometimes useful. For example, appealing to (15), we have

$$
\lim _{z \rightarrow \infty} \frac{1}{z}=\lim _{z \rightarrow 0} \frac{1}{1 / z}=\lim _{z \rightarrow 0} z=0
$$

Similarly, for any constant $c$ and positive integer $n$, we have

$$
\lim _{z \rightarrow \infty} \frac{c}{z^{n}}=\lim _{z \rightarrow 0} \frac{c}{1 / z^{n}}=\lim _{z \rightarrow 0} c z^{n}=0
$$

> 2. Real-variable intuition suggests that $e^{-x} \rightarrow 0$ as $x \rightarrow \infty$, so one might expect $e^{-z} \rightarrow 0$ as $z \rightarrow \infty$ in the complex plane. Why does this expectation fail, and what general lesson does it carry about importing limit intuition from real analysis into complex analysis?

##### problem 1

A complex-valued function $f$ is continuous at $z_0$ if $f$ is defined on a neighborhood of $z_0$ and

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z)=f(z_0).
$$

It is continuous on a set $S$ if it is continuous at every point of $S$.

So continuity means that as $z$ approaches $z_0$, the function values approach the value the function already has at $z_0$.

##### problem 2

The expectation fails because in the complex plane, $z\to\infty$ means that $|z|\to\infty$ along every possible direction, not just along the positive real axis. If $z=x+iy$, then

$$
e^{-z}=e^{-x}e^{-iy}.
$$

Along the positive real axis, where $z=x\to\infty$, we do get $e^{-x}\to 0$. But along the negative real axis, where $z=-x$ with $x\to\infty$,

$$
e^{-z}=e^{x}\to\infty.
$$

So the function does not approach a single limit as $z\to\infty$.

The general lesson is that real-variable intuition must be used cautiously in complex analysis. A complex limit must behave the same way along every path of approach, and different directions can produce very different behavior.

##### problem 3

If $f$ and $g$ are continuous at $z_0$, and $c_1,c_2$ are complex constants, then

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}\bigl(c_1f(z)+c_2g(z)\bigr)
=
c_1\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)+c_2\lim_{z\to z_0}g(z)
=
c_1f(z_0)+c_2g(z_0).
$$

So $c_1f+c_2g$ is continuous at $z_0$.

Similarly,

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z)g(z)
=
\left(\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z)\right)\left(\lim_{z\to z_0} g(z)\right)
=
f(z_0)g(z_0),
$$

so $fg$ is continuous at $z_0$.

For quotients, if in addition $g(z_0)\ne 0$, then continuity of $g$ gives $\lim_{z\to z_0} g(z)=g(z_0)\ne 0$, and therefore

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{f(z)}{g(z)}
=
\frac{\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z)}{\lim_{z\to z_0} g(z)}
=
\frac{f(z_0)}{g(z_0)}.
$$

Thus $\frac{f}{g}$ is continuous at $z_0$ provided $g(z_0)\ne 0$. The natural obstruction is division by zero.

##### problem 4

Yes. If $g$ is continuous at $z_0$ and $f$ is continuous at $g(z_0)$, then

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} g(z)=g(z_0).
$$

Applying continuity of $f$ at the point $g(z_0)$,

$$
\lim_{w\to g(z_0)} f(w)=f(g(z_0)).
$$

Hence, by the composition law for limits,

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} f(g(z))=f(g(z_0)),
$$

so $f\circ g$ is continuous at $z_0$.

The same conclusion holds when the inner function $g$ is real-valued and $f$ is a real-variable function continuous at $x_0=g(z_0)$. Then

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} g(z)=g(z_0)=x_0,
$$

and continuity of the real function $f$ at $x_0$ gives

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} f(g(z))=f(g(z_0)).
$$

So the answer does not change; the composition is continuous in either setting, provided the outer function is continuous at the relevant point.

##### problem 5

Yes. If

$$
f(z)=u(x,y)+iv(x,y),
$$

then $f$ is continuous if and only if $u$ and $v$ are continuous.

If $f$ is continuous at $z_0$ and $f(z_0)=a+ib$, then

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z)=f(z_0)=a+ib.
$$

By the theorem on real and imaginary parts of limits,

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} u(x,y)=a=u(z_0),
\qquad
\lim_{z\to z_0} v(x,y)=b=v(z_0),
$$

so $u$ and $v$ are continuous at $z_0$.

Conversely, if $u$ and $v$ are continuous at $z_0$, then

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} u(x,y)=u(z_0),
\qquad
\lim_{z\to z_0} v(x,y)=v(z_0).
$$

Again using the theorem on real and imaginary parts,

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z)=u(z_0)+iv(z_0)=f(z_0).
$$

Thus $f$ is continuous at $z_0$. Since the argument is pointwise, $f$ is continuous on a set exactly when both $u$ and $v$ are continuous on that set.

While we have successfully used skills from calculus to guide us in taking complex-valued limits, you should be cautioned in using real-variable intuition. For example, the $\operatorname{limit}_{\text {lim }} \lim _{z \rightarrow \infty} e^{-z}$ is not 0 ; in fact, the limit does not exist (Exercise 21).



Often, the limit of $f(z)$ as $z$ approaches $z_{0}$ will equal $f\left(z_{0}\right)$. Functions that satisfy this requirement are said to be continuous.

> [!definition] Continuous Function
> Suppose $f(z)$ is defined on a neighborhood of $z_{0}$. We say that $f(z)$ is continuous at the point $z_{0}$ if $\lim _{z \rightarrow z_{0}} f(z)$ exists and equals $f\left(z_{0}\right)$. We say $f$ is continuous on a set $S$ if it is continuous at every point in $S$.

We see from Example 1 that the functions $f(z)=z$ and $f(z)=c$ are continuous at all points in the plane. To check the continuity of more complicated functions, we can appeal to the properties of limits. Since continuity is defined in terms of limits, many properties of limits extend to continuous functions.


> [!theorem] Theorem 4 Properties of Continuous Functions
> (i) If $f(z)$ and $g(z)$ are continuous at $z_{0}$, and $c_{1}, c_{2}$ are complex constants, then the following functions are continuous at $z_{0}$ :
> 
> $$
> c_{1} f(z)+c_{2} g(z), \quad f(z) g(z), \quad \frac{f(z)}{g(z)}\left(\text { provided } g\left(z_{0}\right) \neq 0\right)
> $$
> 
> (ii) If $g$ is continuous at $z_{0}$ and $f$ is continuous at $g\left(z_{0}\right)$, then the composition $h(z)=f(g(z))$ is continuous at $z_{0}$.
> (iii) If $g(z)$ is real-valued and continuous at $z_{0}$ and $f(x)$ is continuous at $x_{0}=g\left(z_{0}\right)$, then the composition $h(z)=f(g(z))$ is continuous at $z_{0}$.
> (iv) The function $f(z)=u(z)+i v(z)$ is continuous if and only if $u$ and $v$ are continuous.









> [!exercise] Example 5: Polynomial and Rational Functions
> (a) Show that a polynomial $p(z)=a_{n} z^{n}+a_{n-1} z^{n-1}+\cdots+a_{0}$ is continuous at all points in the plane.
> (b) A rational function is a function of the form
> 
> $$
> r(z)=\frac{p(z)}{q(z)}
> $$

##### problem a

A polynomial

$$
p(z)=a_n z^n+a_{n-1}z^{n-1}+\cdots+a_1z+a_0
$$

is built from the functions $z\mapsto z$ and constant functions.

We already know that $z$ and every constant are continuous everywhere. Therefore $z^2=z\cdot z$ is continuous, and by repeating this argument, every power $z^k$ is continuous for $k\ge 0$.

Multiplying by the constant $a_k$ preserves continuity, so each term $a_k z^k$ is continuous. Finally, a finite sum of continuous functions is continuous. Hence $p(z)$ is continuous at every point of $\mathbb{C}$.

##### problem b

A rational function has the form

$$
r(z)=\frac{p(z)}{q(z)},
$$

where $p$ and $q$ are polynomials. By part (a), both $p$ and $q$ are continuous everywhere in the plane.

Now let $z_0$ be a point such that

$$
q(z_0)\ne 0.
$$

Then $p$ and $q$ are continuous at $z_0$, and Theorem 4 for quotients applies. Since the denominator does not vanish at $z_0$, the quotient is continuous there. Thus

$$
r(z)=\frac{p(z)}{q(z)}
$$

is continuous at every point for which $q(z)\ne 0$.

$$
i^2+1=-1+1=0,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\lim_{z\to i}\frac{z-i}{z^2+1}
&=
\lim_{z\to i}\frac{z-i}{(z-i)(z+i)} \\

##### problem 1

A discontinuity at $z_0$ is removable if the limit

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z)
$$

exists as a finite complex number, but the function either is not defined at $z_0$ or is defined there with the wrong value. Removing the discontinuity means redefining the function at that one point so that

$$
f(z_0)=\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z).
$$

A discontinuity is nonremovable if no such redefinition can make the function continuous at the point.

##### problem 2

You can repair a discontinuity at a single point only when the limit of the function at that point already exists. In other words, there must be some complex number $L$ such that

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z)=L.
$$

Then redefining the function by setting

$$
f(z_0)=L
$$

makes the function continuous at $z_0$. If the limit does not exist, then no choice of a single value at $z_0$ can force continuity there.

##### problem 3

The uniqueness property says that if a limit exists, it must be unique. So to prove that a limit does not exist, it is enough to produce two different candidate limits coming from two different approaches to the same point.

If, for example, one can show that along one path

$$
f(z)\to L
$$

and along another path

$$
f(z)\to L'
\qquad \text{with} \qquad
L\ne L',
$$

then the full limit at the point cannot exist, because an actual limit would have to be the same no matter how the point is approached.

##### problem 4

Concretely, you must find two curves $C$ and $C'$ that both approach $z_0$ and then compute the restricted limits of $f$ along those curves. If

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0,\ z\in C} f(z)=L
$$

but

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0,\ z\in C'} f(z)=L'
\qquad \text{and} \qquad
L\ne L',
$$

then

$$
\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z)
$$

does not exist.

Finding two such curves suffices because a genuine complex limit must be independent of path. Once two valid approaches give different answers, the possibility of a single limit is ruled out.

##### problem 5

This method is especially natural in the complex or multivariable setting because there are infinitely many directions and curves along which a point can be approached. Path dependence is therefore a genuine issue.

For a function of one real variable, by contrast, there are only two basic approaches to a point: from the left and from the right. So the analogous test is simply to compare the left-hand and right-hand limits. In complex analysis, the abundance of possible approaches means that checking two carefully chosen curves is a standard way to expose failure of a limit.

##### problem 1

Yes. If $\phi$ is continuous as a one-variable function and

$$
f(x,y)=\phi(x),
$$

then $f$ is continuous as a function of two variables. The content of the claim is that continuity with respect to the single variable $x$ is enough to control the two-variable limit as $(x,y)\to (x_0,y_0)$, even though the point is now being approached in the plane.

To prove it, let $(x_0,y_0)$ be given, with $x_0$ in the interval where $\phi$ is continuous. Since $\phi$ is continuous at $x_0$,

$$
\lim_{x\to x_0}\phi(x)=\phi(x_0).
$$

Now let $(x,y)\to (x_0,y_0)$. Then automatically

$$
x\to x_0.
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
\lim_{(x,y)\to (x_0,y_0)} f(x,y)
&=
\lim_{(x,y)\to (x_0,y_0)} \phi(x) \\
&=
\lim_{x\to x_0} \phi(x) \\
&=
\phi(x_0) \\
&=
f(x_0,y_0).
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence $f$ is continuous at $(x_0,y_0)$. The same argument shows that a function of the form $g(x,y)=\phi(y)$ is continuous whenever $\phi$ is continuous at $y_0$.

##### problem 2

This recognition is useful because it reduces a two-variable continuity problem to simpler one-variable continuity facts together with the closure properties of continuous functions. Once you can write a complicated function as a sum, product, quotient, or composition of building blocks depending only on $x$ or only on $y$, the problem becomes mostly algebraic.

Instead of proving continuity from the two-variable definition each time, you reuse known facts about one-variable functions and then assemble them with general theorems about continuous functions.

##### problem 3

The additional fact you need is Proposition 1: if $\phi$ is continuous in one variable, then $(x,y)\mapsto \phi(x)$ is continuous as a two-variable function, and similarly for $(x,y)\mapsto \phi(y)$.

For example, to handle

$$
e^x\cos y,
$$

you first use Proposition 1 to conclude that

$$
(x,y)\mapsto e^x
\qquad \text{and} \qquad
(x,y)\mapsto \cos y
$$

are continuous. Then Theorem 4 implies that their product is continuous.

##### problem 4

Write

$$
e^z=e^x(\cos y+i\sin y)=e^x\cos y+i\,e^x\sin y.
$$

The real functions $x\mapsto e^x$, $y\mapsto \cos y$, and $y\mapsto \sin y$ are continuous. By Proposition 1, the corresponding two-variable functions

$$
(x,y)\mapsto e^x,\qquad (x,y)\mapsto \cos y,\qquad (x,y)\mapsto \sin y
$$

are continuous. By Theorem 4, products of continuous functions are continuous, so

$$
e^x\cos y
\qquad \text{and} \qquad
e^x\sin y
$$

are continuous. Then scalar multiples and sums of continuous functions are continuous, so

$$
e^x\cos y+i\,e^x\sin y
$$

is continuous. Therefore $e^z$ is continuous as a function of $z$.

##### problem 5

It does not follow automatically because continuity of $\phi$ is initially a statement about a one-dimensional limit,

$$
x\to x_0,
$$

whereas continuity of $(x,y)\mapsto \phi(x)$ is a statement about a two-dimensional limit,

$$
(x,y)\to (x_0,y_0).
$$

Those are different notions, so a bridging result is needed to justify passing from one to the other. The intuition that “nothing depends on $y$” is correct, but it still has to be converted into a proof that whenever $(x,y)\to (x_0,y_0)$, the relevant variable $x$ tends to $x_0$ and hence $\phi(x)\to \phi(x_0)$.

That bridging step is exactly what Proposition 1 supplies.

Many important functions of several variables are made up of products, quotients and linear combinations of functions of a single variable. For example, the function $u(x, y)=e^{x} \cos y$ is the product of two functions of a single variable each; namely, $e^{x}$ and $\cos y$. The exponential function $e^{z}=e^{x}(\cos y+i \sin y)$ is a linear combination of two products of functions of a single variable. In establishing the continuity of such functions, the following simple observations are very useful.

> [!proposition] Proposition 1
> Suppose that $\phi(x)$ is a continuous function of a single variable $x$ over an interval $(a, b)$. Then the function of two variables $f(x, y)=\phi(x)$ is continuous at $\left(x_{0}, y_{0}\right)$ whenever $x_{0}$ is in $(a, b)$. Similarly, $g(x, y)=\phi(y)$ is continuous, at $\left(x_{0}, y_{0}\right)$ whenever $y_{0}$ is in $(a, b)$.

> [!exercise] Exercise 9
> Show that the absolute value $|z|$ is continuous.


**Solution:** Let $z_0\in\mathbb{C}$ be fixed. We show that
$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}|z|=|z_0|.
$$
By the triangle inequality,
$$
\begin{aligned}
|z|
&=|(z-z_0)+z_0|\\
&\le |z-z_0|+|z_0|.
\end{aligned}
$$
Subtracting $|z_0|$ from both sides gives
$$
\begin{aligned}
|z|-|z_0|
&\le |z-z_0|.
\end{aligned}
$$
Also,
$$
\begin{aligned}
|z_0|
&=|(z_0-z)+z|\\
&\le |z_0-z|+|z|\\
&=|z-z_0|+|z|.
\end{aligned}
$$
Subtracting $|z|$ from both sides gives
$$
\begin{aligned}
|z_0|-|z|
&\le |z-z_0|.
\end{aligned}
$$
Therefore,
$$
\begin{aligned}
-|z-z_0|
&\le |z|-|z_0|\\
&\le |z-z_0|.
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence
$$
\begin{aligned}
\bigl||z|-|z_0|\bigr|
&\le |z-z_0|.
\end{aligned}
$$
Now let $\epsilon>0$. If
$$
\begin{aligned}
|z-z_0|
&<\epsilon,
\end{aligned}
$$
then
$$
\begin{aligned}
\bigl||z|-|z_0|\bigr|
&\le |z-z_0|\\
&<\epsilon.
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus, whenever $0<|z-z_0|<\epsilon$, we have $\bigl||z|-|z_0|\bigr|<\epsilon$. So we may take $\delta=\epsilon$, and it follows that
$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}|z|=|z_0|.
$$
Therefore the absolute value function is continuous on $\mathbb{C}$.

# Continuity of the Logarithms



> [!review]
> 1. Where is the principal branch of the logarithm continuous, and where does it fail to be continuous? For each point where continuity fails, is the discontinuity removable or nonremovable? Prove your answer.
> 2. What is a branch cut of a logarithm, and what role does the choice of branch play in determining where it lies?
> 3. How does the continuity behavior of $\log _\alpha z-\mathrm{a}$ logarithm branch other than the principal one - compare to that of the principal branch $\log z$, and why is the continuity structure of the logarithm tied so directly to the continuity structure of the argument function?
> 4. Why is it impossible to define a single branch of the logarithm that is continuous on all of $\mathbb{C} \backslash\{0\}$, and what does the unavoidability of a branch cut reveal about the relationship between the logarithm and the argument?
> 5. Given a point $z_0$ on the branch cut of $\log z$, how would you show concretely that the discontinuity of $\log z$ at $z_0$ cannot be removed - that is, what would you need to exhibit about the behavior of $\log z$ near $z_0$ ?


##### problem 1

The principal branch is
$$
\log z=\ln|z|+i\operatorname{Arg}z,
\qquad
-\pi<\operatorname{Arg}z\le \pi,
\qquad
z\ne 0.
$$
It is continuous at every point of
$$
\mathbb{C}\setminus(-\infty,0].
$$
Indeed, on this set both pieces are continuous: the modulus $|z|$ is continuous, $\ln r$ is continuous for $r>0$, and the principal argument $\operatorname{Arg}z$ is continuous away from the negative real axis. Therefore
$$
\log z=\ln|z|+i\operatorname{Arg}z
$$
is continuous on $\mathbb{C}\setminus(-\infty,0]$.

Now let $z_0=-r$ with $r>0$. Then
$$
\log z_0=\ln r+i\pi,
$$
since $\operatorname{Arg}(-r)=\pi$. To test continuity at $z_0$, approach $z_0$ from above and below:
$$
z_t^+=-r+it,
\qquad
z_t^-=-r-it,
\qquad
t>0.
$$
Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
|z_t^\pm|
&=\sqrt{r^2+t^2}
\longrightarrow r,
\end{aligned}
$$
so
$$
\begin{aligned}
\ln|z_t^\pm|
&=\ln\!\bigl(\sqrt{r^2+t^2}\bigr)
\longrightarrow \ln r.
\end{aligned}
$$
Also,
$$
\operatorname{Arg}(z_t^+)\longrightarrow \pi,
\qquad
\operatorname{Arg}(z_t^-)\longrightarrow -\pi.
$$
Hence
$$
\begin{aligned}
\log z_t^+
&=\ln|z_t^+|+i\operatorname{Arg}(z_t^+)\\
&\longrightarrow \ln r+i\pi,
\end{aligned}
$$
while
$$
\begin{aligned}
\log z_t^-
&=\ln|z_t^-|+i\operatorname{Arg}(z_t^-)\\
&\longrightarrow \ln r-i\pi.
\end{aligned}
$$
These limits are different, so
$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}\log z
$$
does not exist. Therefore the principal branch fails to be continuous at every point $z_0<0$ on the branch cut. This discontinuity is nonremovable, because if a removable discontinuity existed at $z_0$, then the limit would have to exist.

At $z=0$, the principal logarithm is not defined. Moreover,
$$
\Re(\log z)=\ln|z|,
$$
and as $z\to 0$,
$$
\ln|z|\to -\infty.
$$
So $\log z$ cannot have a finite limit at $0$, and there is no way to define $\log 0$ so as to make $\log z$ continuous there. Thus the failure at $0$ is also nonremovable.

##### problem 2

A branch cut is the set removed from the punctured plane so that one may choose a single-valued continuous branch of the argument, and hence a single-valued continuous branch of the logarithm. Since
$$
\log_\alpha z=\ln|z|+i\operatorname{Arg}_\alpha z,
$$
the logarithm becomes continuous exactly where the chosen branch of the argument is continuous.

If one chooses the argument range
$$
\alpha<\operatorname{Arg}_\alpha z\le \alpha+2\pi,
$$
then the jump occurs across the ray
$$
\{re^{i\alpha}:r\ge 0\}.
$$
That ray is the branch cut for the branch $\log_\alpha z$. Different choices of the branch of the argument shift the location of the cut, but they do not remove the need for a cut.

##### problem 3

Any other logarithm branch has the same continuity pattern as the principal branch, except that the cut is rotated. If
$$
\log_\alpha z=\ln|z|+i\operatorname{Arg}_\alpha z,
\qquad
\alpha<\operatorname{Arg}_\alpha z\le \alpha+2\pi,
$$
then $\log_\alpha z$ is continuous on
$$
\mathbb{C}\setminus\{re^{i\alpha}:r\ge 0\},
$$
and it is discontinuous on that ray. At points of the ray with $r>0$, the discontinuity is nonremovable for the same reason as in the principal case: approaching the ray from the two sides produces imaginary parts differing by $2\pi$.

The reason this mirrors the behavior of the argument function is immediate from the formula
$$
\log_\alpha z=\ln|z|+i\operatorname{Arg}_\alpha z.
$$
The term $\ln|z|$ is continuous wherever $z\ne 0$, because $|z|$ is continuous and $\ln r$ is continuous for $r>0$. Therefore all discontinuity comes from $\operatorname{Arg}_\alpha z$. In other words, the continuity structure of the logarithm is tied directly to the continuity structure of the argument because the logarithm is obtained by adding the continuous real part $\ln|z|$ to the chosen branch of the argument multiplied by $i$.

##### problem 4

It is impossible to define a single branch of the logarithm that is continuous on all of $\mathbb{C}\setminus\{0\}$ because that would amount to defining a continuous single-valued argument on all of $\mathbb{C}\setminus\{0\}$, and such a function cannot exist.

To see this concretely, suppose a continuous branch $L$ of the logarithm existed on all of $\mathbb{C}\setminus\{0\}$. Restrict $L$ to the unit circle:
$$
z=e^{it},
\qquad
0\le t\le 2\pi.
$$
Since $|e^{it}|=1$, we would have
$$
\begin{aligned}
L(e^{it})
&=\ln|e^{it}|+i\theta(t)\\
&=\ln 1+i\theta(t)\\
&=0+i\theta(t)\\
&=i\theta(t),
\end{aligned}
$$
where $\theta(t)$ is a continuous choice of argument for $e^{it}$. Because $e^{i\theta(t)}=e^{it}$, we have
$$
\theta(t)-t\in 2\pi\mathbb{Z}.
$$
The function $\theta(t)-t$ is continuous and takes values in the discrete set $2\pi\mathbb{Z}$, so it must be constant. Hence
$$
\theta(t)=t+2k\pi
$$
for some fixed integer $k$.

Now compare $t=0$ and $t=2\pi$. Since
$$
e^{i0}=e^{i2\pi}=1,
$$
single-valuedness of $L$ gives
$$
L(1)=L(e^{i0})=L(e^{i2\pi}).
$$
But the formula above gives
$$
\begin{aligned}
L(e^{i0})
&=i\theta(0)
=i(0+2k\pi),\\
L(e^{i2\pi})
&=i\theta(2\pi)
=i(2\pi+2k\pi)
=i\theta(0)+2\pi i.
\end{aligned}
$$
These are not equal. This contradiction shows that no continuous single-valued branch of the logarithm can exist on all of $\mathbb{C}\setminus\{0\}$.

Thus a branch cut is unavoidable. It reveals that the logarithm cannot be separated from the multivalued nature of the argument: whenever one goes once around the origin, the argument changes by $2\pi$, so any logarithm branch must jump somewhere.

##### problem 5

To show concretely that the discontinuity at a point of the branch cut is nonremovable, one must exhibit two approaches to the same point along which $\log z$ tends to two different values.

Let
$$
z_0=-r,
\qquad
r>0.
$$
Approach $z_0$ from above and below by
$$
z_n^+=-r+\frac{i}{n},
\qquad
z_n^-=-r-\frac{i}{n},
\qquad
n=1,2,3,\dots.
$$
Then
$$
z_n^+\to z_0,
\qquad
z_n^-\to z_0.
$$
Now compute the corresponding logarithms. First,
$$
\begin{aligned}
|z_n^\pm|
&=\sqrt{r^2+\frac{1}{n^2}}
\longrightarrow r,
\end{aligned}
$$
so
$$
\begin{aligned}
\ln|z_n^\pm|
&=\ln\!\left(\sqrt{r^2+\frac{1}{n^2}}\right)
\longrightarrow \ln r.
\end{aligned}
$$
Also,
$$
\operatorname{Arg}(z_n^+)\to \pi,
\qquad
\operatorname{Arg}(z_n^-)\to -\pi.
$$
Therefore
$$
\begin{aligned}
\log z_n^+
&=\ln|z_n^+|+i\operatorname{Arg}(z_n^+)\\
&\longrightarrow \ln r+i\pi,
\end{aligned}
$$
while
$$
\begin{aligned}
\log z_n^-
&=\ln|z_n^-|+i\operatorname{Arg}(z_n^-)\\
&\longrightarrow \ln r-i\pi.
\end{aligned}
$$
Since
$$
\ln r+i\pi\ne \ln r-i\pi,
$$
the limit of $\log z$ as $z\to z_0$ does not exist. Therefore no redefinition of the value at $z_0$ can make $\log z$ continuous there. That is exactly why the discontinuity is nonremovable.

At $z_0=0$, the failure is even stronger: one can exhibit that
$$
\Re(\log z)=\ln|z|\to -\infty
$$
as $z\to 0$, so there is not even a finite candidate value to assign at the origin.

We show that

=

z+i\to 2i,

|z+i|\to |2i|=2.

\end{aligned}

##### problem 18

We show that
$$
\lim_{z\to\infty}\frac{\log z}{z}=0.
$$
Using the principal logarithm,
$$
\log z=\ln|z|+i\operatorname{Arg}z,
$$
with
$$
|\operatorname{Arg}z|\le \pi.
$$
Hence
$$
\begin{aligned}
\left|\frac{\log z}{z}\right|
&=
\frac{|\log z|}{|z|}\\
&=
\frac{|\ln|z|+i\operatorname{Arg}z|}{|z|}\\
&\le
\frac{|\ln|z||+|\operatorname{Arg}z|}{|z|}\\
&\le
\frac{|\ln|z||+\pi}{|z|}.
\end{aligned}
$$
For $|z|>1$, we have $\ln|z|>0$, so
$$
|\ln|z||=\ln|z|.
$$
Therefore, for $|z|>1$,
$$
\begin{aligned}
\left|\frac{\log z}{z}\right|
&\le
\frac{\ln|z|}{|z|}+\frac{\pi}{|z|}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Now as $z\to\infty$, we have $|z|\to\infty$, so by the corresponding real-variable limits,
$$
\frac{\ln|z|}{|z|}\to 0,
\qquad
\frac{\pi}{|z|}\to 0.
$$
Hence
$$
\left|\frac{\log z}{z}\right|\to 0,
$$
and therefore
$$
\lim_{z\to\infty}\frac{\log z}{z}=0.
$$

##### problem 27

We prove that a limit, if it exists, is unique.

First suppose
$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)=L_1
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)=L_2,
$$
where $L_1$ and $L_2$ are finite complex numbers. Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
|L_1-L_2|
&=
|L_1-f(z)+f(z)-L_2|\\
&\le
|L_1-f(z)|+|f(z)-L_2|\\
&=
|L_1-f(z)|+|L_2-f(z)|.
\end{aligned}
$$
Now let $z\to z_0$. Since
$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)=L_1,
$$
we have
$$
|L_1-f(z)|\to 0.
$$
Since
$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)=L_2,
$$
we have
$$
|L_2-f(z)|\to 0.
$$
Therefore
$$
\begin{aligned}
|L_1-L_2|
&\le
|L_1-f(z)|+|L_2-f(z)|\\
&\to
0+0\\
&=
0.
\end{aligned}
$$
But $|L_1-L_2|$ is a constant, so the only way it can be less than or equal to quantities tending to $0$ is for
$$
|L_1-L_2|=0.
$$
Hence
$$
L_1-L_2=0,
$$
and so
$$
L_1=L_2.
$$
Thus a finite limit is unique.

Now suppose the limit is $\infty$. If
$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)=\infty
$$
and also
$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)=L
$$
for some finite complex number $L$, then choose $M=|L|+1$. Since $f(z)\to\infty$, there exists $\delta_1>0$ such that
$$
0<|z-z_0|<\delta_1
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
|f(z)|> |L|+1.
$$
Since $f(z)\to L$, there exists $\delta_2>0$ such that
$$
0<|z-z_0|<\delta_2
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
|f(z)-L|<1.
$$
For such $z$, the triangle inequality gives
$$
\begin{aligned}
|f(z)|
&=
|f(z)-L+L|\\
&\le
|f(z)-L|+|L|\\
&<
1+|L|.
\end{aligned}
$$
This contradicts
$$
|f(z)|>|L|+1.
$$
Therefore a function cannot have both a finite limit and the limit $\infty$ at the same point.

If we had
$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)=\infty
$$
and
$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)=\infty,
$$
these are the same limit. So the infinite limit is also unique.

Hence, whenever the limit of $f$ exists, it is unique.

\left|f\!\left(\frac{1}{z}\right)-L\right|<\epsilon.
$$
So
$$
\lim_{z\to 0}f\!\left(\frac{1}{z}\right)=L.
$$

Conversely, assume that
$$
\lim_{z\to 0}f\!\left(\frac{1}{z}\right)=L.
$$
Let $\epsilon>0$ be given. Then there exists $\delta>0$ such that
$$
0<|z|<\delta
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\left|f\!\left(\frac{1}{z}\right)-L\right|<\epsilon.
$$
Now let $w$ satisfy
$$
|w|>\frac{1}{\delta}.
$$
Set
$$
z=\frac{1}{w}.
$$
Then
$$
0<|z|=\left|\frac{1}{w}\right|=\frac{1}{|w|}<\delta.
$$
Therefore
$$
\left|f(w)-L\right|
=
\left|f\!\left(\frac{1}{z}\right)-L\right|
<\epsilon.
$$
Thus, if we choose
$$
R=\frac{1}{\delta},
$$
then
$$
|w|>R
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
|f(w)-L|<\epsilon.
$$
Hence
$$
\lim_{w\to\infty}f(w)=L.
$$
Therefore
$$
\lim_{z\to\infty}f(z)=L
\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad
\lim_{z\to 0}f\!\left(\frac{1}{z}\right)=L.
$$

##### problem 30

We prove that
$$
\lim_{z\to\infty}f(z)=\infty
\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad
\lim_{z\to 0}f\!\left(\frac{1}{z}\right)=\infty.
$$

First assume that
$$
\lim_{w\to\infty}f(w)=\infty.
$$
Let $M>0$ be given. Then there exists $R>0$ such that
$$
|w|>R
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
|f(w)|>M.
$$
Now if
$$
0<|z|<\frac{1}{R},
$$
then
$$
\left|\frac{1}{z}\right|=\frac{1}{|z|}>R.
$$
Hence
$$
\left|f\!\left(\frac{1}{z}\right)\right|>M.
$$
Therefore
$$
\lim_{z\to 0}f\!\left(\frac{1}{z}\right)=\infty.
$$

Conversely, assume that
$$
\lim_{z\to 0}f\!\left(\frac{1}{z}\right)=\infty.
$$
Let $M>0$ be given. Then there exists $\delta>0$ such that
$$
0<|z|<\delta
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\left|f\!\left(\frac{1}{z}\right)\right|>M.
$$
Now let $w$ satisfy
$$
|w|>\frac{1}{\delta},
$$
and set
$$
z=\frac{1}{w}.
$$
Then
$$
0<|z|=\frac{1}{|w|}<\delta.
$$
So
$$
|f(w)|
=
\left|f\!\left(\frac{1}{z}\right)\right|
>M.
$$
Hence
$$
\lim_{w\to\infty}f(w)=\infty.

Therefore

\lim_{z\to\infty}f(z)=\infty
\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad

##### problem 31

We prove that
$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)=0
\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad
\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{1}{f(z)}=\infty.
$$

First assume that
$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)=0.
$$
Let $M>0$ be given. Choose
$$
\epsilon=\frac{1}{M}.
$$
Since $f(z)\to 0$, there exists $\delta>0$ such that
$$
0<|z-z_0|<\delta
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
|f(z)|<\frac{1}{M}.
$$
For such $z$, we must have $f(z)\ne 0$, and therefore
$$
\begin{aligned}
\left|\frac{1}{f(z)}\right|
&=
\frac{1}{|f(z)|}\\
&>
\frac{1}{1/M}\\
&=
M.
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence
$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{1}{f(z)}=\infty.
$$

Conversely, assume that
$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{1}{f(z)}=\infty.
$$
Let $\epsilon>0$ be given, and choose
$$
M=\frac{1}{\epsilon}.
$$
Then there exists $\delta>0$ such that
$$
0<|z-z_0|<\delta
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\left|\frac{1}{f(z)}\right|>\frac{1}{\epsilon}.
$$
For such $z$, we have $f(z)\ne 0$, so
$$
\begin{aligned}
\left|\frac{1}{f(z)}\right|>\frac{1}{\epsilon}
&\Longrightarrow
\frac{1}{|f(z)|}>\frac{1}{\epsilon}\\
&\Longrightarrow
|f(z)|<\epsilon.
\end{aligned}
$$
Therefore
$$
0<|z-z_0|<\delta
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
|f(z)-0|=|f(z)|<\epsilon,
$$
and hence
$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)=0.
$$
So
$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)=0
\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad
\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{1}{f(z)}=\infty.
$$

From Exercise 10, problem 3, we already know that

\lim_{z\to 0}\frac{z}{\cos z}=0.

=

Therefore

> [!exercise] Exercise 16
> In problems 33-40, determine the set of points where the given function is continuous. For a point of discontinuity, determine whether it is removable or not. Whenever possible, use properties from this section; in particular, use Proposition 1 and Theorem 4.
> 33. $\frac{z-i}{z+1+3 i}$.
> 34. $\frac{2 z+1}{z^{2}+3 z+2}$.
> 35. $\bar{z}$.
> 36. $\log (z+1)$.
> 37. $\sin z$.
> 38. $(\operatorname{Arg} z)^{2}$.
> 39. $z(\operatorname{Arg} z)^{2}$.

##### problem 33

Consider
$$
f(z)=\frac{z-i}{z+1+3i}.
$$
The numerator and denominator are polynomials, hence continuous everywhere. Therefore the quotient is continuous at every point where the denominator is nonzero. The denominator vanishes when
$$
z+1+3i=0,
$$
that is, when
$$
z=-1-3i.
$$
So $f$ is continuous on
$$
\mathbb{C}\setminus\{-1-3i\}.
$$
At $z=-1-3i$, the discontinuity is not removable, because the numerator there is
$$
(-1-3i)-i=-1-4i\ne 0.
$$
Thus no cancellation occurs, and the denominator really vanishes. Hence the discontinuity at $z=-1-3i$ is nonremovable.

##### problem 34

Now consider
$$
f(z)=\frac{2z+1}{z^2+3z+2}.
$$
Factor the denominator:
$$
\begin{aligned}
z^2+3z+2
&=(z+1)(z+2).
\end{aligned}
$$
Therefore
$$
f(z)=\frac{2z+1}{(z+1)(z+2)}.
$$
Again this is a quotient of continuous functions, so it is continuous wherever the denominator is nonzero. Hence $f$ is continuous on
$$
\mathbb{C}\setminus\{-1,-2\}.
$$
At $z=-1$, the numerator is
$$
2(-1)+1=-1\ne 0,
$$
and at $z=-2$, the numerator is
$$
2(-2)+1=-3\ne 0.
$$
So there is no cancellation at either zero of the denominator. Therefore both discontinuities are nonremovable.

##### problem 35

Let
$$
f(z)=\bar z.
$$
If
$$
z=x+iy,
$$
then
$$
\bar z=x-iy.
$$
Thus
$$
u(x,y)=x,
\qquad
v(x,y)=-y.
$$
Both $u$ and $v$ are continuous real-valued functions of $(x,y)$. Hence, by Proposition 1, $\bar z$ is continuous everywhere in $\mathbb{C}$.

##### problem 36

Consider
$$
f(z)=\log(z+1),
$$
where $\log$ denotes the principal branch. The principal logarithm is continuous on
$$
\mathbb{C}\setminus(-\infty,0].
$$
Therefore $\log(z+1)$ is continuous exactly when
$$
z+1\notin(-\infty,0].
$$
This means
$$
z\notin(-\infty,-1].
$$
So $f$ is continuous on
$$
\mathbb{C}\setminus(-\infty,-1].
$$

At points $z<-1$, the value $z+1$ lies on the branch cut of the logarithm, so the discontinuity is nonremovable for the same reason as for $\log z$: approaching from above and below gives imaginary parts tending to $\pi$ and $-\pi$.

At $z=-1$, we have
$$
z+1=0,
$$
and $\log 0$ is not defined. Moreover, as $z\to -1$,
$$
\Re(\log(z+1))=\ln|z+1|\to -\infty,
$$
so the discontinuity at $z=-1$ is also nonremovable.

##### problem 37

The sine function is continuous everywhere in $\mathbb{C}$. One way to see this is to use
$$
\sin z=\frac{e^{iz}-e^{-iz}}{2i}.
$$
The functions $iz$ and $-iz$ are continuous, the exponential function is continuous, differences of continuous functions are continuous, and division by the nonzero constant $2i$ preserves continuity. Hence $\sin z$ is continuous for all
$$
z\in\mathbb{C}.
$$
So there are no points of discontinuity.

##### problem 38

Consider
$$
f(z)=(\operatorname{Arg} z)^2,
$$
with $\operatorname{Arg} z$ the principal argument. Away from the negative real axis and away from $0$, $\operatorname{Arg} z$ is continuous, so $(\operatorname{Arg} z)^2$ is continuous there as a composition of continuous functions.

Now let $z_0<0$. Write
$$
z_0=-r,
\qquad
r>0.
$$
Approach $z_0$ from above and below:
$$
z_t^+=-r+it,
\qquad
z_t^-=-r-it,
\qquad
t>0.
$$
Then
$$
\operatorname{Arg}(z_t^+)\to \pi,
\qquad
\operatorname{Arg}(z_t^-)\to -\pi.
$$
Therefore
$$
(\operatorname{Arg}(z_t^+))^2\to \pi^2,
\qquad
(\operatorname{Arg}(z_t^-))^2\to \pi^2.
$$
Also, since $\operatorname{Arg}(z_0)=\pi$, we have
$$
f(z_0)=\pi^2.
$$
Hence $f$ is continuous at every negative real number $z_0<0$.

At $z=0$, the function is not defined. Moreover, the limit does not exist there. Indeed, along the positive real axis,
$$
\operatorname{Arg} z=0,
$$
so
$$
(\operatorname{Arg} z)^2=0.
$$
Along the negative real axis,
$$
\operatorname{Arg} z=\pi,
$$
so
$$
(\operatorname{Arg} z)^2=\pi^2.
$$
Since these two limiting values are different, the discontinuity at $0$ is nonremovable.

Therefore $(\operatorname{Arg} z)^2$ is continuous on
$$
\mathbb{C}\setminus\{0\},
$$
and it has a nonremovable discontinuity at $0$.

##### problem 39

Now consider
$$
f(z)=z(\operatorname{Arg} z)^2.
$$
For $z\ne 0$, the factor $z$ is continuous and, by problem 38, $(\operatorname{Arg} z)^2$ is continuous. Hence $f$ is continuous on
$$
\mathbb{C}\setminus\{0\}.
$$

At $z=0$, the function is not defined, but the discontinuity is removable. Indeed,
$$
\begin{aligned}
\left|z(\operatorname{Arg} z)^2\right|
&=
|z|\,|(\operatorname{Arg} z)^2|\\
&=
|z|\,(\operatorname{Arg} z)^2\\
&\le
|z|\pi^2,
\end{aligned}
$$
because
$$
|\operatorname{Arg} z|\le \pi
$$
for all $z\ne 0$. As $z\to 0$,
$$
\pi^2|z|\to 0.
$$
Hence, by the squeeze theorem,
$$
\lim_{z\to 0}z(\operatorname{Arg} z)^2=0.
$$
So the discontinuity at $0$ is removable. If we define
$$
f(0)=0,
$$
then the resulting function is continuous on all of $\mathbb{C}$.

##### problem 40

Finally, consider
$$
f(z)=\frac{z}{|z|}.
$$
For $z\ne 0$, both $z$ and $|z|$ are continuous, and the denominator is nonzero. Therefore $f$ is continuous on
$$
\mathbb{C}\setminus\{0\}.
$$

At $z=0$, the function is not defined. The discontinuity is not removable, because the limit does not exist. Along the positive real axis,
$$
z=x,
\qquad
x\to 0^+,
$$
we have
$$
\frac{z}{|z|}
=
\frac{x}{x}
=
1.
$$
Along the positive imaginary axis,
$$
z=iy,
\qquad
y\to 0^+,
$$
we have
$$
\frac{z}{|z|}
=
\frac{iy}{y}
=
i.
$$
Since
$$
1\ne i,
$$
the limit as $z\to 0$ does not exist. Therefore the discontinuity at $0$ is nonremovable.



> [!exercise] Exercise 17
> 41. Pre-image of sets. Let $f$ be a complex-valued function defined on a subset $S$ of $\mathbb{C}$. If $A$ is a set of complex numbers, the pre-image or inverse image of $A$ by $f$ is the set $f^{-1}[A]=\{z \in S: f(z)$ belongs to $A\}$. The following statements are true for arbitrary sets $S$. To simplify the proofs, take $S=\mathbb{C}$.
> (a) Show that $f$ is continuous if and only if $f^{-1}[A]$ is open whenever $A$ is open.
> (b) Show that $f$ is continuous if and only if $f^{-1}[A]$ is closed whenever $A$ is closed. (If $S$ is a proper subset of $\mathbb{C}$, (a) and (b) still hold, but we have to define what we mean for a set such as $f^{-1}[A]$ to be open or closed in $S$. These topics are part of elementary topology and will not be emphasized in this book.)



##### problem 41(a)

We prove that $f$ is continuous if and only if $f^{-1}[A]$ is open whenever $A$ is open.

First assume that $f$ is continuous on $\mathbb{C}$, and let $A$ be an open set in $\mathbb{C}$. We must show that
$$
f^{-1}[A]=\{z\in\mathbb{C}:f(z)\in A\}
$$
is open.

Let
$$
z_0\in f^{-1}[A].
$$
Then
$$
f(z_0)\in A.
$$
Since $A$ is open, there exists $r>0$ such that
$$
B(f(z_0),r)\subset A.
$$
Because $f$ is continuous at $z_0$, for this $r$ there exists $\delta>0$ such that
$$
|z-z_0|<\delta
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
|f(z)-f(z_0)|<r.
$$
But
$$
|f(z)-f(z_0)|<r
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
f(z)\in B(f(z_0),r)\subset A.
$$
Hence
$$
|z-z_0|<\delta
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
z\in f^{-1}[A].
$$
So
$$
B(z_0,\delta)\subset f^{-1}[A].
$$
This shows that every point of $f^{-1}[A]$ is an interior point. Therefore $f^{-1}[A]$ is open.

Conversely, assume that $f^{-1}[A]$ is open whenever $A$ is open. We show that $f$ is continuous at every $z_0\in\mathbb{C}$.

Let $z_0\in\mathbb{C}$, and let $\epsilon>0$ be given. The open disk
$$
B(f(z_0),\epsilon)
$$
is open in $\mathbb{C}$, so by hypothesis
$$
f^{-1}[B(f(z_0),\epsilon)]
$$
is open. Also,
$$
z_0\in f^{-1}[B(f(z_0),\epsilon)],
$$
because
$$
f(z_0)\in B(f(z_0),\epsilon).
$$
Since the pre-image is open, there exists $\delta>0$ such that
$$
B(z_0,\delta)\subset f^{-1}[B(f(z_0),\epsilon)].
$$
Therefore,
$$
|z-z_0|<\delta
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
z\in f^{-1}[B(f(z_0),\epsilon)]
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
f(z)\in B(f(z_0),\epsilon).
$$
That is,
$$
|z-z_0|<\delta
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
|f(z)-f(z_0)|<\epsilon.
$$
So $f$ is continuous at $z_0$. Since $z_0$ was arbitrary, $f$ is continuous on $\mathbb{C}$.

Hence
$$
f\text{ is continuous}
\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad
f^{-1}[A]\text{ is open whenever }A\text{ is open}.
$$

##### problem 41(b)

We now prove that $f$ is continuous if and only if $f^{-1}[A]$ is closed whenever $A$ is closed.

Assume first that $f$ is continuous, and let $A$ be closed. Then
$$
\mathbb{C}\setminus A
$$
is open. By part (a),
$$
f^{-1}[\mathbb{C}\setminus A]
$$
is open. But pre-images commute with complements:
$$
\begin{aligned}
f^{-1}[\mathbb{C}\setminus A]
&=
\{z\in\mathbb{C}:f(z)\in \mathbb{C}\setminus A\}\\
&=
\{z\in\mathbb{C}:f(z)\notin A\}\\
&=
\mathbb{C}\setminus \{z\in\mathbb{C}:f(z)\in A\}\\
&=
\mathbb{C}\setminus f^{-1}[A].
\end{aligned}
$$
So
$$
\mathbb{C}\setminus f^{-1}[A]
$$
is open, which means that
$$
f^{-1}[A]
$$
is closed.

Conversely, assume that $f^{-1}[A]$ is closed whenever $A$ is closed. Let $U$ be any open set. Then
$$
\mathbb{C}\setminus U
$$
is closed, so by hypothesis
$$
f^{-1}[\mathbb{C}\setminus U]
$$
is closed. Again using complements,
$$
\begin{aligned}
f^{-1}[\mathbb{C}\setminus U]
&=
\mathbb{C}\setminus f^{-1}[U].
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus
$$
\mathbb{C}\setminus f^{-1}[U]
$$
is closed, which means that
$$
f^{-1}[U]
$$
is open.

By part (a), this implies that $f$ is continuous.

Hence
$$
f\text{ is continuous}
\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad
f^{-1}[A]\text{ is closed whenever }A\text{ is closed}.
$$


> [!exercise] Exercise 18
> 42. Continuity and boundedness. Show that if $f$ is continuous at $z_{0}$, then it is bounded in a neighborhood of $z_{0}$.
> 43. Show that a discontinuity at $z=z_{0}$ of a function $f(z)=u(z)+i v(z)$ is it is bounded in a neighborhood of $z_{0}$. removable if and only if $z_{0}$ is a removable discontinuity of both $u$ and $v$.


##### problem 42

Assume that $f$ is continuous at $z_0$. We show that $f$ is bounded in some neighborhood of $z_0$.

Since $f$ is continuous at $z_0$, for $\epsilon=1$ there exists $\delta>0$ such that
$$
|z-z_0|<\delta
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
|f(z)-f(z_0)|<1.
$$
Now let $z$ satisfy
$$
|z-z_0|<\delta.
$$
Then, by the triangle inequality,
$$
\begin{aligned}
|f(z)|
&=
|f(z)-f(z_0)+f(z_0)|\\
&\le
|f(z)-f(z_0)|+|f(z_0)|\\
&<
1+|f(z_0)|.
\end{aligned}
$$
Therefore, for every $z$ in the neighborhood
$$
B(z_0,\delta)=\{z:|z-z_0|<\delta\},
$$
we have
$$
|f(z)|<1+|f(z_0)|.
$$
So $f$ is bounded in a neighborhood of $z_0$.

##### problem 43

We show that a discontinuity of
$$
f(z)=u(z)+iv(z)
$$
at $z=z_0$ is removable if and only if $z_0$ is a removable discontinuity of both $u$ and $v$.

First assume that $z_0$ is a removable discontinuity of $f$. Then there exists a complex number
$$
w=a+ib
$$
such that
$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)=a+ib.
$$
Since
$$
f(z)=u(z)+iv(z),
$$
Proposition 1 gives
$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}u(z)=a
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\lim_{z\to z_0}v(z)=b.
$$
Therefore the real-valued functions $u$ and $v$ each have finite limits at $z_0$. Hence each can be made continuous at $z_0$ by defining
$$
u(z_0)=a,
\qquad
v(z_0)=b.
$$
So $z_0$ is a removable discontinuity of both $u$ and $v$.

Conversely, assume that $z_0$ is a removable discontinuity of both $u$ and $v$. Then there exist real numbers $a$ and $b$ such that
$$
\lim_{z\to z_0}u(z)=a
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\lim_{z\to z_0}v(z)=b.
$$
Now
$$
f(z)=u(z)+iv(z),
$$
so by the limit laws,
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)
&=
\lim_{z\to z_0}\bigl(u(z)+iv(z)\bigr)\\
&=
\lim_{z\to z_0}u(z)+i\lim_{z\to z_0}v(z)\\
&=
a+ib.
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus $f$ has a finite limit at $z_0$. Therefore the discontinuity of $f$ at $z_0$ is removable, since we can define
$$
f(z_0)=a+ib
$$
and make $f$ continuous there.

Hence a discontinuity of $f=u+iv$ at $z_0$ is removable if and only if the discontinuities of $u$ and $v$ at $z_0$ are both removable.
