> 1.) How is the definite integral $\int_a^b f(x) d x$ of a continuous real-valued function on $[a, b]$ defined as a limit of Riemann sums, and what role does the norm $|P|$ of the partition play in this definition?
> 2.) How does the fundamental theorem of calculus evaluate $\int_a^b f(x) d x$ in terms of an antiderivative of $f$ ?


##### solution

##### problem 1

Let

$$
P:\quad a=x_0<x_1<\cdots<x_m=b
$$

be a partition of $[a,b]$, and in each subinterval $[x_{k-1},x_k]$ choose a sample point $x_k^\star$. The corresponding Riemann sum is

$$
\sum_{k=1}^m f(x_k^\star)(x_k-x_{k-1}).
$$

If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$, then these sums approach a limit as the partition is refined, and the definite integral is defined by

$$
\int_a^b f(x)\,dx
=
\lim_{|P|\to 0}\sum_{k=1}^m f(x_k^\star)(x_k-x_{k-1}).
$$

Here

$$
|P|=\max_{1\le k\le m}(x_k-x_{k-1})
$$

is the norm of the partition. Its role is to measure how fine the partition is. The condition $|P|\to 0$ means that the largest subinterval length tends to $0$, so every subinterval becomes small. The definition requires that no matter how the sample points $x_k^\star$ are chosen, the Riemann sums converge to the same number when the partition norm tends to $0$.

##### problem 2

If $F$ is an antiderivative of $f$ on $[a,b]$, so that

$$
F'(x)=f(x),
$$

then the fundamental theorem of calculus gives

$$
\int_a^b f(x)\,dx=F(b)-F(a).
$$

Thus the definite integral is evaluated by finding any antiderivative $F$ of $f$ and subtracting its value at the lower endpoint from its value at the upper endpoint.

# 3.2.1 Riemann Integral of Complex-Valued Functions



> [!review]
> 1.) How is the definite integral of a piecewise continuous complex-valued function $f(x)= u(x)+i v(x)$ on $[a, b]$ defined in terms of the real integrals of $u$ and $v$, and what motivates this definition from the Riemann-sum construction?
> 2.) What linearity and additivity properties does the Riemann integral of a piecewise continuous complex-valued function on $[a, b]$ inherit from the real-valued case? Prove your answer.


##### solution

##### problem 1

Let

$$
f(x)=u(x)+iv(x),
$$

where $u$ and $v$ are real-valued. If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$, then for a partition

$$
P:\quad a=x_0<x_1<\cdots<x_m=b
$$

with sample points $x_k^\star\in [x_{k-1},x_k]$, the Riemann sum for $f$ is

$$
\sum_{k=1}^m f(x_k^\star)(x_k-x_{k-1}).
$$

Substituting $f(x)=u(x)+iv(x)$ gives

$$
\begin{aligned}
\sum_{k=1}^m f(x_k^\star)(x_k-x_{k-1})
&=\sum_{k=1}^m \bigl(u(x_k^\star)+iv(x_k^\star)\bigr)(x_k-x_{k-1}) \\
&=\sum_{k=1}^m u(x_k^\star)(x_k-x_{k-1})
+i\sum_{k=1}^m v(x_k^\star)(x_k-x_{k-1}).
\end{aligned}
$$

As the partition is refined, the first sum converges to $\int_a^b u(x)\,dx$ and the second converges to $\int_a^b v(x)\,dx$. This motivates the definition

$$
\int_a^b f(x)\,dx
=
\int_a^b u(x)\,dx+i\int_a^b v(x)\,dx.
$$

If $f$ is only piecewise continuous on $[a,b]$, we divide $[a,b]$ into finitely many adjacent subintervals on which $f$ is continuous and define the integral by summing the integrals over those subintervals. Thus the complex integral is defined by integrating the real and imaginary parts separately, exactly as suggested by the Riemann-sum decomposition.

##### problem 2

The integral inherits the same linearity and additivity properties as the real-valued Riemann integral.

If $\alpha,\beta\in \mathbb{C}$ and

$$
f(x)=u(x)+iv(x),\qquad g(x)=p(x)+iq(x),
$$

then

$$
\int_a^b \bigl(\alpha f(x)+\beta g(x)\bigr)\,dx
=
\alpha\int_a^b f(x)\,dx+\beta\int_a^b g(x)\,dx.
$$

To prove this, write

$$
\int_a^b f(x)\,dx=\int_a^b u(x)\,dx+i\int_a^b v(x)\,dx,
$$

and similarly for $g$. Since complex constants distribute over Riemann sums, the real and imaginary parts of $\alpha f+\beta g$ are linear combinations of the real-valued functions $u,v,p,q$. Applying the real-valued linearity property to those component integrals gives the stated formula.

The integral is also additive over adjacent intervals: if $a\le b\le c$ and $f$ is piecewise continuous on $[a,c]$, then

$$
\int_a^c f(x)\,dx
=
\int_a^b f(x)\,dx+\int_b^c f(x)\,dx.
$$

Indeed, if $f(x)=u(x)+iv(x)$, then

$$
\int_a^c f(x)\,dx
=
\int_a^c u(x)\,dx+i\int_a^c v(x)\,dx.
$$

By additivity of the real-valued integral,

$$
\int_a^c u(x)\,dx=\int_a^b u(x)\,dx+\int_b^c u(x)\,dx
$$

and

$$
\int_a^c v(x)\,dx=\int_a^b v(x)\,dx+\int_b^c v(x)\,dx.
$$

Substituting these into the complex integral gives

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_a^c f(x)\,dx
&=\left(\int_a^b u(x)\,dx+\int_b^c u(x)\,dx\right)
+i\left(\int_a^b v(x)\,dx+\int_b^c v(x)\,dx\right) \\
&=\left(\int_a^b u(x)\,dx+i\int_a^b v(x)\,dx\right)
+\left(\int_b^c u(x)\,dx+i\int_b^c v(x)\,dx\right) \\
&=\int_a^b f(x)\,dx+\int_b^c f(x)\,dx.
\end{aligned}
$$

So the complex Riemann integral is linear in the integrand and additive with respect to subdivision of the interval, just as in the real-valued case.

# 3.2.2 Antiderivatives of Complex-Valued Functions


> [!review]
> 1.) What does it mean for a function $F$ to be an antiderivative of a piecewise continuous complex-valued function $f$ on $[a, b]$ ? Why might an antiderivative built interval-by-interval fail to be continuous at the partition points, and how can the constants of integration be chosen to yield a continuous antiderivative on all of $[a, b]$ ?
> 2.) How does the fundamental theorem of calculus extend from real-valued continuous functions to piecewise continuous complex-valued functions on $[a, b]$ ? Prove your answer.
> 3.) How do any two continuous antiderivatives of the same piecewise continuous complexvalued function on $[a, b]$ relate to each other? Prove your answer.



##### solution

##### problem 1

Let $f$ be a piecewise continuous complex-valued function on $[a,b]$. A function $F$ is called an antiderivative of $f$ on $[a,b]$ if

$$
F'(x)=f(x)
$$

at every point where $f$ is continuous. If we write

$$
f(x)=u(x)+iv(x),\qquad F(x)=U(x)+iV(x),
$$

then this means

$$
U'(x)=u(x),\qquad V'(x)=v(x)
$$

at all points of continuity of $f$.

Now write $[a,b]$ as a finite union of adjacent closed subintervals

$$
[a_0,a_1],[a_1,a_2],\ldots,[a_{m-1},a_m],
\qquad a_0=a,\quad a_m=b,
$$

such that $f$ is continuous on each subinterval. On each interval $[a_{j-1},a_j]$, let $F_j$ be an antiderivative of the restriction of $f$ to that interval. Each $F_j$ is determined only up to an arbitrary complex constant. If these constants are chosen independently, then the values of $F_j$ and $F_{j+1}$ at the common endpoint $a_j$ need not agree, so the piecewise-defined antiderivative may fail to be continuous at the partition points.

To obtain a continuous antiderivative on all of $[a,b]$, choose the constants successively. First choose the constant in $F_1$. Then choose the constant in $F_2$ so that

$$
\lim_{x\uparrow a_1}F_1(x)=\lim_{x\downarrow a_1}F_2(x).
$$

After that, choose the constant in $F_3$ so that

$$
\lim_{x\uparrow a_2}F_2(x)=\lim_{x\downarrow a_2}F_3(x),
$$

and continue in the same way. In general, once $F_j$ has been fixed, choose the constant in $F_{j+1}$ so that

$$
\lim_{x\uparrow a_j}F_j(x)=\lim_{x\downarrow a_j}F_{j+1}(x).
$$

With these choices, the piecewise-defined function $F$ is continuous on each subinterval and also continuous at every partition point, so it is a continuous antiderivative on all of $[a,b]$.

##### problem 2

The fundamental theorem of calculus extends as follows: if $f$ is a piecewise continuous complex-valued function on $[a,b]$ and $F$ is a continuous antiderivative of $f$ on $[a,b]$, then

$$
\int_a^b f(x)\,dx=F(b)-F(a).
$$

To prove this, first consider the case in which $f$ is continuous on all of $[a,b]$. Write

$$
f(x)=u(x)+iv(x),\qquad F(x)=U(x)+iV(x).
$$

Since $F'(x)=f(x)$, we have

$$
U'(x)=u(x),\qquad V'(x)=v(x).
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_a^b f(x)\,dx
&=\int_a^b u(x)\,dx+i\int_a^b v(x)\,dx \\
&=(U(b)-U(a))+i(V(b)-V(a)) \\
&=(U(b)+iV(b))-(U(a)+iV(a)) \\
&=F(b)-F(a).
\end{aligned}
$$

Now suppose $f$ is only piecewise continuous. Choose partition points

$$
a=a_0<a_1<\cdots<a_m=b
$$

so that $f$ is continuous on each subinterval $[a_{j-1},a_j]$. Since $F$ is a continuous antiderivative, the continuous-case result applies on each subinterval, so

$$
\int_{a_{j-1}}^{a_j} f(x)\,dx=F(a_j)-F(a_{j-1})
$$

for each $j$. Summing over $j=1,\dots,m$, we obtain

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_a^b f(x)\,dx
&=\sum_{j=1}^m \int_{a_{j-1}}^{a_j} f(x)\,dx \\
&=\sum_{j=1}^m \bigl(F(a_j)-F(a_{j-1})\bigr) \\
&=\bigl(F(a_1)-F(a_0)\bigr)+\bigl(F(a_2)-F(a_1)\bigr)+\cdots+\bigl(F(a_m)-F(a_{m-1})\bigr) \\
&=F(a_m)-F(a_0) \\
&=F(b)-F(a).
\end{aligned}
$$

So the same formula holds for piecewise continuous complex-valued functions.

##### problem 3

Any two continuous antiderivatives of the same piecewise continuous complex-valued function on $[a,b]$ differ by a complex constant.

Indeed, let $F$ and $G$ be continuous antiderivatives of the same function $f$, and define

$$
H(x)=F(x)-G(x).
$$

Then $H$ is continuous on $[a,b]$. At every point where $f$ is continuous,

$$
H'(x)=F'(x)-G'(x)=f(x)-f(x)=0.
$$

Now choose partition points

$$
a=a_0<a_1<\cdots<a_m=b
$$

so that $f$ is continuous on each subinterval $[a_{j-1},a_j]$. On each such interval we have $H'(x)=0$, so by the real-variable fact applied to the real and imaginary parts of $H$, the function $H$ is constant on each interval $[a_{j-1},a_j]$. Write that constant as $C_j$.

Since $H$ is continuous on all of $[a,b]$, the constants on adjacent intervals must agree at the common endpoint:

$$
C_j=\lim_{x\uparrow a_j}H(x)=H(a_j)=\lim_{x\downarrow a_j}H(x)=C_{j+1}.
$$

Thus

$$
C_1=C_2=\cdots=C_m=:C,
$$

and therefore

$$
H(x)=C
$$

for every $x\in [a,b]$. Hence

$$
F(x)-G(x)=C,
$$

or equivalently,

$$
F(x)=G(x)+C.
$$

So any two continuous antiderivatives of the same piecewise continuous complex-valued function differ by a single complex constant on all of $[a,b]$.

The following is an extension of the fundamental theorem of calculus to piecewise continuous complex functions.

> [!theorem] Theorem 1: Definite Integral of
> 
> Suppose that $f$ is a piecewise continuous complex-valued function on the interval $[a, b]$ and let $F$ be a continuous antiderivative of $f$ in $[a, b]$. Then
> 
> $$
> \int_{a}^{b} f(x) d x=F(b)-F(a)
> $$
> 
> 

**Proof** Suppose first that $f=u+i v$ is continuous on $[a, b]$. Let $F(x)=U(x)+ i V(x)$ be an antiderivative of $f$. Using (4) and the fundamental theorem of calculus, we sce that

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{a}^{b} f(x) d x & =\int_{a}^{b} u(x) d x+i \int_{a}^{b} v(x) d x \\
& =(U(b)-U(a))+i(V(b)-V(a)) \\
& =(U(b)+i V(b))-(U(a)+i V(a))=F(b)-F(a)
\end{aligned}
$$

and hence (8) holds. If $f$ is piecewise continuous on $[a, b]$, we use (5) and the previous case, and get

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{a}^{b} f(x) d x & =\sum_{j=1}^{m} \int_{a_{j-1}}^{a_{j}} f(x) d x=\sum_{j=1}^{m}\left(F\left(a_{j}\right)-F\left(a_{j-1}\right)\right) \\
& =F\left(a_{m}\right)-F\left(a_{0}\right)=F(b)-F(a)
\end{aligned}
$$

which proves (8).

It is easy to show that two continuous antiderivatives of $f$ differ by a complex constant on $[a, b]$. Motivated by Theorem 1, we will write

$$
\int f(x) d x=F(x)+C
$$

where $C$ is an arbitrary complex constant, to denote the set of all continuous antiderivatives of $f$. For example, if $\alpha \neq 0$ is a complex number, then

$$
\int e^{\alpha t} d t=\frac{1}{\alpha} e^{\alpha t}+C \quad(\alpha \neq 0)
$$

as can be checked by verifying that the derivative of the right side is equal to the integrand on the left side. This simple integral of a complex-valued function has an interesting application to the evaluation of tedious integrals from calculus.


> [!exercise] Exercise 3: Integrating $e^{a x} \cos b x$ and $e^{a x} \sin b x$
> 
> Let $a$ and $b$ be arbitrary nonzero real numbers. Compute
> 
> $$
> I_{1}=\int e^{a x} \cos b x d x \quad \text { and } \quad I_{2}=\int e^{a x} \sin b x d x
> $$
> 


##### solution

Consider

$$
\int e^{(a+i b)x}\,dx=\frac{1}{a+i b}e^{(a+i b)x}+C.
$$

Since

$$
e^{(a+i b)x}=e^{a x}(\cos b x+i\sin b x)
$$

and

$$
\frac{1}{a+i b}=\frac{a-i b}{(a+i b)(a-i b)}=\frac{a-i b}{a^2+b^2},
$$

we get

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int e^{(a+i b)x}\,dx
&=\frac{a-i b}{a^2+b^2}e^{a x}(\cos b x+i\sin b x)+C \\
&=\frac{e^{a x}}{a^2+b^2}(a-i b)(\cos b x+i\sin b x)+C \\
&=\frac{e^{a x}}{a^2+b^2}\left(a\cos b x+a i\sin b x-i b\cos b x-i^2 b\sin b x\right)+C \\
&=\frac{e^{a x}}{a^2+b^2}\left(a\cos b x+a i\sin b x-i b\cos b x+b\sin b x\right)+C \\
&=\frac{e^{a x}}{a^2+b^2}\left((a\cos b x+b\sin b x)+i(a\sin b x-b\cos b x)\right)+C.
\end{aligned}
$$

But also

$$
\int e^{(a+i b)x}\,dx
=\int e^{a x}\cos b x\,dx+i\int e^{a x}\sin b x\,dx
=I_1+iI_2.
$$

Equating real and imaginary parts, we obtain

$$
I_1=\int e^{a x}\cos b x\,dx=\frac{e^{a x}}{a^2+b^2}(a\cos b x+b\sin b x)+C
$$

and

$$
I_2=\int e^{a x}\sin b x\,dx=\frac{e^{a x}}{a^2+b^2}(a\sin b x-b\cos b x)+C.
$$

# 3.2.3 Path or Contour Integrals


> [!review]
> 1.) How is the path (contour) integral $\int_\gamma f(z) d z$ of a continuous complex-valued function $f$ over a path $\gamma:[a, b] \rightarrow \mathbb{C}$ defined, and how does a Riemann-sum construction on the path lead to this definition?
> 2.) What linearity, path-reversal, and contour-additivity properties does the path integral satisfy? Prove your answer.


##### solution

##### problem 1

Let $\gamma:[a,b]\to\mathbb{C}$ be a path, so $\gamma$ is continuous and $\gamma'$ is piecewise continuous, and let $f$ be continuous on the graph of $\gamma$. The path integral of $f$ over $\gamma$ is defined by

$$
\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz=\int_a^b f(\gamma(t))\gamma'(t)\,dt.
$$

This definition is motivated by a Riemann-sum construction along the path. If

$$
a=t_0<t_1<\cdots<t_m=b
$$

is a partition of $[a,b]$ and $t_k^\star\in [t_{k-1},t_k]$, then one forms the sum

$$
\sum_{k=1}^m f(\gamma(t_k^\star))\bigl(\gamma(t_k)-\gamma(t_{k-1})\bigr).
$$

Writing

$$
\gamma(t)=x(t)+iy(t),
$$

we have

$$
\gamma(t_k)-\gamma(t_{k-1})
=
\bigl(x(t_k)-x(t_{k-1})\bigr)+i\bigl(y(t_k)-y(t_{k-1})\bigr).
$$

So the Riemann-like sum becomes

$$
\sum_{k=1}^m f(\gamma(t_k^\star))\bigl(x(t_k)-x(t_{k-1})\bigr)
+i\sum_{k=1}^m f(\gamma(t_k^\star))\bigl(y(t_k)-y(t_{k-1})\bigr).
$$

As the partition is refined, these become the ordinary Riemann integrals

$$
\int_a^b f(\gamma(t))x'(t)\,dt
+i\int_a^b f(\gamma(t))y'(t)\,dt,
$$

and therefore

$$
\int_a^b f(\gamma(t))x'(t)\,dt
+i\int_a^b f(\gamma(t))y'(t)\,dt
=
\int_a^b f(\gamma(t))\bigl(x'(t)+iy'(t)\bigr)\,dt
=
\int_a^b f(\gamma(t))\gamma'(t)\,dt.
$$

This is why the path integral is defined by the formula above.

##### problem 2

The path integral satisfies the same linearity property as the Riemann integral. If $f$ and $g$ are continuous on $\gamma$ and $\alpha,\beta\in\mathbb{C}$, then

$$
\int_\gamma (\alpha f(z)+\beta g(z))\,dz
=
\alpha\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz+\beta\int_\gamma g(z)\,dz.
$$

Indeed,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_\gamma (\alpha f(z)+\beta g(z))\,dz
&=\int_a^b (\alpha f(\gamma(t))+\beta g(\gamma(t)))\gamma'(t)\,dt \\
&=\int_a^b \alpha f(\gamma(t))\gamma'(t)\,dt+\int_a^b \beta g(\gamma(t))\gamma'(t)\,dt \\
&=\alpha\int_a^b f(\gamma(t))\gamma'(t)\,dt+\beta\int_a^b g(\gamma(t))\gamma'(t)\,dt \\
&=\alpha\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz+\beta\int_\gamma g(z)\,dz.
\end{aligned}
$$

If $\gamma_r$ denotes the reverse of $\gamma$, then

$$
\int_{\gamma_r} f(z)\,dz=-\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz.
$$

To prove this, use the parametrization

$$
\gamma_r(t)=\gamma(a+b-t),\qquad a\le t\le b.
$$

Then

$$
\gamma_r'(t)=-\gamma'(a+b-t),
$$

so

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{\gamma_r} f(z)\,dz
&=\int_a^b f(\gamma_r(t))\gamma_r'(t)\,dt \\
&=\int_a^b f(\gamma(a+b-t))\bigl(-\gamma'(a+b-t)\bigr)\,dt \\
&=-\int_a^b f(\gamma(a+b-t))\gamma'(a+b-t)\,dt.
\end{aligned}
$$

Now let

$$
T=a+b-t.
$$

Then

$$
dT=-dt,
$$

and when $t=a$, $T=b$, while when $t=b$, $T=a$. Hence

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{\gamma_r} f(z)\,dz
&=-\int_b^a f(\gamma(T))\gamma'(T)(-\,dT) \\
&=\int_b^a f(\gamma(T))\gamma'(T)\,dT \\
&=-\int_a^b f(\gamma(T))\gamma'(T)\,dT \\
&=-\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz.
\end{aligned}
$$

Finally, if $\Gamma=(\gamma_1,\gamma_2,\ldots,\gamma_m)$ is a contour made of successive path segments and $f$ is continuous on $\Gamma$, then

$$
\int_\Gamma f(z)\,dz=\sum_{k=1}^m \int_{\gamma_k} f(z)\,dz.
$$

This is the additivity of the path integral with respect to concatenation of paths. It follows because, after parametrizing the whole contour by traversing $\gamma_1$, then $\gamma_2$, and so on, the defining integral over the full parameter interval splits into a sum of integrals over the subintervals corresponding to the individual pieces. That is exactly the additivity property of the Riemann integral over adjacent intervals.

DEFINITION 1 PATH OR CONTOUR INTEGRALS

For general piecewise smooth $\gamma$, the derivative $\gamma^{\prime}(t)$ may not be continuous but just piecewise continuous. This makes the integrand in (14) piecewise continuous. We evaluate the integral in this case as a finite sum of integrals of continuous functions, as we did in (5). All of this leads us to the following important definition.



> [!definition] definition 1: Path (or Contour) Integrals
> Suppose that $\gamma$ is a path over a closed interval $[a, b]$ and that $f$ is a cont inuous complex-valued function defined on the graph of $\gamma$. The path or contour integral of $f$ on $\gamma$ is given by
> 
> $$
> \int_{\gamma} f(z) d z=\int_{a}^{b} f(\gamma(t)) \gamma^{\prime}(t) d t
> $$
> 
> Other notations for the path integral are $\int_{\gamma} f(z) d \gamma$ or simply $\int_{\gamma} f d \gamma$.


Thus, after parametrizing the path by a closed interval $[a, b]$, the path integral becomes a Riemann integral of a piecewise continuous complex-valued function over the interval $[a, b]$.

Because the path integral is a Riemann integral, several properties of the latter integral carry over to the path integral. We have the following useful properties.



> [!proposition] proposition 2: Properties of the Path Integral
> 
> (i) Suppose that $\gamma(t)$ is a path on $[a, b], f$ and $g$ are continuous functions on $\gamma$, and $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are complex numbers. Then
> 
> $$
> \int_{\gamma}(\alpha f(z)+\beta g(z)) d z=\alpha \int_{\gamma} f(z) d z+\beta \int_{\gamma} g(z) d z
> $$
> 
> (ii) Let $\gamma_{r}$ denote the reverse of $\gamma$. Then
> 
> $$
> \int_{\gamma_{r}} f(z) d z=-\int_{\gamma} f(z) d z
> $$
> 
> (iii) If $\Gamma=\left(\gamma_{1}, \gamma_{2}, \ldots, \gamma_{m}\right)$ is a contour and $f$ is continuous on $\Gamma$, then
> 
> $$
> \int_{\Gamma} f(z) d z=\sum_{k=1}^{m} \int_{\gamma_{k}} f(z) d z
> $$
> 


**Proof** (i) Express the path integrals as Riemann integrals using (15) and then use (6). (ii) Recall the parametrization of the reverse of $\gamma$ from (3), Section 3.1: $\gamma_{r}(t)=\gamma(b+a-t)$, where $t$ runs over the same interval $[a, b]$ that parametrizes $\gamma$. Then $\gamma_{r}^{\prime}(t)=-\gamma^{\prime}(b+a-t)$ and so

$$
\int_{\gamma_{r}} f(z) d z=-\int_{a}^{b} f(\gamma(b+a-t)) \gamma^{\prime}(b+a-t) d t
$$

Making the change of variables $T=b+a-t$, we obtain

$$
\int_{\gamma_{r}} f(z) d z=\int_{b}^{a} f(\gamma(T)) \gamma^{\prime}(T) d T=-\int_{a}^{b} f(\gamma(T)) \gamma^{\prime}(T) d T=-\int_{\gamma} f(z) d z
$$



by (15). The proof of (iii) involves parametrizing $\Gamma=\left(\gamma_{1}, \gamma_{2}, \ldots, \gamma_{m}\right)$ and using additivity of the Riemann integral over intervals (7). The details are worked in Exercise 42. $\square$

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{[z_1,z_2,z_3,z_1]}\bar z\,dz
&=\int_{[z_1,z_2]}\bar z\,dz+\int_{[z_2,z_3]}\bar z\,dz+\int_{[z_3,z_1]}\bar z\,dz \\
&=0+i+i \\
&=2i.
\end{aligned}
$$

##### problem 7(f)

For the segment $\left[z_1,z_3\right]$ from $-1$ to $i$, take

$$
\gamma(t)=-1+t(i-(-1))=-1+t+it,\qquad 0\le t\le 1.
$$

Then

$$
\overline{\gamma(t)}=-1+t-it
$$

and

$$
\gamma'(t)=1+i.
$$

Hence

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{[z_1,z_3]}\bar z\,dz
&=\int_0^1 \overline{\gamma(t)}\,\gamma'(t)\,dt \\
&=\int_0^1 (-1+t-it)(1+i)\,dt \\
&=\int_0^1 \bigl(-1-i+t+it-it-i^2t\bigr)\,dt \\
&=\int_0^1 \bigl(-1-i+t+it-it+t\bigr)\,dt \\
&=\int_0^1 \bigl(2t-1-i\bigr)\,dt \\
&=\left[t^2-t-it\right]_0^1 \\
&=\left(1-1-i\right)-0 \\
&=-i.
\end{aligned}
$$

> [!review]
> 1.) Does the path integral $\int_\gamma f(z) d z$ of a continuous complex-valued function between two points depend only on the endpoints of $\gamma$, or can it depend on the full path joining them?
> 2.) What does it mean for two parametrizations $\gamma_1:[a, b] \rightarrow \mathbb{C}$ and $\gamma_2:[c, d] \rightarrow \mathbb{C}$ to be equivalent parametrizations of the same path, and how does the path integral behave under equivalent parametrizations? Prove your answer.


##### solution

##### problem 1

In general, the path integral

$$
\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz
$$

does not depend only on the endpoints of $\gamma$. It can depend on the entire path joining those endpoints. That is, if two different paths connect the same initial point to the same terminal point, the corresponding integrals need not be equal.

This is exactly what the examples preceding this review show. Two different paths from the same starting point to the same ending point can produce different values of the integral. So, at this stage, path independence is not automatic. It is a special property that holds only under additional hypotheses on $f$, which are developed in the next section.

##### problem 2

Two parametrizations

$$
\gamma_1:[a,b]\to\mathbb C
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\gamma_2:[c,d]\to\mathbb C
$$

are called equivalent parametrizations of the same path if there is an increasing continuously differentiable function

$$
\phi:[c,d]\to [a,b]
$$

such that

$$
\phi(c)=a,\qquad \phi(d)=b,
$$

and

$$
\gamma_2(t)=\gamma_1(\phi(t))
$$

for every $t\in[c,d]$.

The path integral is unchanged under equivalent parametrizations. More precisely,

$$
\int_{\gamma_1} f(z)\,dz=\int_{\gamma_2} f(z)\,dz.
$$

To prove this, start from the definition of the path integral:

$$
\int_{\gamma_2} f(z)\,dz=\int_c^d f(\gamma_2(t))\gamma_2'(t)\,dt.
$$

Since

$$
\gamma_2(t)=\gamma_1(\phi(t)),
$$

the chain rule gives

$$
\gamma_2'(t)=\gamma_1'(\phi(t))\phi'(t).
$$

Substituting these into the integral, we obtain

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{\gamma_2} f(z)\,dz
&=\int_c^d f(\gamma_1(\phi(t)))\gamma_1'(\phi(t))\phi'(t)\,dt.
\end{aligned}
$$

Now make the change of variables

$$
s=\phi(t),
\qquad
ds=\phi'(t)\,dt.
$$

Because $\phi(c)=a$ and $\phi(d)=b$, the limits change from $t=c,d$ to $s=a,b$. Hence

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{\gamma_2} f(z)\,dz
&=\int_a^b f(\gamma_1(s))\gamma_1'(s)\,ds \\
&=\int_{\gamma_1} f(z)\,dz.
\end{aligned}
$$

So the value of the integral depends on the geometric path and its orientation, but not on which equivalent parametrization is used to trace that same path.


+++++



Comparing the integrals in (23) and (25), we are led to the following somewhat disturbing conclusion: If $f$ is a continuous function in a region that contains $z_{1}, z_{2}$, and $z_{3}$, then the path integral of $f$ on a line directly connecting $z_{1}$ to $z_{3}$ is not necessarily equal to the path integral of $f$ from $z_{1}$ to $z_{3}$ along a different path (say, one that goes through $z_{2}$ ). In other words, the path integral of $f$ from $z_{1}$ to $z_{3}$ is not independent of the path joining $z_{1}$ to $z_{3}$. Is the path integral ever independent from path? We answer this important question in the next section by formulating necessary and sufficient conditions for independence of path.

Another question that comes to mind as we work with path integrals concerns the parametrization of the path. Since a given path may be parametrized in many different ways, it is natural to ask whether the integral is independent of the choice of the parametrization. Noting that the Riemann-like sum of the path integral (13) can be written in the form

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{m} f\left(z_{k}^{\star}\right)\left(z_{k}-z_{k-1}\right),
$$

which makes no reference to $t$, we would expect that the integral is independent of the parametrization. Indeed, this is true as long as we are describing the same path.

For example, the positively oriented unit circle $C_{1}(0)$ can be parametrized by $\gamma_{1}(t)=e^{i t}, 0 \leq t \leq 2 \pi$ or $\gamma_{2}(t)=e^{2 i t}, 0 \leq t \leq \pi$. If $f(z)$ is a continuous function on $C_{1}(0)$, we would expect that $\int_{\gamma_{1}} f(z) d z=\int_{\gamma_{2}} f(z) d z$. In fact, using the definition of path integrals and a simple change of variables $s=2 t$, $d s=2 d t$, we have

$$
\int_{\gamma_{2}} f(z) d z=\int_{0}^{\pi} f\left(e^{2 i t}\right) 2 i e^{2 i t} d t=\int_{0}^{2 \pi} f\left(e^{i s}\right) i e^{i s} d s=\int_{\gamma_{1}} f(z) d z
$$

as expected. The same proof works in general, but we have to explain what we mean by two parametrizations being the same.

We will say that $\gamma_{1}(t), a \leq t \leq b$, and $\gamma_{2}(t), c \leq t \leq d$, are **equivalent parametrizations** if there is an increasing continuously differentiable function $\phi(t)$ from $[c, d]$ onto $[a, b]$ such that $\phi(c)=a$ and $\phi(d)=b$ and $\gamma_{2}(t)=\gamma_{1} \circ \phi(t)$ for all $t$ in $[c, d]$.

In the case of the unit circle and the two parametrizations $\gamma_{1}(t)=e^{i t}$, $0 \leq t \leq 2 \pi$ and $\gamma_{2}(t)=e^{2 i t}, 0 \leq t \leq \pi$, we see that $\gamma_{1}$ and $\gamma_{2}$ are equivalent by taking $\phi(t)=2 t$.

The next result confirms a property that we would expect from a path integral.



> [!proposition] Proposition 3: Independence of Parametrization
> Suppose that $\gamma_{1}(t), a \leq t \leq b$, and $\gamma_{2}(t), c \leq t \leq d$, are equivalent parametrizations of the same path $\gamma$ and let $f$ be a continuous function on $\gamma$. Then
> 
> $$
> \int_{\gamma_{1}} f(z) d z=\int_{\gamma_{2}} f(z) d z
> $$
> 

**Proof** Applying the definition of path integrals, and using $\gamma_{2}(t)=\gamma_{1}(\phi(t))$ and $\gamma_{2}^{\prime}(t)=\gamma_{1}^{\prime}(\phi(t)) \phi^{\prime}(t)$, we obtain

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{\gamma_{2}} f(z) d z & =\int_{c}^{d} f\left(\gamma_{2}(t)\right) \gamma_{2}^{\prime}(t) d t=\int_{c}^{d} f\left(\gamma_{1}(\phi(t))\right) \gamma_{1}^{\prime}(\phi(t)) \phi^{\prime}(t) d t \\
& =\int_{a}^{b} f\left(\gamma_{1}(s)\right) \gamma_{1}^{\prime}(s) d s=\int_{\gamma_{1}} f(z) d z
\end{aligned}
$$

where the equality before last follows by setting $s=\phi(t), d s=\phi^{\prime}(t) d t$.

# 3.2.4 Arc Length and Bounds for Integrals


> [!review]
> 1.) How is the length of a smooth path $\gamma:[a, b] \rightarrow \mathbb{C}$ defined in terms of inscribed polygonal approximation, and how does this lead to the integral formula $l(\gamma)=\int_a^b\left|\gamma^{\prime}(t)\right| d t$ ? Derive the formula.
> 2.) If $f$ is a continuous function on a path $\gamma$ with $|f(z)| \leq M$ on $\gamma$, what upper bounds on $\left|\int_\gamma f(z) d z\right|$ can be derived - both in terms of $M$ and the length $l(\gamma)$, and as a sharper bound in terms of the integrand $|f(\gamma(t))|\left|\gamma^{\prime}(t)\right|$ ? Prove both.
> 3.) Under what choice of path does the path integral $\int_\gamma f(z) d z$ reduce to the Riemann integral $\int_a^b f(t) d t$ of a piecewise continuous complex-valued function on $[a, b]$, and what inequalities on $\left|\int_a^b f(t) d t\right|$ follow from the ML-inequality in this case?




##### solution

##### problem 1

Let $\gamma:[a,b]\to\mathbb C$ be a smooth path, and write

$$
\gamma(t)=x(t)+iy(t).
$$

To approximate the length of the path, choose a partition

$$
a=t_0<t_1<\cdots<t_m=b
$$

and join the points $\gamma(t_0),\gamma(t_1),\ldots,\gamma(t_m)$ by line segments. The length of the corresponding inscribed polygonal path is

$$
\sum_{k=1}^m |\gamma(t_k)-\gamma(t_{k-1})|.
$$

Since

$$
\gamma(t_k)-\gamma(t_{k-1})
=
\bigl(x(t_k)-x(t_{k-1})\bigr)+i\bigl(y(t_k)-y(t_{k-1})\bigr),
$$

we have

$$
|\gamma(t_k)-\gamma(t_{k-1})|
=
\sqrt{\bigl(x(t_k)-x(t_{k-1})\bigr)^2+\bigl(y(t_k)-y(t_{k-1})\bigr)^2}.
$$

Hence the polygonal approximation is

$$
\sum_{k=1}^m \sqrt{\bigl(x(t_k)-x(t_{k-1})\bigr)^2+\bigl(y(t_k)-y(t_{k-1})\bigr)^2}.
$$

The length $l(\gamma)$ is defined as the limit of these sums as the partition is refined. To identify the limit, apply the mean value theorem to $x$ and $y$ on each subinterval:

$$
x(t_k)-x(t_{k-1})=x'(\alpha_k)(t_k-t_{k-1}),
$$

$$
y(t_k)-y(t_{k-1})=y'(\beta_k)(t_k-t_{k-1}),
$$

for some $\alpha_k,\beta_k\in [t_{k-1},t_k]$. Substituting gives

$$
\begin{aligned}
&\sum_{k=1}^m \sqrt{\bigl(x(t_k)-x(t_{k-1})\bigr)^2+\bigl(y(t_k)-y(t_{k-1})\bigr)^2} \\
&\qquad=
\sum_{k=1}^m \sqrt{x'(\alpha_k)^2+y'(\beta_k)^2}\,(t_k-t_{k-1}).
\end{aligned}
$$

As the partition gets finer, this is recognized as a Riemann sum for

$$
\int_a^b \sqrt{x'(t)^2+y'(t)^2}\,dt.
$$

Therefore

$$
l(\gamma)=\int_a^b \sqrt{x'(t)^2+y'(t)^2}\,dt.
$$

Since

$$
\gamma'(t)=x'(t)+iy'(t),
$$

we also have

$$
|\gamma'(t)|=\sqrt{x'(t)^2+y'(t)^2},
$$

and hence

$$
l(\gamma)=\int_a^b |\gamma'(t)|\,dt.
$$

For a piecewise smooth path, we apply the same formula on each smooth piece and add the resulting lengths.

##### problem 2

Let $f$ be continuous on $\gamma$ and suppose that

$$
|f(z)|\le M
$$

for all $z$ on $\gamma$. Then the path integral satisfies the $Ml$-inequality

$$
\left|\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz\right|\le M\,l(\gamma).
$$

To prove this, begin with the definition

$$
\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz=\int_a^b f(\gamma(t))\gamma'(t)\,dt.
$$

Using the sharper estimate for an ordinary integral,

$$
\left|\int_a^b g(t)\,dt\right|\le \int_a^b |g(t)|\,dt,
$$

with

$$
g(t)=f(\gamma(t))\gamma'(t),
$$

we obtain

$$
\begin{aligned}
\left|\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz\right|
&=\left|\int_a^b f(\gamma(t))\gamma'(t)\,dt\right| \\
&\le \int_a^b |f(\gamma(t))\gamma'(t)|\,dt \\
&=\int_a^b |f(\gamma(t))|\,|\gamma'(t)|\,dt.
\end{aligned}
$$

This is the sharper bound:

$$
\left|\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz\right|
\le
\int_a^b |f(\gamma(t))|\,|\gamma'(t)|\,dt.
$$

Since $|f(\gamma(t))|\le M$ for all $t$, we further get

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_a^b |f(\gamma(t))|\,|\gamma'(t)|\,dt
&\le \int_a^b M|\gamma'(t)|\,dt \\
&=M\int_a^b |\gamma'(t)|\,dt \\
&=Ml(\gamma).
\end{aligned}
$$

Combining the two estimates gives

$$
\left|\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz\right|
\le
\int_a^b |f(\gamma(t))|\,|\gamma'(t)|\,dt
\le
Ml(\gamma).
$$

##### problem 3

If we choose the path

$$
\gamma(t)=t,\qquad a\le t\le b,
$$

then $\gamma(t)$ is real for every $t\in [a,b]$, so the graph of $\gamma$ is exactly the closed interval $[a,b]$ viewed as a path in the complex plane. Also,

$$
\gamma'(t)=\frac{d}{dt}(t)=1.
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz
\ &=\int_a^b f(\gamma(t))\gamma'(t)\,dt \\
&=\int_a^b f(t)\cdot 1\,dt \\
&=\int_a^b f(t)\,dt.
\end{aligned}
$$

So the ordinary Riemann integral is a special case of a path integral.

To compute the length of this path, use the arc-length formula:

$$
\begin{aligned}
l(\gamma)
&=\int_a^b |\gamma'(t)|\,dt \\
&=\int_a^b |1|\,dt \\
&=\int_a^b 1\,dt \\
&=[t]_a^b \\
&=b-a.
\end{aligned}
$$

Now apply the sharper bound for path integrals:

$$
\left|\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz\right|
\le
\int_a^b |f(\gamma(t))|\,|\gamma'(t)|\,dt.
$$

Since $\gamma(t)=t$ and $\gamma'(t)=1$, this becomes

$$
\begin{aligned}
\left|\int_a^b f(t)\,dt\right|
&=\left|\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz\right| \\
&\le \int_a^b |f(\gamma(t))|\,|\gamma'(t)|\,dt \\
&=\int_a^b |f(t)|\,|1|\,dt \\
&=\int_a^b |f(t)|\,dt.
\end{aligned}
$$

If in addition $|f(t)|\le M$ for all $t\in [a,b]$, then

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_a^b |f(t)|\,dt
&\le \int_a^b M\,dt \\
&=M\int_a^b 1\,dt \\
&=M[t]_a^b \\
&=M(b-a).
\end{aligned}
$$

Combining the last two inequalities, we obtain

$$
\left|\int_a^b f(t)\,dt\right|
\le
\int_a^b |f(t)|\,dt
\le
M(b-a).
$$

These are exactly the Riemann-integral inequalities obtained from the path-integral bounds in this special case.



+++++



Given a smooth path $\gamma:[a, b] \rightarrow \mathbb{C}$, write $\gamma(t)=x(t)+i y(t)$. The length of $\gamma$, denoted $l(\gamma)$, can be approximated by adding the length of line segments joining consecutive points on the graph of $\gamma$ as in _Figure 9_. The sum of the lengths of the line segments is given by

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{m}\left|\gamma\left(t_{k}\right)-\gamma\left(t_{k-1}\right)\right|=\sum_{k=1}^{m} \sqrt{\left(x\left(t_{k}\right)-x\left(t_{k-1}\right)\right)^{2}+\left(y\left(t_{k}\right)-y\left(t_{k-1}\right)\right)^{2}}
$$

where $a=t_{0}<t_{1}<t_{2}<\cdots<t_{m}=b$ is a partition of $[a, b]$. 



> [!figure] Figure 9
> 
> ![300](./images/858d24a8-ffa3-45ac-9ebe-5fc1408bbf28-022_520_529_324_76.jpg)
> Figure 9 Approximating the length of a path by adding the length of line segments.




Thus the length of $\gamma$ is the limit (when it exists) of the sums on the right side of (26) as the partition of $[a, b]$ gets finer and finer. To find this limit, we use the mean value theorem and write $\left(x\left(t_{k}\right)-x\left(t_{k-1}\right)\right)^{2}=\left(x^{\prime}\left(\alpha_{k}\right)\left(t_{k}-t_{k-1}\right)\right)^{2}$ and $\left(y\left(t_{k}\right)-y\left(t_{k-1}\right)\right)^{2}=\left(y^{\prime}\left(\beta_{k}\right)\left(t_{k}-t_{k-1}\right)\right)^{2}$, where $\alpha_{k}$ and $\beta_{k}$ are in $\left[t_{k-1}, t_{k}\right]$. Then (26) becomes

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{m} \sqrt{x^{\prime}\left(\alpha_{k}\right)^{2}+y^{\prime}\left(\beta_{k}\right)^{2}}\left(t_{k}-t_{k-1}\right)
$$

Recognizing this sum as a Riemann sum and taking limits as the partition gets finer, we recover the formula for arc length from calculus:

> [!equation] Equation
> 
> $$
> l(\gamma)=\int_{a}^{b} \sqrt{x^{\prime}(t)^{2}+y^{\prime}(t)^{2}} d t=\int_{a}^{b}\left|\gamma^{\prime}(t)\right| d t
> $$
> 

where the second equality follows from the complex notation $\gamma^{\prime}(t)=x^{\prime}(t)+ i y^{\prime}(t)$ and so $\sqrt{x^{\prime}(t)^{2}+y^{\prime}(t)^{2}}=\left|\gamma^{\prime}(t)\right|$. For piecewise smooth $\gamma$, we can repeat the previous analysis for each smooth part of $\gamma$ and then add the lengths of all smooth parts. This results in formula (27) for the arc length, where the integrand in this case is piecewise continuous. The element of arc length is usually denoted by $d s$. Thus,

$$
d s=\sqrt{x^{\prime}(t)^{2}+y^{\prime}(t)^{2}} d t .
$$

Use the arc-length formula

$$
l(\gamma)=\int_0^{2\pi} |\gamma'(t)|\,dt.

Differentiate $\gamma(t)$:

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
|\gamma'(t)|

&=a\sqrt{2-2\cos t} \\

Using the notion of length, we can extend to path integrals the familiar inequality for the Riemann integral: If $f$ is a continuous real-valued function of $[a, b]$ with $|f(x)| \leq M$ on $[a, b]$, then

$$
\left|\int_{a}^{b} f(x) d x\right| \leq M(b-a) .
$$

For path integrals, we have the following useful inequality, often called the $M l$-inequality.



> [!theorem] Theorem 2: Bounds for Path Integrals
> Suppose that $\gamma$ is a path over $[a, b]$ with length $l(\gamma)$, and $f$ is a continuous function on $\gamma$ such that $|f(z)| \leq M$ for all $z$ on $\gamma$. Then
> (29)
> 
> $$
> \left|\int_{\gamma} f(z) d z\right| \leq M l(\gamma) .
> $$
> 



**Proof** We start with the case of a smooth path $\gamma$ and consider a Riemann sum defining the path integral (13). Using the triangle inequality and the inequality $|f(z)| \leq M$ for $z$ on $\gamma$, we get

$$
\begin{aligned}
\left|\sum_{k=1}^{m} f\left(\gamma\left(t_{k}^{\star}\right)\right)\left(\gamma\left(t_{k}\right)-\gamma\left(t_{k-1}\right)\right)\right| & \leq \sum_{k=1}^{m}\left|f\left(\gamma\left(t_{k}^{\star}\right)\right)\left(\gamma\left(t_{k}\right)-\gamma\left(t_{k-1}\right)\right)\right| \\
& =\sum_{k=1}^{m}\left|f\left(\gamma\left(t_{k}^{\star}\right)\right)\right|\left|\left(\gamma\left(t_{k}\right)-\gamma\left(t_{k-1}\right)\right)\right| \\
& \leq M \sum_{k=1}^{m}\left|\left(\gamma\left(t_{k}\right)-\gamma\left(t_{k-1}\right)\right)\right| .
\end{aligned}
$$

Taking limits as the partition of $[a, b]$ gets finer, the sum on the left side converges to $\int_{\gamma} f(z) d z$ while the sum on the right side converges to $l(\gamma)$, implying (29) in the case of a smooth $\gamma$. If $\gamma$ is piecewise smooth, we can write $\gamma=\left(\gamma_{1}, \gamma_{2}, \ldots, \gamma_{n}\right)$, where each $\gamma_{j}$ is smooth. Then using (22) and applying (29) to each smooth part
of $\gamma$, we obtain

$$
\begin{aligned}
\left|\int_{\gamma} f(z) d z\right| & =\left|\sum_{j=1}^{n} \int_{\gamma_{j}} f(z) d z\right| \leq \sum_{j=1}^{n}\left|\int_{\gamma_{j}} f(z) d z\right| \\
& \leq M \sum_{j=1}^{n} l\left(\gamma_{j}\right)=M l(\gamma)
\end{aligned}
$$

where in the last equality we used the fact that the length of $\gamma$ is equal to the sum of the lengths of its parts.






Remembering that $\left|\left(\gamma\left(t_{k}\right)-\gamma\left(t_{k-1}\right)\right)\right|$ is an approximation of the length of the portion of the path $\gamma$ that joins the points $\gamma\left(t_{k-1}\right)$ and $\gamma\left(t_{k}\right)$, using an argument based on Riemann sums (recall (26) and (28)), we see that

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{m}\left|f\left(\gamma\left(t_{k}^{\star}\right)\right)\right|\left|\left(\gamma\left(t_{k}\right)-\gamma\left(t_{k-1}\right)\right)\right| \rightarrow \int_{a}^{b}|f(\gamma(t))|\left|\gamma^{\prime}(t)\right| d t
$$

as the partition of $[a, b]$ gets finer. Thus going back to (30) and arguing as we did in the proof of Theorem 2, we obtain the inequalities

> [!equation] Equation
> 
> $$
> \left|\int_{\gamma} f(z) d z\right| \leq \int_{a}^{b}|f(\gamma(t))|\left|\gamma^{\prime}(t)\right| d t \leq M l(\gamma)
> $$
> 

where $f$ and $\gamma$ are as in Theorem 2.

> [!exercise] Exercise 9: Bounding a path integral

> $$

> where $C_{1}(0)$ is the positively oriented unit circle.


##### solution

Let

$$
\gamma(t)=e^{it},\qquad 0\le t\le 2\pi,
$$

be the standard positively oriented parametrization of $C_1(0)$. Then the length of the path is

$$
l(C_1(0))=2\pi.
$$

To apply the ML-inequality, we need an upper bound for

$$
\left|e^{1/z}\right|
$$

on the unit circle. If $z\in C_1(0)$, then $z=e^{it}$ for some $t$, so

$$
\frac{1}{z}=\frac{1}{e^{it}}=e^{-it}=\cos t-i\sin t.
$$

Hence

$$
\operatorname{Re}\left(\frac{1}{z}\right)=\cos t.
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
\left|e^{1/z}\right|
&=\left|e^{\cos t-i\sin t}\right| \\
&=\left|e^{\cos t}e^{-i\sin t}\right| \\
&=e^{\cos t}\left|e^{-i\sin t}\right| \\
&=e^{\cos t}\cdot 1 \\
&=e^{\cos t} \\
&\le e.
\end{aligned}
$$

So we may take

$$
M=e.
$$

Now apply the ML-inequality:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\left|\int_{C_1(0)} e^{1/z}\,dz\right|
&\le Ml(C_1(0)) \\
&\le e\cdot 2\pi \\
&=2\pi e.
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus an upper bound is

$$
\boxed{2\pi e}.
$$

We close this section with a simple remark about path integrals. The path described by $\gamma(t)=t$ for $t$ in $[a, b]$ is the closed interval $[a, b]$. For such a path, we have $\gamma^{\prime}(t) d t=d t$, and if $f$ is a function defined on $\gamma$, the path integral (15) becomes

$$
\int_{\gamma} f(z) d z=\int_{a}^{b} f(t) d t
$$

showing that a Riemann integral of a piecewise continuous complex-valued function over $[a, b]$ is a special case of a path integral, where the path is the line segment $[a, b]$. So results about path integrals apply in particular to Riemann integrals. For example, if $f$ is a piecewise continuous complex-valued function on $[a, b]$ such that $|f(t)| \leq M$ for all $t$ in $[a, b]$, then inequalities (31) become

> [!equation] Equation
> $$
> \left|\int_{a}^{b} f(t) d t\right| \leq \int_{a}^{b}|f(t)| d t \leq M(b-a)
> $$

> [!exercise] Exercise 12
> 10. Prove all of Proposition 1. **(Hint: Split complex Riemann integrals into real and imaginary parts, then invoke relevant properties of real integrals.)**



##### solution

Proposition 1 has two parts.

##### problem 10(i)

Let

$$
f(x)=u(x)+iv(x),\qquad g(x)=p(x)+iq(x),
$$

where $u,v,p,q$ are real-valued piecewise continuous functions on $[a,b]$. Also write

$$
\alpha=\alpha_1+i\alpha_2,\qquad \beta=\beta_1+i\beta_2,
$$

where $\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\beta_1,\beta_2$ are real numbers.

We first expand $\alpha f(x)$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\alpha f(x)
&=(\alpha_1+i\alpha_2)(u(x)+iv(x)) \\
&=\alpha_1u(x)+i\alpha_1v(x)+i\alpha_2u(x)+i^2\alpha_2v(x) \\
&=\alpha_1u(x)-\alpha_2v(x)+i\bigl(\alpha_1v(x)+\alpha_2u(x)\bigr).
\end{aligned}
$$

Similarly,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\beta g(x)
&=(\beta_1+i\beta_2)(p(x)+iq(x)) \\
&=\beta_1p(x)+i\beta_1q(x)+i\beta_2p(x)+i^2\beta_2q(x) \\
&=\beta_1p(x)-\beta_2q(x)+i\bigl(\beta_1q(x)+\beta_2p(x)\bigr).
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
\alpha f(x)+\beta g(x)
&=\bigl(\alpha_1u(x)-\alpha_2v(x)+\beta_1p(x)-\beta_2q(x)\bigr) \\
&\quad+i\bigl(\alpha_1v(x)+\alpha_2u(x)+\beta_1q(x)+\beta_2p(x)\bigr).
\end{aligned}
$$

Now use the definition of the complex Riemann integral:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_a^b (\alpha f(x)+\beta g(x))\,dx
&=\int_a^b \bigl(\alpha_1u(x)-\alpha_2v(x)+\beta_1p(x)-\beta_2q(x)\bigr)\,dx \\
&\quad+i\int_a^b \bigl(\alpha_1v(x)+\alpha_2u(x)+\beta_1q(x)+\beta_2p(x)\bigr)\,dx.
\end{aligned}
$$

By linearity of the real-valued Riemann integral,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_a^b (\alpha f(x)+\beta g(x))\,dx
&=\alpha_1\int_a^b u(x)\,dx-\alpha_2\int_a^b v(x)\,dx+\beta_1\int_a^b p(x)\,dx-\beta_2\int_a^b q(x)\,dx \\
&\quad+i\alpha_1\int_a^b v(x)\,dx+i\alpha_2\int_a^b u(x)\,dx+i\beta_1\int_a^b q(x)\,dx+i\beta_2\int_a^b p(x)\,dx.
\end{aligned}
$$

Group the terms involving $f$ and the terms involving $g$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_a^b (\alpha f(x)+\beta g(x))\,dx
&=\left(\alpha_1\int_a^b u(x)\,dx-\alpha_2\int_a^b v(x)\,dx+i\alpha_1\int_a^b v(x)\,dx+i\alpha_2\int_a^b u(x)\,dx\right) \\
&\quad+\left(\beta_1\int_a^b p(x)\,dx-\beta_2\int_a^b q(x)\,dx+i\beta_1\int_a^b q(x)\,dx+i\beta_2\int_a^b p(x)\,dx\right).
\end{aligned}
$$

Factor $\alpha_1+i\alpha_2=\alpha$ and $\beta_1+i\beta_2=\beta$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_a^b (\alpha f(x)+\beta g(x))\,dx
&=(\alpha_1+i\alpha_2)\left(\int_a^b u(x)\,dx+i\int_a^b v(x)\,dx\right) \\
&\quad+(\beta_1+i\beta_2)\left(\int_a^b p(x)\,dx+i\int_a^b q(x)\,dx\right) \\
&=\alpha\int_a^b f(x)\,dx+\beta\int_a^b g(x)\,dx.
\end{aligned}
$$

This proves part (i).

##### problem 10(ii)

Let

$$
f(x)=u(x)+iv(x),
$$

where $u$ and $v$ are real-valued piecewise continuous functions on $[a,c]$, and let $a\le b\le c$.

By definition,

$$
\int_a^c f(x)\,dx=\int_a^c u(x)\,dx+i\int_a^c v(x)\,dx.
$$

Using additivity of the real-valued Riemann integral over adjacent intervals,

$$
\int_a^c u(x)\,dx=\int_a^b u(x)\,dx+\int_b^c u(x)\,dx
$$

and

$$
\int_a^c v(x)\,dx=\int_a^b v(x)\,dx+\int_b^c v(x)\,dx.
$$

Substitute these into the complex integral:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_a^c f(x)\,dx
&=\left(\int_a^b u(x)\,dx+\int_b^c u(x)\,dx\right)+i\left(\int_a^b v(x)\,dx+\int_b^c v(x)\,dx\right) \\
&=\left(\int_a^b u(x)\,dx+i\int_a^b v(x)\,dx\right)+\left(\int_b^c u(x)\,dx+i\int_b^c v(x)\,dx\right) \\
&=\int_a^b f(x)\,dx+\int_b^c f(x)\,dx.
\end{aligned}
$$

This proves part (ii).

\gamma(t)=z_0+Re^{it},\qquad 0\le t\le 2\pi.
$$

Then

$$
\gamma'(t)=iRe^{it}
$$

and

$$
\gamma(t)-z_0=Re^{it}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{C_R(z_0)} [z-z_0]^n\,dz
&=\int_0^{2\pi} [\gamma(t)-z_0]^n\gamma'(t)\,dt \\
&=\int_0^{2\pi} (Re^{it})^n iRe^{it}\,dt \\
&=\int_0^{2\pi} R^n e^{int} iRe^{it}\,dt \\
&=iR^{n+1}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{i(n+1)t}\,dt.
\end{aligned}

##### problem 11(a)

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{C_R(z_0)} [z-z_0]^n\,dz
&=iR^{n+1}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{i(n+1)t}\,dt \\
&=iR^{n+1}\left[\frac{e^{i(n+1)t}}{i(n+1)}\right]_0^{2\pi} \\
&=\frac{iR^{n+1}}{i(n+1)}\left(e^{2\pi i(n+1)}-1\right).
\end{aligned}
$$

Since $n+1$ is an integer,

$$
e^{2\pi i(n+1)}=1.
$$

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{C_R(z_0)} [z-z_0]^n\,dz
&=\frac{iR^{n+1}}{i(n+1)}(1-1) \\
&=0.
\end{aligned}

$$
[z-z_0]^n=[z-z_0]^{-1}=\frac{1}{z-z_0},
$$

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{C_R(z_0)} [z-z_0]^{-1}\,dz
&=\int_0^{2\pi} \frac{1}{\gamma(t)-z_0}\gamma'(t)\,dt \\
&=\int_0^{2\pi} \frac{1}{Re^{it}} iRe^{it}\,dt \\
&=\int_0^{2\pi} i\,dt \\
&=i[t]_0^{2\pi} \\
&=i(2\pi-0) \\
&=2\pi i.
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore

$$
\int_{C_R(z_0)} [z-z_0]^n\,dz=
\begin{cases}
0, & \text{if } n\neq -1,\\[4pt]

\end{cases}
$$

When $p=\pi$, we showed in Exercise 12 that the functions in this system are orthogonal on the interval $[-\pi, \pi]$. The corresponding result for arbitrary $p>0$ is as follows.
> (b) Show that
> 
> $$
> \int_{-p}^{p} e^{i \frac{m \pi}{p} x} e^{-i \frac{n \pi}{p} x} d x= \begin{cases}0 & \text { if } m \neq n \\ 2 p & \text { if } m=n\end{cases}
> $$
> 
> Thus the functions in the $2 p$-periodic exponential system are orthogonal on the interval $[-p, p]$.
> (c) Now suppose $m$ and $n$ are nonnegative integers. With the help of the identity in (a), or by Exercise 12(b), show that
> 
> $$
> \begin{array}{cl}
> \int_{-p}^{p} \cos \left(\frac{m \pi}{p} x\right) \cos \left(\frac{n \pi}{p} x\right) d x=0 & \text { if } m \neq n ; \\
> \int_{-p}^{p} \cos \left(\frac{m \pi}{p} x\right) \sin \left(\frac{n \pi}{p} x\right) d x=0 & \text { for all } m \text { and } n ; \\
> \int_{-p}^{p} \sin \left(\frac{m \pi}{p} x\right) \sin \left(\frac{n \pi}{p} x\right) d x=0 & \text { if } m \neq n ; \\
> \int_{-p}^{p} \cos ^{2}\left(\frac{m \pi}{p} x\right) d x=\int_{-p}^{p} \sin ^{2}\left(\frac{m \pi}{p} x\right) d x=p & \text { for all } m \neq 0
> \end{array}
> $$
> 
> These identities state that the $2 p$-periodic functions $1, \cos \left(\frac{\pi}{p} x\right), \cos \left(\frac{2 \pi}{p} x\right), \ldots$, $\sin \left(\frac{\pi}{p} x\right), \sin \left(\frac{2 \pi}{p} x\right), \ldots$, are orthogonal on the interval $[-p, p]$.



##### solution

##### problem 12(a)

We have

$$
e^{imx}e^{-inx}=e^{i(m-n)x}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} e^{imx}e^{-inx}\,dx
=
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} e^{i(m-n)x}\,dx.
$$

If $m=n$, then $m-n=0$, so

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} e^{imx}e^{-inx}\,dx
&=\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} 1\,dx \\
&=[x]_{-\pi}^{\pi} \\
&=\pi-(-\pi) \\
&=2\pi.
\end{aligned}
$$

If $m\neq n$, then $m-n\neq 0$, so

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} e^{imx}e^{-inx}\,dx
&=\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} e^{i(m-n)x}\,dx \\
&=\left[\frac{e^{i(m-n)x}}{i(m-n)}\right]_{-\pi}^{\pi} \\
&=\frac{e^{i(m-n)\pi}-e^{-i(m-n)\pi}}{i(m-n)}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Since $m-n$ is an integer,

$$
e^{i(m-n)\pi}=(-1)^{m-n}
$$

and

$$
e^{-i(m-n)\pi}=(-1)^{m-n}.
$$

Hence

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} e^{imx}e^{-inx}\,dx
&=\frac{(-1)^{m-n}-(-1)^{m-n}}{i(m-n)} \\
&=0.
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus

$$
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} e^{imx}e^{-inx}\,dx=
\begin{cases}
0, & \text{if } m\neq n,\\[4pt]
2\pi, & \text{if } m=n.
\end{cases}
$$

##### problem 12(b)

Use Euler's formulas

$$
\cos mx=\frac{e^{imx}+e^{-imx}}{2},
\qquad
\sin nx=\frac{e^{inx}-e^{-inx}}{2i}.
$$

First,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\cos mx\cos nx
&=\frac{e^{imx}+e^{-imx}}{2}\cdot \frac{e^{inx}+e^{-inx}}{2} \\
&=\frac{1}{4}\left(e^{i(m+n)x}+e^{i(m-n)x}+e^{i(n-m)x}+e^{-i(m+n)x}\right).
\end{aligned}
$$

If $m\neq n$, then $m-n\neq 0$, $n-m\neq 0$, and also $m+n\neq 0$ unless $m=n=0$, which is excluded by $m\neq n$. Hence every exponential term has nonzero frequency, and by part (a) each integral is $0$. Therefore

$$
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos mx\cos nx\,dx=0
\qquad (m\neq n).
$$

If $m=n\neq 0$, then

$$
\begin{aligned}
\cos^2 mx
&=\frac{1}{4}\left(e^{i2mx}+e^0+e^0+e^{-i2mx}\right) \\
&=\frac{1}{4}\left(e^{i2mx}+2+e^{-i2mx}\right).
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos^2 mx\,dx
&=\frac{1}{4}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} e^{i2mx}\,dx+\frac{1}{4}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} 2\,dx+\frac{1}{4}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} e^{-i2mx}\,dx \\
&=\frac{1}{4}(0)+\frac{1}{2}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} 1\,dx+\frac{1}{4}(0) \\
&=\frac{1}{2}(2\pi) \\
&=\pi.
\end{aligned}
$$

Next,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\cos mx\sin nx
&=\frac{e^{imx}+e^{-imx}}{2}\cdot \frac{e^{inx}-e^{-inx}}{2i} \\
&=\frac{1}{4i}\left(e^{i(m+n)x}-e^{i(m-n)x}+e^{i(n-m)x}-e^{-i(m+n)x}\right).
\end{aligned}
$$

If $m\neq n$, then all frequencies are nonzero except possibly $m+n$, but $m+n=0$ can occur only when $m=n=0$, and then $\sin nx=0$ identically. If $m=n$, then the middle two terms become $-1+1=0$. Thus in all cases the integral is $0$, and therefore

$$
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos mx\sin nx\,dx=0
\qquad \text{for all } m,n.
$$

Finally,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\sin mx\sin nx
&=\frac{e^{imx}-e^{-imx}}{2i}\cdot \frac{e^{inx}-e^{-inx}}{2i} \\
&=-\frac{1}{4}\left(e^{i(m+n)x}-e^{i(m-n)x}-e^{i(n-m)x}+e^{-i(m+n)x}\right).
\end{aligned}
$$

If $m\neq n$, then each exponential has nonzero frequency, so

$$
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \sin mx\sin nx\,dx=0.
$$

If $m=n\neq 0$, then

$$
\begin{aligned}
\sin^2 mx
&=-\frac{1}{4}\left(e^{i2mx}-1-1+e^{-i2mx}\right) \\
&=\frac{1}{4}\left(2-e^{i2mx}-e^{-i2mx}\right).
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \sin^2 mx\,dx
&=\frac{1}{2}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} 1\,dx-\frac{1}{4}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} e^{i2mx}\,dx-\frac{1}{4}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} e^{-i2mx}\,dx \\
&=\frac{1}{2}(2\pi)-\frac{1}{4}(0)-\frac{1}{4}(0) \\
&=\pi.
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus

$$
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos mx\cos nx\,dx=0
\qquad (m\neq n),
$$

$$
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos mx\sin nx\,dx=0
\qquad \text{for all } m,n,
$$

$$
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \sin mx\sin nx\,dx=0
\qquad (m\neq n),
$$

and

$$
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos^2 mx\,dx=\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \sin^2 mx\,dx=\pi
\qquad (m\neq 0).
$$

##### problem 13(a)

Let

$$
f_m(x)=e^{i\frac{m\pi}{p}x}.
$$

Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
f_m(x+2p)
&=e^{i\frac{m\pi}{p}(x+2p)} \\
&=e^{i\frac{m\pi}{p}x}e^{i\frac{m\pi}{p}(2p)} \\
&=e^{i\frac{m\pi}{p}x}e^{i2m\pi} \\
&=e^{i\frac{m\pi}{p}x}\cdot 1 \\
&=f_m(x).
\end{aligned}
$$

So each function $e^{i\frac{m\pi}{p}x}$ is $2p$-periodic.

##### problem 13(b)

We have

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{-p}^{p} e^{i\frac{m\pi}{p}x}e^{-i\frac{n\pi}{p}x}\,dx
&=\int_{-p}^{p} e^{i\frac{(m-n)\pi}{p}x}\,dx.
\end{aligned}
$$

Make the change of variables

$$
u=\frac{\pi}{p}x,
\qquad
x=\frac{p}{\pi}u,
\qquad
dx=\frac{p}{\pi}\,du.
$$

When $x=-p$, we have $u=-\pi$, and when $x=p$, we have $u=\pi$. Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{-p}^{p} e^{i\frac{m\pi}{p}x}e^{-i\frac{n\pi}{p}x}\,dx
&=\frac{p}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} e^{i(m-n)u}\,du.
\end{aligned}
$$

Now use the previously established identity

> [!equation] Equation
> $$
> \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} e^{ik u}\,du=
> \begin{cases}
> 0, & \text{if } k\neq 0,\\[4pt]
> 2\pi, & \text{if } k=0,
> \end{cases}
> $$

with

$$
k=m-n.
$$

Then

$$
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} e^{i(m-n)u}\,du=
\begin{cases}
0, & \text{if } m\neq n,\\[4pt]
2\pi, & \text{if } m=n.
\end{cases}
$$

Hence

$$
\int_{-p}^{p} e^{i\frac{m\pi}{p}x}e^{-i\frac{n\pi}{p}x}\,dx=
\begin{cases}
0, & \text{if } m\neq n,\\[4pt]
\frac{p}{\pi}(2\pi)=2p, & \text{if } m=n.
\end{cases}
$$

That is,

$$
\int_{-p}^{p} e^{i\frac{m\pi}{p}x}e^{-i\frac{n\pi}{p}x}\,dx=
\begin{cases}
0, & \text{if } m\neq n,\\[4pt]
2p, & \text{if } m=n.
\end{cases}
$$

##### problem 13(c)

Set

$$
u=\frac{\pi}{p}x,
\qquad
dx=\frac{p}{\pi}\,du.
$$

Then as $x$ runs from $-p$ to $p$, the variable $u$ runs from $-\pi$ to $\pi$. Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{-p}^{p} \cos\left(\frac{m\pi}{p}x\right)\cos\left(\frac{n\pi}{p}x\right)\,dx
&=\frac{p}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos(mu)\cos(nu)\,du,
\end{aligned}
$$

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{-p}^{p} \cos\left(\frac{m\pi}{p}x\right)\sin\left(\frac{n\pi}{p}x\right)\,dx
&=\frac{p}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos(mu)\sin(nu)\,du,
\end{aligned}
$$

and

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{-p}^{p} \sin\left(\frac{m\pi}{p}x\right)\sin\left(\frac{n\pi}{p}x\right)\,dx
&=\frac{p}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \sin(mu)\sin(nu)\,du.
\end{aligned}
$$

Now use the previously established identities on the interval $[-\pi,\pi]$:

> [!equation] Equation
> $$
> \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos(mu)\cos(nu)\,du=0
> \qquad (m\neq n),
> $$
> $$
> \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos(mu)\sin(nu)\,du=0
> \qquad \text{for all } m,n,
> $$
> $$
> \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \sin(mu)\sin(nu)\,du=0
> \qquad (m\neq n),
> $$
> $$
> \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos^2(mu)\,du=\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \sin^2(mu)\,du=\pi
> \qquad (m\neq 0).
> $$

If $m\neq n$, then

$$
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos(mu)\cos(nu)\,du=0
$$

and

$$
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \sin(mu)\sin(nu)\,du=0,
$$

so

$$
\int_{-p}^{p} \cos\left(\frac{m\pi}{p}x\right)\cos\left(\frac{n\pi}{p}x\right)\,dx=0
$$

and

$$
\int_{-p}^{p} \sin\left(\frac{m\pi}{p}x\right)\sin\left(\frac{n\pi}{p}x\right)\,dx=0.
$$

Also,

$$
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos(mu)\sin(nu)\,du=0
\qquad \text{for all } m,n,
$$

so

$$
\int_{-p}^{p} \cos\left(\frac{m\pi}{p}x\right)\sin\left(\frac{n\pi}{p}x\right)\,dx=0
\qquad \text{for all } m,n.
$$

Finally, if $m\neq 0$, then

$$
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos^2(mu)\,du=\pi
$$

and

$$
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \sin^2(mu)\,du=\pi,
$$

so

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{-p}^{p} \cos^2\left(\frac{m\pi}{p}x\right)\,dx
&=\frac{p}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos^2(mu)\,du \\
&=\frac{p}{\pi}\cdot \pi \\
&=p
\end{aligned}
$$

and

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{-p}^{p} \sin^2\left(\frac{m\pi}{p}x\right)\,dx
&=\frac{p}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \sin^2(mu)\,du \\
&=\frac{p}{\pi}\cdot \pi \\
&=p.
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus

$$
\int_{-p}^{p} \cos\left(\frac{m\pi}{p}x\right)\cos\left(\frac{n\pi}{p}x\right)\,dx=0
\qquad (m\neq n),
$$

$$
\int_{-p}^{p} \cos\left(\frac{m\pi}{p}x\right)\sin\left(\frac{n\pi}{p}x\right)\,dx=0
\qquad \text{for all } m,n,
$$

$$
\int_{-p}^{p} \sin\left(\frac{m\pi}{p}x\right)\sin\left(\frac{n\pi}{p}x\right)\,dx=0
\qquad (m\neq n),
$$

and

$$
\int_{-p}^{p} \cos^2\left(\frac{m\pi}{p}x\right)\,dx
=
\int_{-p}^{p} \sin^2\left(\frac{m\pi}{p}x\right)\,dx
=p
\qquad (m\neq 0).
$$

##### solution

##### problem 14

Consider

$$
\int \cos((a+ib)x)\,dx.
$$

On the one hand,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\cos((a+ib)x)
&=\cos(ax+ibx) \\
&=\cos(ax)\cos(ibx)-\sin(ax)\sin(ibx).
\end{aligned}
$$

Now

$$
\cos(ibx)=\cosh(bx)
$$

and

$$
\sin(ibx)=i\sinh(bx),
$$

so

$$
\begin{aligned}
\cos((a+ib)x)
&=\cos(ax)\cosh(bx)-\sin(ax)\bigl(i\sinh(bx)\bigr) \\
&=\cos(ax)\cosh(bx)-i\sin(ax)\sinh(bx).
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore

$$
\int \cos((a+ib)x)\,dx
=
\int \cos(ax)\cosh(bx)\,dx
-i\int \sin(ax)\sinh(bx)\,dx.
$$

On the other hand,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int \cos((a+ib)x)\,dx
&=\frac{\sin((a+ib)x)}{a+ib}+c.
\end{aligned}
$$

Now expand the numerator:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\sin((a+ib)x)
&=\sin(ax+ibx) \\
&=\sin(ax)\cos(ibx)+\cos(ax)\sin(ibx) \\
&=\sin(ax)\cosh(bx)+\cos(ax)\bigl(i\sinh(bx)\bigr) \\
&=\sin(ax)\cosh(bx)+i\cos(ax)\sinh(bx).
\end{aligned}
$$

Also,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{1}{a+ib}
&=\frac{1}{a+ib}\cdot \frac{a-ib}{a-ib} \\
&=\frac{a-ib}{(a+ib)(a-ib)} \\
&=\frac{a-ib}{a^2+b^2}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\sin((a+ib)x)}{a+ib}
&=\frac{a-ib}{a^2+b^2}\Bigl(\sin(ax)\cosh(bx)+i\cos(ax)\sinh(bx)\Bigr).
\end{aligned}
$$

Let

$$
S=\sin(ax)\cosh(bx),
\qquad
T=\cos(ax)\sinh(bx).
$$

Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
(a-ib)(S+iT)
&=aS+aiT-ibS-i^2bT \\
&=aS+aiT-ibS+bT \\
&=(aS+bT)+i(aT-bS).
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int \cos((a+ib)x)\,dx
&=\frac{1}{a^2+b^2}\Bigl((aS+bT)+i(aT-bS)\Bigr)+c \\
&=\frac{1}{a^2+b^2}\Bigl(a\sin(ax)\cosh(bx)+b\cos(ax)\sinh(bx)\Bigr) \\
&\quad+\frac{i}{a^2+b^2}\Bigl(a\cos(ax)\sinh(bx)-b\sin(ax)\cosh(bx)\Bigr)+c.
\end{aligned}
$$

Now compare real parts:

$$
\int \cos(ax)\cosh(bx)\,dx
=
\frac{1}{a^2+b^2}\Bigl(a\cosh(bx)\sin(ax)+b\cos(ax)\sinh(bx)\Bigr)+c.
$$

Next compare imaginary parts. Since

$$
\int \cos((a+ib)x)\,dx
=
\int \cos(ax)\cosh(bx)\,dx
-i\int \sin(ax)\sinh(bx)\,dx,
$$

the imaginary part on the left is

$$
-\int \sin(ax)\sinh(bx)\,dx.
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
-\int \sin(ax)\sinh(bx)\,dx
&=
\frac{1}{a^2+b^2}\Bigl(a\cos(ax)\sinh(bx)-b\sin(ax)\cosh(bx)\Bigr),
\end{aligned}
$$

so

$$
\int \sin(ax)\sinh(bx)\,dx
=
\frac{1}{a^2+b^2}\Bigl(b\cosh(bx)\sin(ax)-a\cos(ax)\sinh(bx)\Bigr)+c.
$$

This proves both formulas in Problem 14.

##### problem 15

Now consider

$$
\int \sin((a+ib)x)\,dx.
$$

On the one hand,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\sin((a+ib)x)
&=\sin(ax+ibx) \\
&=\sin(ax)\cos(ibx)+\cos(ax)\sin(ibx) \\
&=\sin(ax)\cosh(bx)+\cos(ax)\bigl(i\sinh(bx)\bigr) \\
&=\sin(ax)\cosh(bx)+i\cos(ax)\sinh(bx).
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore

$$
\int \sin((a+ib)x)\,dx
=
\int \sin(ax)\cosh(bx)\,dx
+i\int \cos(ax)\sinh(bx)\,dx.
$$

On the other hand,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int \sin((a+ib)x)\,dx
&=-\frac{\cos((a+ib)x)}{a+ib}+c.
\end{aligned}
$$

Using

$$
\cos((a+ib)x)=\cos(ax)\cosh(bx)-i\sin(ax)\sinh(bx)
$$

and

$$
\frac{1}{a+ib}=\frac{a-ib}{a^2+b^2},
$$

we get

$$
\begin{aligned}
-\frac{\cos((a+ib)x)}{a+ib}
&=-\frac{a-ib}{a^2+b^2}\Bigl(\cos(ax)\cosh(bx)-i\sin(ax)\sinh(bx)\Bigr).
\end{aligned}
$$

Let

$$
U=\cos(ax)\cosh(bx),
\qquad
V=\sin(ax)\sinh(bx).
$$

Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
(a-ib)(U-iV)
&=aU-aiV-ibU+i^2bV \\
&=aU-aiV-ibU-bV \\
&=(aU-bV)-i(aV+bU).
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
-\frac{\cos((a+ib)x)}{a+ib}
&=\frac{1}{a^2+b^2}\Bigl(-(aU-bV)+i(aV+bU)\Bigr) \\
&=\frac{1}{a^2+b^2}\Bigl(-a\cos(ax)\cosh(bx)+b\sin(ax)\sinh(bx)\Bigr) \\
&\quad+\frac{i}{a^2+b^2}\Bigl(a\sin(ax)\sinh(bx)+b\cos(ax)\cosh(bx)\Bigr).
\end{aligned}
$$

Now compare real parts:

$$
\int \sin(ax)\cosh(bx)\,dx
=
\frac{1}{a^2+b^2}\Bigl(-a\cos(ax)\cosh(bx)+b\sin(ax)\sinh(bx)\Bigr)+c.
$$

Next compare imaginary parts:

$$
\int \cos(ax)\sinh(bx)\,dx
=
\frac{1}{a^2+b^2}\Bigl(b\cos(ax)\cosh(bx)+a\sin(ax)\sinh(bx)\Bigr)+c.
$$

This proves both formulas in Problem 15.

##### problem 17

By additivity of path integrals,

$$
\int_{[z_1,z_2,z_3]} 2\bar z\,dz
=
\int_{[z_1,z_2]} 2\bar z\,dz+\int_{[z_2,z_3]} 2\bar z\,dz.
$$

For the segment from $z_1=1$ to $z_2=i$, use

$$
\gamma_1(t)=1+t(i-1)=1-t+it,\qquad 0\le t\le 1.
$$

Then

$$
\overline{\gamma_1(t)}=1-t-it
$$

and

$$
\gamma_1'(t)=i-1.
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{[z_1,z_2]} 2\bar z\,dz
&=2\int_0^1 \overline{\gamma_1(t)}\,\gamma_1'(t)\,dt \\
&=2\int_0^1 (1-t-it)(i-1)\,dt.
\end{aligned}
$$

From the earlier computation of the same segment with integrand $\bar z\,dz$, we have

> [!equation] Equation
> $$
> \int_{[1,i]} \bar z\,dz=i.
> $$

Hence

$$
\int_{[z_1,z_2]} 2\bar z\,dz=2i.
$$

For the segment from $z_2=i$ to $z_3=1+i$, use

$$
\gamma_2(t)=i+t,\qquad 0\le t\le 1.
$$

Then

$$
\overline{\gamma_2(t)}=t-i
$$

and

$$
\gamma_2'(t)=1.
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{[z_2,z_3]} 2\bar z\,dz
&=2\int_0^1 (t-i)\,dt \\
&=2\left[\frac{t^2}{2}-it\right]_0^1 \\
&=2\left(\frac12-i\right) \\
&=1-2i.
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{[z_1,z_2,z_3]} 2\bar z\,dz
&=2i+(1-2i) \\
&=1.
\end{aligned}
$$

$$

##### problem 30

Here

$$
\gamma(t)=2t+\frac{2i}{3}t^{3/2},
\qquad

Differentiate:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\gamma'(t)
&=\frac{d}{dt}\left(2t+\frac{2i}{3}t^{3/2}\right) \\
&=2+\frac{2i}{3}\cdot \frac{3}{2}t^{1/2} \\
&=2+it^{1/2}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
|\gamma'(t)|
&=\left|2+it^{1/2}\right| \\
&=\sqrt{2^2+\left(t^{1/2}\right)^2} \\
&=\sqrt{4+t}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence

$$
\begin{aligned}
l(\gamma)
&=\int_1^2 \sqrt{4+t}\,dt \\
&=\int_1^2 (4+t)^{1/2}\,dt \\
&=\left[\frac{2}{3}(4+t)^{3/2}\right]_1^2 \\

\end{aligned}

##### problem 31

Here

$$
\gamma(t)=\frac{1}{5}t^5+\frac{i}{4}t^4,
\qquad
0\le t\le 1.
$$

Differentiate:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\gamma'(t)
&=\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{1}{5}t^5+\frac{i}{4}t^4\right) \\
&=t^4+it^3.
\end{aligned}

$$
\begin{aligned}

&=t^3\sqrt{t^2+1},
\end{aligned}

because $t\ge 0$ on $[0,1]$.

\begin{aligned}

> [!exercise] Exercise 18
> In Exercises 34-39, derive the given integral estimate.
> 34. $\left|\int_{\gamma} e^{z} d z\right| \leq \frac{\pi}{4}$, where $\gamma(t)=e^{i t}, \frac{\pi}{2} \leq t \leq \frac{3 \pi}{4}$.

##### solution

In each case we use the ML-inequality

> [!equation] Equation
> $$
> \left|\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz\right|\le Ml(\gamma),
> $$

where $M$ is any upper bound for $|f(z)|$ on the contour $\gamma$.

##### problem 34

Here

$$
\gamma(t)=e^{it},
\qquad
\frac{\pi}{2}\le t\le \frac{3\pi}{4}.
$$

The contour is an arc of the unit circle, so its length is

$$
l(\gamma)=\frac{3\pi}{4}-\frac{\pi}{2}=\frac{\pi}{4}.
$$

For $z=e^{it}$ on this arc,

$$
\begin{aligned}
|e^z|
&=|e^{e^{it}}| \\
&=e^{\operatorname{Re}(e^{it})} \\
&=e^{\cos t}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Since

$$
\frac{\pi}{2}\le t\le \frac{3\pi}{4},
$$

we have

$$
\cos t\le 0,
$$

so

$$
|e^z|=e^{\cos t}\le 1.
$$

Thus we may take

$$
M=1.
$$

Therefore

$$
\left|\int_\gamma e^z\,dz\right|
\le Ml(\gamma)
=1\cdot \frac{\pi}{4}
=\frac{\pi}{4}.
$$

##### problem 35

On the unit circle $C_1(0)$, we have

$$
|z|=1.
$$

Hence

$$
\log z=\ln|z|+i\operatorname{Arg}(z)=i\operatorname{Arg}(z),
$$

so

$$
|\log z|=|\operatorname{Arg}(z)|.
$$

For the principal argument,

$$
|\operatorname{Arg}(z)|\le \pi,
$$

so

$$
|\log z|\le \pi.
$$

Thus we may take

$$
M=\pi.
$$

Also, the length of the unit circle is

$$
l(C_1(0))=2\pi.
$$

Therefore

$$
\left|\int_{C_1(0)} \log z\,dz\right|
\le Ml(C_1(0))
=\pi(2\pi)
=2\pi^2.
$$

##### problem 36

$$
|z|=2.
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
|z-1|
&\ge ||z|-|1|| \\
&=|2-1| \\
&=1.
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence

$$
\left|\frac{1}{z-1}\right|=\frac{1}{|z-1|}\le 1.
$$

Thus we may take

$$
M=1.
$$

Also,

$$
l(C_2(0))=2\pi(2)=4\pi.
$$

Therefore

$$
\left|\int_{C_2(0)} \frac{1}{z-1}\,dz\right|
\le Ml(C_2(0))
=1\cdot 4\pi
=4\pi.
$$

##### problem 37

The contour is the triangle with vertices

$$

Its three side lengths are

$$
\begin{aligned}
|z_2-z_1|
&=|(1-i)-(-1-i)| \\
&=|2| \\

\end{aligned}

$$
\begin{aligned}

&=|i-(1-i)| \\
&=|-1+2i| \\
&=\sqrt{(-1)^2+2^2} \\
&=\sqrt5,
\end{aligned}
$$

and

$$
\begin{aligned}
|z_1-z_3|

&=|-1-2i| \\
&=\sqrt{(-1)^2+(-2)^2} \\
&=\sqrt5.
\end{aligned}

Therefore the length of the contour is

$$
l(\gamma)=2+\sqrt5+\sqrt5=2+2\sqrt5.

$$
\left|z^{-5}\right|=\frac{1}{|z|^5}.
$$

Also, every point on the contour satisfies

Indeed, on the bottom segment from $-1-i$ to $1-i$ we have

|z|=\sqrt{x^2+1}\ge 1,

and on the other two segments the endpoints already have modulus $\sqrt2,\sqrt2,\text{ and }1$, so the contour never enters the open unit disk. Therefore

$$

for every point on the contour. Thus we may take

$$

Hence

$$
\left|\int_{[z_1,z_2,z_3,z_1]} z^{-5}\,dz\right|
\le Ml(\gamma)
=5^{5/2}(2+2\sqrt5).

> [!exercise] Exercise 19
> 40. Let $f(z)$ be a continuous function on a region $\Omega$, and let $\gamma(t)$ be a path in $\Omega$ that has degenerated into a point; that is, $\gamma(t)=z_{0}, a \leq t \leq b$. Show that $\int_{\gamma} f(z) d z=0$.
> 41. Let $f(z)$ be a continuous function on a region $\Omega$, and let $\gamma$ be a path in $\Omega$. Let $\Gamma=(\gamma,-\gamma)$, that is, $\gamma$ followed by its reverse. Show that $\int_{\Gamma} f(z) d z=0$.



##### solution

##### problem 40

If the path has degenerated into the single point $z_0$, then

$$
\gamma(t)=z_0
\qquad \text{for all } t\in [a,b].
$$

Therefore

$$
\gamma'(t)=0
\qquad \text{for all } t\in [a,b].
$$

By the definition of the path integral,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz
&=\int_a^b f(\gamma(t))\gamma'(t)\,dt \\
&=\int_a^b f(z_0)\cdot 0\,dt \\
&=\int_a^b 0\,dt \\
&=0.
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence

$$
\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz=0.
$$

##### problem 41

Let $\Gamma=(\gamma,-\gamma)$, that is, the contour obtained by first traversing $\gamma$ and then traversing its reverse.

By additivity of the path integral over concatenated contours,

$$
\int_\Gamma f(z)\,dz=\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz+\int_{-\gamma} f(z)\,dz.
$$

Now use the path-reversal property

> [!equation] Equation
> $$
> \int_{-\gamma} f(z)\,dz=-\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz.
> $$

Substituting this into the previous line gives

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_\Gamma f(z)\,dz
&=\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz+\left(-\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz\right) \\
&=0.
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore

$$
\int_\Gamma f(z)\,dz=0.
$$

> [!exercise] Exercise 20
> 42. Project Problem: Additivity over paths. In this exercise, we prove part (iii) of Proposition 2.
> (a) We have $\Gamma=\left(\gamma_{1}, \gamma_{2}, \ldots, \gamma_{m}\right)$. Let us suppose each $\gamma_{k}(t)$ maps the interval $[0,1]$ to $\gamma_{k}$. Show that we can parametrize $\Gamma$ over the interval $[0, m]$ by defining $\Gamma(t)$ piecewise as
> 
> $$
> \Gamma(t)=\gamma_{k}(t+1-k) \text { for } k-1 \leq t \leq k
> $$
> 
> (b) Justify the following steps:
> 
> $$
> \begin{aligned}
> \int_{\Gamma} f(z) d z & =\int_{0}^{m} f(\Gamma(t)) \Gamma^{\prime}(t) d t=\sum_{k=1}^{m} \int_{k-1}^{k} f(\Gamma(t)) \Gamma^{\prime}(t) d t \\
> & =\sum_{k=1}^{m} \int_{0}^{1} f\left(\gamma_{k}(T)\right) \gamma_{k}^{\prime}(T) d T \text { **(Hint: Let } T=t+1-k \text { and use (33))** } \\
> & =\sum_{k=1}^{m} \int_{\gamma_{k}} f(z) d z .
> \end{aligned}
> $$
> 


##### solution

##### problem 42(a)

Assume that each path $\gamma_k$ is parametrized over $[0,1]$. Define a function $\Gamma$ on $[0,m]$ by

$$
\Gamma(t)=\gamma_k(t+1-k)
\qquad \text{for } k-1\le t\le k.
$$

We check that this traces the contour

$$
\Gamma=(\gamma_1,\gamma_2,\ldots,\gamma_m).
$$

If $0\le t\le 1$, then $k=1$, so

$$
\Gamma(t)=\gamma_1(t+1-1)=\gamma_1(t).
$$

Thus on the first subinterval $[0,1]$, the function $\Gamma$ traces $\gamma_1$.

If $1\le t\le 2$, then $k=2$, so

$$
\Gamma(t)=\gamma_2(t+1-2)=\gamma_2(t-1).
$$

As $t$ runs from $1$ to $2$, the quantity $t-1$ runs from $0$ to $1$, so on the second subinterval $[1,2]$, the function $\Gamma$ traces $\gamma_2$.

More generally, if

$$
k-1\le t\le k,
$$

then

$$
0\le t+1-k\le 1,
$$

so $\Gamma(t)=\gamma_k(t+1-k)$ traces the path $\gamma_k$ exactly once as $t$ runs from $k-1$ to $k$.

We must also check that the parametrization is continuous at the junction points. If $k=1,2,\ldots,m-1$, then at the point $t=k$ we have

$$
\Gamma(k)=\gamma_k(k+1-k)=\gamma_k(1)
$$

when we compute $\Gamma(k)$ from the $k$th piece, and

$$
\Gamma(k)=\gamma_{k+1}(k+1-(k+1))=\gamma_{k+1}(0)
$$

when we compute $\Gamma(k)$ from the $(k+1)$st piece.

Because the contour $(\gamma_1,\gamma_2,\ldots,\gamma_m)$ is formed by following $\gamma_k$ and then $\gamma_{k+1}$, the terminal point of $\gamma_k$ is the initial point of $\gamma_{k+1}$. Therefore

$$
\gamma_k(1)=\gamma_{k+1}(0).
$$

Hence the two formulas for $\Gamma(k)$ agree, so the piecewise definition is continuous at each joining point.

Therefore $\Gamma$ traces $\gamma_1$ on $[0,1]$, then $\gamma_2$ on $[1,2]$, and so on, finally tracing $\gamma_m$ on $[m-1,m]$. Hence $\Gamma$ is a continuous parametrization of the contour

$$
(\gamma_1,\gamma_2,\ldots,\gamma_m)
$$

over the interval $[0,m]$.

##### problem 42(b)

By definition of the path integral,

$$
\int_\Gamma f(z)\,dz=\int_0^m f(\Gamma(t))\Gamma'(t)\,dt.
$$

Now use additivity of the Riemann integral over adjacent intervals:

> [!equation] Equation
> $$
> \int_a^c h(t)\,dt=\int_a^b h(t)\,dt+\int_b^c h(t)\,dt
> \qquad (a\le b\le c).
> $$

First split the interval $[0,m]$ at the point $1$:

$$
\int_0^m f(\Gamma(t))\Gamma'(t)\,dt
=
\int_0^1 f(\Gamma(t))\Gamma'(t)\,dt
+
\int_1^m f(\Gamma(t))\Gamma'(t)\,dt.
$$

Next split the second integral at the point $2$:

$$
\int_1^m f(\Gamma(t))\Gamma'(t)\,dt
=
\int_1^2 f(\Gamma(t))\Gamma'(t)\,dt
+
\int_2^m f(\Gamma(t))\Gamma'(t)\,dt.
$$

Continuing in this way, we obtain

$$
\int_0^m f(\Gamma(t))\Gamma'(t)\,dt
=
\sum_{k=1}^m \int_{k-1}^k f(\Gamma(t))\Gamma'(t)\,dt.
$$

Thus

$$
\int_\Gamma f(z)\,dz
=
\sum_{k=1}^m \int_{k-1}^k f(\Gamma(t))\Gamma'(t)\,dt.
$$

Now fix one value of $k$. On the interval $k-1\le t\le k$, we have

$$
\Gamma(t)=\gamma_k(t+1-k).
$$

Let

$$
T=t+1-k.
$$

Then

$$
dT=dt.
$$

Also,

$$
\frac{d}{dt}(t+1-k)=1.
$$

Also, when $t=k-1$, we have

$$
T=(k-1)+1-k=0,
$$

and when $t=k$, we have

$$
T=k+1-k=1.
$$

By the chain rule,

$$
\Gamma'(t)
=\frac{d}{dt}\gamma_k(t+1-k)
=\gamma_k'(t+1-k)\frac{d}{dt}(t+1-k)
=\gamma_k'(t+1-k)\cdot 1
=\gamma_k'(t+1-k).
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{k-1}^k f(\Gamma(t))\Gamma'(t)\,dt
&=\int_{k-1}^k f(\gamma_k(t+1-k))\gamma_k'(t+1-k)\,dt \\
&=\int_0^1 f(\gamma_k(T))\gamma_k'(T)\,dT.
\end{aligned}
$$

Substitute this into the sum:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_\Gamma f(z)\,dz
&=\sum_{k=1}^m \int_{k-1}^k f(\Gamma(t))\Gamma'(t)\,dt \\
&=\sum_{k=1}^m \int_0^1 f(\gamma_k(T))\gamma_k'(T)\,dT.
\end{aligned}
$$

Finally, by the definition of the path integral for each $\gamma_k$,

$$
\int_{\gamma_k} f(z)\,dz=\int_0^1 f(\gamma_k(T))\gamma_k'(T)\,dT.
$$

Hence

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_\Gamma f(z)\,dz
&=\sum_{k=1}^m \int_{\gamma_k} f(z)\,dz.
\end{aligned}
$$

This proves part (iii) of Proposition 2.
