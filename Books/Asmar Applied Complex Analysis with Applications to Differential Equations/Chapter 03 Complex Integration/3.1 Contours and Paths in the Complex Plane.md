Having studied the derivative in Chapter 2, our next step is to study the integral of a function of a complex variable. If $f(z)$ is defined on a subset $\Omega$ of $\mathbb{C}$ and $z_{1}$ and $z_{2}$ are points in $\Omega$, how can we define the integral of $f$ from $z_{1}$ to $z_{2}$ ? First we must define the notion of path $\gamma$ in $\Omega$ from $z_{1}$ to $z_{2}$ (Section 3.1); then we will define the integral over this path (Section 3.2), denoted by $\int_{\gamma} f(z) d z$. In Section 3.2, an expression of this integral is given in terms of the usual Riemann integral from calculus, but applied to complex-valued functions on an interval $[a, b]$.

The most important question in this chapter concerns the independence of path: When does $\int_{\gamma} f(z) d z$ depend only on $z_{1}$ and $z_{2}$ and not on $\gamma$ ? The answer to this question is provided by Cauchy's theorem (Theorem 2, Section 3.4). Cauchy's theorem is to complex analysis what the fundamental theorem of calculus is to calculus. The discussion of Cauchy's theorem in Section 3.4 will lead us to important topics such as simple connectedness of regions in $\mathbb{C}$. We will derive very useful versions of Cauchy's theorem for simply and multiply connected regions.

The proof of Cauchy's theorem is nontrivial. It will be presented in complete detail in Section 3.5.

In Sections 3.6 and 3.7 we start the applications of Cauchy's theorem and derive Cauchy's generalized integral formula, which is the basis for all the applications that will follow. ##### problem 1

The parametric form of a curve is a representation of the coordinates of points on the curve as functions of a real parameter $t$. In the plane, one writes

$$
x=x(t), \qquad y=y(t),
$$

for $t$ in some interval

$$
a\le t\le b.
$$

Each value of $t$ determines one point on the curve, namely

$$
(x(t),y(t)).
$$

Thus a curve is traced by allowing $t$ to vary over an interval of real numbers, usually a closed interval $[a,b]$.

##### problem 2(a)

For the upper semicircle

$$
y=\sqrt{1-x^2},\qquad -1\le x\le 1,
$$

a standard parametrization is

$$
x=\cos t,\qquad y=\sin t,\qquad 0\le t\le \pi.
$$

Indeed,

$$
x^2+y^2=\cos^2 t+\sin^2 t=1,
$$

and since

$$
0\le t\le \pi,
$$

we have

$$
\sin t\ge 0,
$$

so the point lies on the upper semicircle.

By convention, a curve is referred to by its parametrization. This convention is adopted because the same geometric set of points may be traced in different ways, with different starting points, speeds, and orientations. The parametrization records not only the set of points but also how the curve is traversed.

> [!exercise] parametrization of the upper semicircle
> 
> 1. Find a parametrization of the upper semicircle $y=\sqrt{1-x^2},-1 \leq x \leq 1$, in complex notation. Identify the initial point, terminal point, and orientation.


##### solution

For the upper semicircle $y=\sqrt{1-x^2}$, $-1\le x\le 1$, a standard parametrization is

$$
x=\cos t,\qquad y=\sin t,\qquad 0\le t\le \pi.
$$

In complex notation, this becomes

$$
\gamma(t)=x(t)+iy(t)=\cos t+i\sin t=e^{it},
\qquad
0\le t\le \pi.
$$

To check that this really parametrizes the upper semicircle, note first that $x(t)=\cos t$, so because $0\le t\le \pi$, we have $-1\le \cos t\le 1$, hence $-1\le x(t)\le 1$.

Also,

$$
\begin{aligned}
1-x(t)^2
&=1-\cos^2 t \\
&=\sin^2 t.
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore $\sqrt{1-x(t)^2}=\sqrt{\sin^2 t}=|\sin t|$. Since $0\le t\le \pi$, we have $\sin t\ge 0$, so $|\sin t|=\sin t$. Because $y(t)=\sin t$, it follows that $y(t)=\sqrt{1-x(t)^2}$. Thus the point $(x(t),y(t))$ lies on the curve $y=\sqrt{1-x^2}$ with $-1\le x\le 1$.

The initial point is $\gamma(0)=e^{i\cdot 0}=e^0=1$, and the terminal point is $\gamma(\pi)=e^{i\pi}=-1$.

To describe the orientation, observe that $\gamma(0)=1$, $\gamma\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right)=e^{i\pi/2}=i$, and $\gamma(\pi)=-1$. So as $t$ increases from $0$ to $\pi$, the point moves from $1$ to $i$ to $-1$, which is motion along the upper semicircle in the counterclockwise direction.

Therefore the orientation is counterclockwise, that is, positive orientation.

> [!exercise] multiple parametrizations of $[ 0,1 ]$
> 
> 1. Verify that $\gamma_1(t)=t$ and $\gamma_2(t)=t^2$, both for $0 \leq t \leq 1$, parametrize the same directed segment $[0,1] \subset \mathbb{R}$. Identify the initial and terminal points of each, and describe in what sense the two parametrizations differ even though they represent the same curve.


##### solution

For

$$
\gamma_1(t)=t,
\qquad
0\le t\le 1,
$$

we have $\gamma_1(t)\in [0,1]$ for every $t\in [0,1]$, and as $t$ runs from $0$ to $1$, the value of $\gamma_1(t)$ runs through every point of the interval $[0,1]$ exactly once.

For

$$
\gamma_2(t)=t^2,
\qquad
0\le t\le 1,
$$

we again have $\gamma_2(t)\in [0,1]$ for every $t\in [0,1]$, because squaring a number in $[0,1]$ keeps it in $[0,1]$. Also, if $x\in [0,1]$, then

$$
t=\sqrt{x}
$$

satisfies $0\le t\le 1$, and

$$
\gamma_2(t)=t^2=(\sqrt{x})^2=x.
$$

So $\gamma_2$ also traces every point of the interval $[0,1]$.

Thus both $\gamma_1$ and $\gamma_2$ parametrize the same directed segment $[0,1]\subset \mathbb{R}$.

For $\gamma_1$, the initial point is

$$
\gamma_1(0)=0
$$

and the terminal point is

$$
\gamma_1(1)=1.
$$

For $\gamma_2$, the initial point is

$$
\gamma_2(0)=0
$$

and the terminal point is

$$
\gamma_2(1)=1.
$$

So both parametrizations have the same initial point and the same terminal point, and both move from $0$ to $1$.

They differ in the way the interval is traced. The parametrization $\gamma_1(t)=t$ moves at constant speed, since

$$
\gamma_1'(t)=1.
$$

The parametrization $\gamma_2(t)=t^2$ does not move at constant speed, since

$$
\gamma_2'(t)=2t.
$$

At the beginning, when $t=0$, the speed is $0$, and as $t$ increases the speed increases. So $\gamma_2$ starts more slowly than $\gamma_1$ and then speeds up, even though both represent the same directed curve.

A parametric form of a curve is thus a representation of the curve by a pair of equations: $x=x(t)$ and $y=y(t)$, where $t$ ranges over a set of real numbers, usually a closed interval $[a, b]$ (see _Figure 2_). Each value of $t$ determines a point $\gamma(t)=(x(t), y(t))$, which traces the curve as $t$ varies.

A curve may have more than one parametrization. For example, the interval $[0,1]$ can be parametrized as $\gamma_{1}(t)=t, 0 \leq t \leq 1$ or $\gamma_{2}(t)=t^{2}, 0 \leq t \leq 1$. Both $\gamma_{1}$ and $\gamma_{2}$ represent the same curve. In our analysis, we will always choose and work with a specific parametrization of the curve. For that reason, it will be convenient to refer to a curve by its parametrization $\gamma(t)$ or simply $\gamma$, even though the curve may have more than one parametrization.

Let $\gamma(t), a \leq t \leq b$, be a parametrization of a curve. As $t$ varies from $a$ to $b$ the point $\gamma(t)$ traces the curve in a specific direction, starting with $\gamma(a)$, the initial point of $\gamma$, and ending at $\gamma(b)$, the terminal point of $\gamma$. For continuous curves, this direction is usually denoted by an arrow on the curve. A curve is closed if $\gamma(a)=\gamma(b)$. For circles and circular arcs, if the arrow points in the counterclockwise direction, we will say that the curve has positive orientation. Curves traversed in the clockwise direction have negative orientation (see _Figure 3_).

```python
import math
from pathlib import Path

import numpy as np
from PIL import Image, ImageDraw, ImageFont

W, H = 980, 980
M = 110
BG = "white"
AX = (0, 0, 0)
BLUE = (44, 103, 176)
GREEN = (46, 125, 50)
RED = (190, 45, 45)
BLACK = (45, 45, 45)

outdir = Path("images")
outdir.mkdir(exist_ok=True)

font = ImageFont.truetype(
    "/System/Library/Fonts/Supplemental/Times New Roman.ttf", 30
)
small = ImageFont.truetype(
    "/System/Library/Fonts/Supplemental/Times New Roman.ttf", 24
)


def make_canvas(xmin, xmax, ymin, ymax):
    image = Image.new("RGB", (W, H), BG)
    draw = ImageDraw.Draw(image)

    def pt(x, y):
        px = M + (x - xmin) / (xmax - xmin) * (W - 2 * M)
        py = H - M - (y - ymin) / (ymax - ymin) * (H - 2 * M)
        return px, py

    if xmin <= 0 <= xmax:
        draw.line([pt(0, ymin), pt(0, ymax)], fill=AX, width=3)
    if ymin <= 0 <= ymax:
        draw.line([pt(xmin, 0), pt(xmax, 0)], fill=AX, width=3)

    draw.text((W - M + 20, H - M - 10), "x", fill=AX, font=font)
    draw.text((M - 18, M - 55), "y", fill=AX, font=font)
    return image, draw, pt


def draw_arrowhead(draw, p_prev, p_tip, color, head=20,
                   half_angle=math.pi / 7):
    dx = p_tip[0] - p_prev[0]
    dy = p_tip[1] - p_prev[1]
    angle = math.atan2(dy, dx)
    left = (
        p_tip[0] - head * math.cos(angle - half_angle),
        p_tip[1] - head * math.sin(angle - half_angle),
    )
    right = (
        p_tip[0] - head * math.cos(angle + half_angle),
        p_tip[1] - head * math.sin(angle + half_angle),
    )
    draw.polygon([p_tip, left, right], fill=color)


def draw_point(draw, p, color, r=10):
    x, y = p
    draw.ellipse((x - r, y - r, x + r, y + r), fill=color, outline=color)


def add_arrowheads(draw, points, positions, color):
    n = len(points)
    for fraction in positions:
        i = max(1, min(n - 1, int(fraction * (n - 1))))
        draw_arrowhead(draw, points[i - 1], points[i], color)


image, draw, pt = make_canvas(-1.15, 1.15, -0.05, 1.15)
t = np.linspace(np.pi / 6, 2 * np.pi / 3, 500)
curve = [pt(float(np.cos(s)), float(np.sin(s))) for s in t]
draw.line(curve, fill=BLUE, width=7)
add_arrowheads(draw, curve, [0.32, 0.62], BLUE)

z_initial = np.exp(1j * np.pi / 6)
z_terminal = np.exp(1j * 2 * np.pi / 3)
draw_point(draw, pt(z_initial.real, z_initial.imag), GREEN)
draw_point(draw, pt(z_terminal.real, z_terminal.imag), RED)

draw.text(
    (pt(z_initial.real, z_initial.imag)[0] + 18,
     pt(z_initial.real, z_initial.imag)[1] + 10),
    "start",
    fill=GREEN,
    font=small,
)
draw.text(
    (pt(z_terminal.real, z_terminal.imag)[0] - 80,
     pt(z_terminal.real, z_terminal.imag)[1] - 36),
    "end",
    fill=RED,
    font=small,
)

image.save(outdir / "exercise1_problem1_python.png")
```

> [!exercise] Exercise 2: A hypotrochoid in complex notation
> A hypotrochoid is a curve given in parametric form by
> 
> $$
> x(t)=a \cos t+b \cos \left(\frac{a t}{2}\right), \quad y(t)=a \sin t-b \sin \left(\frac{a t}{2}\right) .

##### solution

Write the hypotrochoid in complex notation as

$$
z(t)=x(t)+iy(t).
$$

Substituting the given formulas for $x(t)$ and $y(t)$ gives

$$
\begin{aligned}
z(t)
&=a\cos t+b\cos\left(\frac{at}{2}\right)

$\cos \theta+i\sin \theta=e^{i\theta}$ and
$\cos \theta-i\sin \theta=e^{-i\theta}$:

$$
z(t)=ae^{it}+be^{-iat/2}.
$$

Therefore the equation of the hypotrochoid in complex notation is

$$
z(t)=ae^{it}+be^{-iat/2}.
$$

> [!review]
> How can a curve $\gamma:[a, b] \rightarrow \mathbb{C}$ be reparametrized so that the same set of points is traced in the opposite direction, using a parametrization still defined on $[a, b]$ ?



##### solution

To trace the same curve in the opposite direction while keeping the parameter interval $[a,b]$, define

$$
\gamma_r(t)=\gamma(a+b-t),
\qquad
a\le t\le b.
$$

This works because as $t$ increases from $a$ to $b$, the quantity $a+b-t$ decreases from $b$ to $a$. Indeed,
$a+b-a=b$ and $a+b-b=a$, so the parameter inside $\gamma$ runs through the original interval in reverse order.

The endpoints are therefore reversed:
$\gamma_r(a)=\gamma(b)$ and $\gamma_r(b)=\gamma(a)$.

Moreover, the set of points traced by $\gamma_r$ is the same as the set of points traced by $\gamma$, because $\gamma_r(t)$ is obtained by evaluating $\gamma$ at the same parameter values, only in the opposite order.

Thus $\gamma_r(t)=\gamma(a+b-t)$ is a reparametrization of $\gamma$ that traces the same curve in the opposite direction.

> [!exercise]
> Given the directed line segment $\left[z_1, z_2\right]$ parametrized by $\gamma(t)=(1-t) z_1+t z_2$ for $0 \leq t \leq$ 1 , compute its reverse $\gamma_r$ using the general formula $\gamma_r(t)=\gamma(b+a-t)$, and verify that $\gamma_r$ traces the segment from $z_2$ to $z_1$.



##### solution

Here the original parametrization is

$$
\gamma(t)=(1-t)z_1+t z_2,
\qquad
0\le t\le 1.
$$

Since the interval is $[0,1]$, we have $a=0$ and $b=1$. Therefore the general reverse-path formula gives

$$
\gamma_r(t)=\gamma(b+a-t)=\gamma(1-t).
$$

Now substitute $1-t$ into the formula for $\gamma$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\gamma_r(t)
&=\gamma(1-t) \\
&=\bigl(1-(1-t)\bigr)z_1+(1-t)z_2 \\
&=t z_1+(1-t)z_2.
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus the reverse parametrization is

$$
\gamma_r(t)=t z_1+(1-t)z_2,
\qquad
0\le t\le 1.
$$

To verify that this traces the segment from $z_2$ to $z_1$, evaluate the endpoints:
$\gamma_r(0)=z_2$ and $\gamma_r(1)=z_1$.

So $\gamma_r$ traces the same line segment, but it starts at $z_2$ and ends at $z_1$. Hence it is the reverse of the directed segment $\left[z_1,z_2\right]$.

If $\gamma(t)$ is a curve parametrized by the interval $[a, b]$, the reverse of $\gamma$ is the curve $\gamma_{r}(t)$ (sometimes written $-\gamma$ ) parametrized by the same interval $[a, b]$ and given by

$$
\gamma_{r}(t)=\gamma(b+a-t) .
$$

The reverse of $\gamma$ traces the same set of points as $\gamma$ but in the opposite direction, starting with $\gamma_{r}(a)=\gamma(b)$ and ending with $\gamma_{r}(b)=\gamma(a)$.

The reverse of the directed line segment $\left[z_{1}, z_{2}\right]$ in Example 1(d) is clearly the line segment $\left[z_{2}, z_{1}\right]$ with equation

$$
\gamma_{r}(t)=\gamma(1-t)=t z_{1}+(1-t) z_{2}, \quad 0 \leq t \leq 1 .
$$

The curves in Example 1 are all continuous, as can be seen from their graphs. In fact, these curves have differentiability properties that will be needed when studying integrals along curves. Recall that a curve is given as the graph of a complex-valued function of a real variable. So, in order to study their differentiability properties, we next investigate the derivative of a complex-valued function of a real variable (not to be confused with the derivative of a complex-valued function of a complex variable, $f^{\prime}(z)$ ).




# 3.1.1 Complex-Valued Functions of a Real Variable


> [!review]
> 1.) How is the derivative of a complex-valued function $f(t)=u(t)+i v(t)$ of a real variable defined, and under what conditions on $u$ and $v$ does this derivative exist? Express $f^{\prime}(t)$ in terms of $u^{\prime}$ and $v^{\prime}$, and prove your answer.
> 2.) What are the rules for differentiating linear combinations (with complex-scalar coefficients), products, quotients, and compositions of complex-valued functions of a real variable? Prove each rule.



##### solution

##### problem 1

Let

$$
f(t)=u(t)+iv(t),
$$

where $u$ and $v$ are real-valued functions of the real variable $t$. The derivative of $f$ is defined by

$$
f'(t)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(t+h)-f(t)}{h},
$$

provided this limit exists.

Now compute the difference quotient:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{f(t+h)-f(t)}{h}
&=\frac{u(t+h)+iv(t+h)-u(t)-iv(t)}{h} \\
&=\frac{u(t+h)-u(t)}{h}
+i\frac{v(t+h)-v(t)}{h}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Suppose first that $u'(t)$ and $v'(t)$ both exist. Then taking limits as $h\to 0$ gives

$$
\begin{aligned}
f'(t)
&=\lim_{h\to 0}\left(
\frac{u(t+h)-u(t)}{h}
+i\frac{v(t+h)-v(t)}{h}
\right) \\
&=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{u(t+h)-u(t)}{h}
+i\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{v(t+h)-v(t)}{h} \\
&=u'(t)+iv'(t).
\end{aligned}
$$

So if $u'(t)$ and $v'(t)$ exist, then $f'(t)$ exists and
$f'(t)=u'(t)+iv'(t)$.

Conversely, suppose that $f'(t)$ exists. Then

$$
\frac{f(t+h)-f(t)}{h}
=
\frac{u(t+h)-u(t)}{h}
+i\frac{v(t+h)-v(t)}{h}
$$

has a limit as $h\to 0$. Two complex numbers are equal if and only if their real parts are equal and their imaginary parts are equal, so the real part and imaginary part of this quotient must each have limits. Therefore

$$
\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{u(t+h)-u(t)}{h}
\quad\text{and}\quad
\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{v(t+h)-v(t)}{h}
$$

both exist. That is, $u'(t)$ and $v'(t)$ both exist. Moreover, the same identity shows that

$$
f'(t)=u'(t)+iv'(t).
$$

Thus $f'(t)$ exists if and only if $u'(t)$ and $v'(t)$ both exist, and in that case

$$
f'(t)=u'(t)+iv'(t).
$$

##### problem 2

Let $f$ and $g$ be complex-valued differentiable functions of the real variable $t$, and let $\alpha$ and $\beta$ be complex constants.

For linear combinations, start from the definition:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{d}{dt}\bigl(\alpha f(t)+\beta g(t)\bigr)
&=\lim_{h\to 0}
\frac{\alpha f(t+h)+\beta g(t+h)-\alpha f(t)-\beta g(t)}{h} \\
&=\lim_{h\to 0}
\left(
\alpha\frac{f(t+h)-f(t)}{h}
+\beta\frac{g(t+h)-g(t)}{h}
\right) \\
&=\alpha\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(t+h)-f(t)}{h}
+\beta\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{g(t+h)-g(t)}{h} \\
&=\alpha f'(t)+\beta g'(t).
\end{aligned}
$$

So

$$
\bigl(\alpha f(t)+\beta g(t)\bigr)'=\alpha f'(t)+\beta g'(t).
$$

For products, write

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{d}{dt}\bigl(f(t)g(t)\bigr)
&=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(t+h)g(t+h)-f(t)g(t)}{h}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Add and subtract $f(t+h)g(t)$ inside the numerator:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{f(t+h)g(t+h)-f(t)g(t)}{h}
&=\frac{f(t+h)g(t+h)-f(t+h)g(t)}{h} \\
&\qquad
+\frac{f(t+h)g(t)-f(t)g(t)}{h} \\
&=f(t+h)\frac{g(t+h)-g(t)}{h}
+g(t)\frac{f(t+h)-f(t)}{h}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Now let $h\to 0$. Since differentiability implies continuity, $f(t+h)\to f(t)$. Hence

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{d}{dt}\bigl(f(t)g(t)\bigr)
&=f(t)\,g'(t)+g(t)\,f'(t).
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore

$$
\bigl(f(t)g(t)\bigr)'=f'(t)g(t)+g'(t)f(t).
$$

For quotients, assume $g(t)\ne 0$. Apply the product rule to
$f(t)/g(t)=f(t)\bigl(g(t)^{-1}\bigr)$, so it is enough to differentiate $g(t)^{-1}$.

Since

$$
g(t)\,g(t)^{-1}=1,
$$

differentiating both sides gives

$$
g'(t)g(t)^{-1}+g(t)\bigl(g(t)^{-1}\bigr)'=0.
$$

Solving for $\bigl(g(t)^{-1}\bigr)'$ gives

$$
\begin{aligned}
g(t)\bigl(g(t)^{-1}\bigr)'
&=-g'(t)g(t)^{-1}, \\
\bigl(g(t)^{-1}\bigr)'
&=-g'(t)g(t)^{-2}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Now use the product rule:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\left(\frac{f(t)}{g(t)}\right)'
&=\bigl(f(t)g(t)^{-1}\bigr)' \\
&=f'(t)g(t)^{-1}+f(t)\bigl(g(t)^{-1}\bigr)' \\
&=\frac{f'(t)}{g(t)}-f(t)\frac{g'(t)}{g(t)^2} \\
&=\frac{f'(t)g(t)-g'(t)f(t)}{g(t)^2}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus

$$
\left(\frac{f(t)}{g(t)}\right)'
=\frac{f'(t)g(t)-g'(t)f(t)}{g(t)^2},
\qquad g(t)\ne 0.
$$

For compositions, let $F(t)=f(g(t))$. Then

$$
\frac{F(t+h)-F(t)}{h}
=
\frac{f(g(t+h))-f(g(t))}{h}.
$$

Insert the factor $g(t+h)-g(t)$:

$$
\frac{f(g(t+h))-f(g(t))}{h}
=
\frac{f(g(t+h))-f(g(t))}{g(t+h)-g(t)}
\cdot
\frac{g(t+h)-g(t)}{h},
$$

provided $g(t+h)\ne g(t)$. As $h\to 0$, the second factor tends to $g'(t)$, and the first factor tends to $f'(g(t))$ because $g(t+h)\to g(t)$. Therefore

$$
\frac{d}{dt}f(g(t))=f'(g(t))\,g'(t).
$$

So the differentiation rules are

$$
\bigl(\alpha f(t)+\beta g(t)\bigr)'=\alpha f'(t)+\beta g'(t),
$$

$$
\bigl(f(t)g(t)\bigr)'=f'(t)g(t)+g'(t)f(t),
$$

$$
\left(\frac{f(t)}{g(t)}\right)'
=\frac{f'(t)g(t)-g'(t)f(t)}{g(t)^2},
\qquad g(t)\ne 0,
$$

and

$$
\frac{d}{dt}f(g(t))=f'(g(t))\,g'(t).
$$

Given a complex-valued function of a real variable $f(t)=u(t)+i v(t)$, we define the derivative of $f$ in the usual way by

$$
f^{\prime}(t)=\frac{d}{d t} f(t)=\lim _{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{f(t+h)-f(t)}{h} .
$$

As we did in the proof of Theorem 3, Section 2.2, you can show that $f^{\prime}(t)$ exists if and only if $u^{\prime}(t)$ and $v^{\prime}(t)$ both exist, and

$$
f^{\prime}(t)=u^{\prime}(t)+i v^{\prime}(t)
$$

The derivative of a complex-valued function of a real variable satisfies many properties similar to those of the usual derivative of a real-valued function. In particular, if $f$ and $g$ are complex-valued differentiable functions, $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are complex numbers, then

$$
\begin{aligned}
& (\alpha f(t)+\beta g(t))^{\prime}=\alpha f^{\prime}(t)+\beta g^{\prime}(t), \\
& (f(t) g(t))^{\prime}=f^{\prime}(t) g(t)+g^{\prime}(t) f(t), \\
& \left(\frac{f(t)}{g(t)}\right)^{\prime}=\frac{f^{\prime}(t) g(t)-g^{\prime}(t) f(t)}{g(t)^{2}} \quad(g(t) \neq 0), \\
& (f(g(t)))^{\prime}=f^{\prime}(g(t)) g^{\prime}(t) .
\end{aligned}
$$

We leave the verification of these rules as an exercise.



> [!exercise] Exercise 3: Derivative of $e^{\alpha t}$
> Suppose that $\alpha=a+i b$ is a complex number. Show that $\frac{d}{d t} e^{\alpha t}=\alpha e^{\alpha t}$. Hence we recover the same formula from calculus. In particular, if $\alpha=i \omega$ is purely imaginary, then
> 
> $$
> \frac{d}{d t} e^{i \omega t}=i \omega e^{i \omega t} .
> $$
> 



##### solution

Write

$$
\alpha=a+ib,
$$

where $a$ and $b$ are real numbers. Then

$$
e^{\alpha t}=e^{(a+ib)t}=e^{at}e^{ibt}.
$$

Using Euler's formula, we have

$$
e^{ibt}=\cos(bt)+i\sin(bt),
$$

so

$$
e^{\alpha t}=e^{at}\bigl(\cos(bt)+i\sin(bt)\bigr).
$$

Now differentiate. Using the product rule and the fact that
$\dfrac{d}{dt}e^{at}=ae^{at}$,
$\dfrac{d}{dt}\cos(bt)=-b\sin(bt)$, and
$\dfrac{d}{dt}\sin(bt)=b\cos(bt)$, we get

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{d}{dt}e^{\alpha t}
&=\frac{d}{dt}\left(e^{at}\bigl(\cos(bt)+i\sin(bt)\bigr)\right) \\
&=ae^{at}\bigl(\cos(bt)+i\sin(bt)\bigr)
+e^{at}\bigl(-b\sin(bt)+ib\cos(bt)\bigr) \\
&=e^{at}\left(a\cos(bt)+ia\sin(bt)-b\sin(bt)+ib\cos(bt)\right).
\end{aligned}
$$

Now factor the real and imaginary parts:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{d}{dt}e^{\alpha t}
&=e^{at}\left(\bigl(a\cos(bt)-b\sin(bt)\bigr)
+i\bigl(a\sin(bt)+b\cos(bt)\bigr)\right).
\end{aligned}
$$

Next compute $\alpha e^{\alpha t}$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\alpha e^{\alpha t}
&=(a+ib)e^{at}\bigl(\cos(bt)+i\sin(bt)\bigr) \\
&=e^{at}(a+ib)\bigl(\cos(bt)+i\sin(bt)\bigr) \\
&=e^{at}\left(a\cos(bt)+ia\sin(bt)+ib\cos(bt)+i^2b\sin(bt)\right) \\
&=e^{at}\left(a\cos(bt)+ia\sin(bt)+ib\cos(bt)-b\sin(bt)\right) \\
&=e^{at}\left(\bigl(a\cos(bt)-b\sin(bt)\bigr)
+i\bigl(a\sin(bt)+b\cos(bt)\bigr)\right).
\end{aligned}
$$

The two expressions are equal, so

$$
\frac{d}{dt}e^{\alpha t}=\alpha e^{\alpha t}.
$$

In particular, if $\alpha=i\omega$, then $a=0$ and $b=\omega$, so the formula becomes

$$
\frac{d}{dt}e^{i\omega t}=i\omega e^{i\omega t}.
$$

> [!review]
> The mean value theorem for a real-valued function of a real variable guarantees that the total change of the function over an interval equals the derivative at some intermediate point times the length of the interval. Does this property extend to complex-valued functions of a real variable?


##### solution

No. The mean value property for real-valued functions of a real variable does not extend to complex-valued functions of a real variable.

A counterexample is
$f(x)=e^{ix}$ on $[0,2\pi]$. This function is continuous on $[0,2\pi]$ and differentiable on $(0,2\pi)$, with

$$
f'(x)=ie^{ix}.
$$

Also,
$f(2\pi)-f(0)=1-1=0$.

If the mean value property were valid in the complex-valued case, there would have to be some $c\in(0,2\pi)$ such that

$$
f(2\pi)-f(0)=f'(c)(2\pi-0).
$$

Since the left-hand side is $0$, this would force
$f'(c)=0$. But

$$
\left|f'(x)\right|
=
\left|ie^{ix}\right|
=
\left|e^{ix}\right|
=1,
$$

so $f'(x)\neq 0$ for every $x\in(0,2\pi)$.

Therefore no such $c$ exists, and the mean value property fails for complex-valued functions of a real variable.

Having stated properties of the derivative of a real-valued function that hold for complex-valued functions of a real variable, we should caution you that not all properties of derivatives that hold in the real case hold in the complex case. Most notably, the mean value property does not hold for complex-valued functions. In the real case, the mean value property states that if $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$ and differentiable on $(a, b)$, then $f(b)-f(a)= f^{\prime}(c)(b-a)$ for some $c$ in $(a, b)$. This property does not hold for complexvalued functions. To see this, consider $f(x)=e^{i x}$ for $x$ in $[0,2 \pi]$. Then $f$ is continuous on $[0,2 \pi]$ and has a derivative $f^{\prime}(x)=i e^{i x}$ on $(0,2 \pi)$. Also, $f(2 \pi)-f(0)=1-1=0$, but $f^{\prime}(x)$ never vanishes since $\left|f^{\prime}(x)\right|=\left|i e^{i x}\right|= \left|e^{i x}\right|=1$. Hence there is no number $c$ in $(0,2 \pi)$ such that $f(2 \pi)-f(0)= (2 \pi-0) f^{\prime}(c)$, and so the mean value property does not hold (_Figure 6_).

> [!exercise] Exercise 4: A second order ordinary differential equation
> Find a particular solution $y_{p}(x)$ of
> 
> $$
> y^{\prime \prime}-2 y^{\prime}+y=\cos 2 x .
> $$
>

##### solution

We parametrize the three pieces on the three subintervals
$[-2,-1]$, $[-1,1]$, and $[1,2]$.

For the first line segment from $-2$ to $-1$, we use
$\gamma(t)=t$ for $-2\le t\le -1$.

For the upper semicircle of radius $1$ centered at the origin, traced from $-1$ to $1$, we want a parametrization satisfying
$\gamma(-1)=-1$, $\gamma(0)=i$, and $\gamma(1)=1$. A convenient choice is

$$
\gamma(t)=e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}(1-t)},
\qquad
-1\le t\le 1.
$$

Indeed,
$\gamma(-1)=e^{i\pi}=-1$,
$\gamma(0)=e^{i\pi/2}=i$, and
$\gamma(1)=e^0=1$.

For the last line segment from $1$ to $2$, we again use
$\gamma(t)=t$ for $1\le t\le 2$.

Therefore a continuous piecewise-smooth parametrization on $[-2,2]$ is

$$
\gamma(t)=
\begin{cases}
t, & -2\le t\le -1,\\[4pt]
e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}(1-t)}, & -1\le t\le 1,\\[4pt]
t, & 1\le t\le 2.
\end{cases}
$$

This curve is continuous on each subinterval, and the endpoint values match:
$\gamma(-1)=-1$ from both the first and second pieces, and
$\gamma(1)=1$ from both the second and third pieces. Hence $\gamma$ is continuous on $[-2,2]$.

Each piece is smooth on its own subinterval. On $[-2,-1]$ and $[1,2]$, we have $\gamma'(t)=1$. On $[-1,1]$,

$$
\gamma'(t)
=
e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}(1-t)}
\cdot
\frac{d}{dt}\left(i\frac{\pi}{2}(1-t)\right)
=
-\frac{\pi i}{2}e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}(1-t)},
$$

which is continuous on $[-1,1]$.

So $\gamma$ is a continuous piecewise-smooth curve over the closed interval $[-2,2]$. Therefore $\gamma$ is a path in the sense of Definition 2.

# 3.1.2 Contours and Paths


> [!review]
> 1.) What does it mean for a complex-valued function of a real variable to be piecewise continuous on a closed interval $[a, b]$, and why does the definition require all one-sided limits (at discontinuities in the interior and at the endpoints) to exist and be finite?
> 2.) What does it mean for a complex-valued function of a real variable to be piecewise smooth on $[a, b]$, and what additional property makes such a function smooth (continuously differentiable) on $[a, b]$ ?
> 3.) What is a path (or contour) in the complex plane, how does the definition allow a path to be decomposed into smooth pieces, and what does it mean for a path to be closed?



##### solution

##### problem 1

A complex-valued function $f(t)$ is piecewise continuous on a closed interval $[a,b]$ if it is continuous at all but finitely many points of $(a,b)$, and at every point where continuity fails, the one-sided limits exist and are finite. In addition, the right limit at $a$ and the left limit at $b$ must also exist and be finite.

The reason for requiring these one-sided limits is that a piecewise continuous function is allowed to jump, but it is not allowed to oscillate wildly or blow up to infinity at the break points. The existence of finite one-sided limits guarantees that each discontinuity is controlled and that the graph can be broken into finitely many continuous pieces with well-defined endpoint behavior.

##### problem 2

A complex-valued function $f(t)$ is piecewise smooth on $[a,b]$ if both $f$ and $f'(t)$ are piecewise continuous on $[a,b]$.

Thus piecewise smoothness means that the function can be divided into finitely many pieces on which it is differentiable, with both the function and its derivative behaving in a controlled way at the joining points.

Such a function is smooth, or continuously differentiable, on $[a,b]$ if the stronger condition holds that both $f$ and $f'$ are continuous on the entire interval $[a,b]$.

##### problem 3

A path, or contour, in the complex plane is a continuous piecewise smooth curve $\gamma(t)$ defined on a closed interval $[a,b]$.

Because $\gamma$ is piecewise smooth, it may be decomposed into finitely many smooth pieces
$\gamma_1,\gamma_2,\ldots,\gamma_n$ joined end-to-end. In other words, a path need not be smooth on the entire interval at once; it is enough that it be continuous and built from finitely many smooth subcurves.

The path is closed if the initial point of the first piece is joined to the terminal point of the last piece. Equivalently, the curve ends where it begins.


+++++


Returning to our discussion of complex-valued functions of a real variable. we introduce the following useful definitions.


> [!definition] Definition 1: Piecewise Continuous and Piecewise Smooth Functions
> A complex-valued function of a real variable $f(t)$ is said to be piecewise continuous on $[a, b]$ if the following hold:
> (i) $f(t)$ exists and is continuous for all but finitely many points in $(a, b)$.
> (ii) At any point $c$ in $(a, b)$ where $f$ fails to be continuous. both the left limit $\lim f(t)$ and the right limit $\lim f(t)$ exist and are finite.
> (iii) At the endpoints, the right limit $\lim _{t, a} f(t)$ and the left limit $\lim _{t \mid b} f(t)$ exist and are finite.
> The function $f(t)$ is said to be piecewise smooth on $[a, b]$ if $f$ and $f^{\prime}$ are both piecewise continuous on $[a, b]$.




If $f$ and $f^{\prime}$ are both continuous on $[a, b]$ we will say that $f$ is smooth or continuously differentiable on $[a, b]$.

We are now in a position to introduce the notion of path, which is fundamental to our development of the theory of integration. Recall that we have referred to a curve by its parametrization $\gamma(t)$. In addition, we will attribute to the curve the analytical properties of the function $\gamma(t)$, such as continuity and differentiability. Thus, when we talk about a continuous curve or a differentiable curve, we have in mind a specific parametrization with these properties.


> [!definition] Definition 2: Path or Contour
> By a path or a contour we mean a continuous piecewise smooth curve $\gamma(t)$ over a closed interval $[a, b]$.

A path $\gamma$ is continuously differentiable or smooth on $[a, b]$ if $\gamma(t)$ and $\gamma^{\prime}(t)$ are continuous on $[a, b]$. Thus, according to Definition 2, a path or a contour $\gamma$ is a finite sequence of continuously differentiable curves, $\gamma_{1}, \gamma_{2}, \ldots, \gamma_{n}$, joined together end-to-end. In this case, it is sometimes convenient to write $\gamma=\left(\gamma_{1}, \gamma_{2}, \ldots, \gamma_{n}\right)$. The path is closed if the initial point of $\gamma_{1}$ is joined to the terminal point of $\gamma_{n}$.



> [!exercise] Exercise 5: Path
> Construct a continuous piecewise-smooth parametrization on $[-2,2]$ of the curve $\gamma$ that consists of the line segment from -2 to -1 , the upper semicircle of radius 1 centered at the origin (traced from -1 to 1 ), and the line segment from 1 to 2 . Conclude that $\gamma$ is a path in the sense of Definition 2 .




##### solution

We split the curve into three pieces on the subintervals
$[-2,-1]$, $[-1,1]$, and $[1,2]$.

On the first subinterval, the curve is the line segment from $-2$ to $-1$, so we may take
$\gamma(t)=t$ for $-2\le t\le -1$.

On the middle subinterval, we need the upper semicircle of radius $1$ centered at the origin, traced from $-1$ to $1$. A convenient parametrization is

$$
\gamma(t)=e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}(1-t)},
\qquad
-1\le t\le 1.
$$

Indeed,
$\gamma(-1)=e^{i\pi}=-1$,
$\gamma(0)=e^{i\pi/2}=i$, and
$\gamma(1)=e^0=1$.

On the last subinterval, the curve is the line segment from $1$ to $2$, so we again take
$\gamma(t)=t$ for $1\le t\le 2$.

Therefore a continuous piecewise-smooth parametrization on $[-2,2]$ is

$$
\gamma(t)=
\begin{cases}
t, & -2\le t\le -1,\\[4pt]
e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}(1-t)}, & -1\le t\le 1,\\[4pt]
t, & 1\le t\le 2.
\end{cases}
$$

The endpoint values agree at the junctions:
$\gamma(-1)=-1$ from both the first and second pieces, and
$\gamma(1)=1$ from both the second and third pieces. Hence $\gamma$ is continuous on $[-2,2]$.

Each piece is smooth on its own interval. On the two line segments, $\gamma'(t)=1$. On the semicircular piece,

$$
\gamma'(t)
=
e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}(1-t)}
\cdot
\frac{d}{dt}\left(i\frac{\pi}{2}(1-t)\right)
=
-\frac{\pi i}{2}e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}(1-t)},
$$

which is continuous on $[-1,1]$.

So $\gamma$ is a continuous piecewise-smooth curve on the closed interval $[-2,2]$. Therefore $\gamma$ is a path in the sense of Definition 2.

\gamma_{3}(t)=(1-t)(-1+i) . & 0 \leq t \leq 1 .

To parametrize $\gamma_{1}$ over $\left[0, \frac{1}{3}\right]$, it suffices to change $t$ to $3 t$. This yields

$$
\gamma_{1}(t)=3 t(1+i), \quad 0 \leq t \leq \frac{1}{3} .
$$

To parametrize $\gamma_{2}$ over $\left[\frac{1}{3}, \frac{2}{3}\right]$, we first scale down by changing $t$ to $3 t$,

$$
\gamma_{2}(t)=(1+i)-6 t, \quad 0 \leq t \leq \frac{1}{3} .
$$

We then shift to the right by $\frac{1}{3}$ units and get

$$
\gamma_{2}(t)=(1+i)-6\left(t-\frac{1}{3}\right)=3+i-6 t, \quad \frac{1}{3} \leq t \leq \frac{2}{3} .
$$

For $\gamma_{3}$, we first scale by a factor of $\frac{1}{3}$ and get

$$
\gamma_{3}(t)=(1-3 t)(-1+i), \quad 0 \leq t \leq \frac{1}{3} .
$$

We then shift to the right by $\frac{2}{3}$ units and get

$$
\gamma_{3}(t)=\left(1-3\left(t-\frac{2}{3}\right)\right)(-1+i)=(-1+i)(3-3 t), \quad \frac{2}{3} \leq t \leq 1 .
$$

Pasting the equations together, we obtain

$$
\gamma(t)= \begin{cases}3 t(1+i) & \text { if } 0 \leq t \leq \frac{1}{3}, \\ 3+i-6 t & \text { if } \frac{1}{3} \leq t \leq \frac{2}{3}, \\ (-1+i)(3-3 t) & \text { if } \frac{2}{3} \leq t \leq 1 .\end{cases}
$$

The polygonal path $\gamma$ is clearly continuous with a piecewise continuous derivative. It is thus a path in the sense of Definition 2.
