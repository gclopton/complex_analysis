##### review 2

The correspondence between real numbers and complex numbers is not a bijection from $\mathbb{R}$ onto $\mathbb{C}$. The map

$$
x \mapsto x+0 i
$$

is injective, because different real numbers give different complex numbers, but it is not surjective, because complex numbers with nonzero imaginary part are not reached.

This matters geometrically because a real number can be represented on a line, while a general complex number requires two real coordinates and is therefore represented in the plane. In the complex plane, we can think of a complex number both as a point $(x,y)$ and as the vector from the origin to that point. These two geometric viewpoints coincide in the plane, but they cannot be captured by a single real line.


##### review 3

Purely real numbers are complex numbers of the form

$$
a+0 i,
$$

so they lie on the real axis. Purely imaginary numbers are complex numbers of the form

$$
0+i b=i b,
$$

so they lie on the imaginary axis. Thus a complex number is purely real exactly when its imaginary part is $0$, and it is purely imaginary exactly when its real part is $0$.



---




> [!NOTE]
> Historically, the geometric representation of complex numbers is due to Gauss and two lesser known mathematicians: the Frenchman Jean Robert Argand (1768-1822) and a Norwegian surveyor Caspar Wessel (1745-1818). This relatively simple idea dispelled the mystery and skepticism surrounding complex numbers. Much as real numbers are represented as points on a line, complex numbers are represented as points in the plane. As you will soon see, our ability to visualize complex numbers will greatly enhance our understanding of facts concerning complex numbers, and in some cases greatly simplify their proofs.

## 1.2.1 Geometric Interpretation of Algebraic Rules


> [!review] Review 3
> Contents


The vector representation allows us to add complex numbers by adding their vectors in the plane, with the usual head-to-tail or parallelogram methods (see Figure 3).

> [!figure] Figure 3
> 
> ![](13d4bcc6-f973-445e-ad7d-b22a7a43507d-12_388_427_650_96.jpg)
> Figure 3 Vector addition of complex numbers: the head-to-tail method. Slide $z_{2}$ over so its tail lies atop $z_{1}$ 's head. The resultant vector is then $z_{1}+z_{2}$.


Multiplying a complex number by -1 has the effect of reflecting it about the origin. If $z=x+i y=(x, y)$, then $-z=-x-i y=(-x,-y)$. This will allow us to subtract complex numbers. The complex subtraction $z_{1}-z_{2}$ can be performed by first multiplying the $z_{2}$ vector by -1 , then adding the resultant to $z_{1}$. Alternatively, we can draw both vectors with their tails at the origin, and the difference will be the vector that points from the head of $z_{2}$ to the head of $z_{1}$ (see Figure 4).



> [!figure] Figure 4
> 
> ![](13d4bcc6-f973-445e-ad7d-b22a7a43507d-12_508_520_540_788.jpg)
> Figure 4 Subtraction: We will find $z_{1}-z_{2}$. We could take $-z_{2}$, then add it to $z_{1}$. Or we could take the vector with its tail at $z_{2}$ 's head, and its head at $z_{1}$ 's head.



Conjugation has an interesting interpretation in the complex plane. Since the conjugate of $z=x+i y=(x, y)$ is $\bar{z}=x-i y=(x,-y)$, conjugates are reflections of each other across the real axis (see Figure 5).



> [!figure] Figure 5
>

> Figure 5 Complex conjugation reflects a point about the horizontal axis.

## 1.2.2 The Absolute Value


> [!review] Review 4
> Contents


We now discuss an important geometric aspect of complex numbers. For a complex number $z=x+i y$, we define the norm or modulus or absolute value of $z$ by

$$
|z|=\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}
$$

If $z=x$ is real, then $|z|=\sqrt{x^{2}}=|x|$. Thus the absolute value of a complex number $z$ reduces to the familiar absolute value when $z$ is real. Just as the absolute value $|x|$ of a real number $x$ represents the distance from $x$ to the origin on the real line, the absolute value $|z|$ of a complex number $z$ represents the distance from the point $z$ to the origin in the complex plane (see Figure 6). 



> [!figure] Figure 6
> 
> ![](13d4bcc6-f973-445e-ad7d-b22a7a43507d-13_462_510_238_134.jpg)
> Figure 6 The distance from $z$ to the origin is $|z|$.



It is easy to see from (1) or Figure 6 that

$$
|z|=0 \Leftrightarrow z=0 ;
$$

and

$$
|-z|=|z| .
$$

If $z_{1}=x_{1}+i y_{1}$ and $z_{2}=x_{2}+i y_{2}$, applying (1) to the complex number $z_{1}-z_{2}=\left(x_{1}-x_{2}\right)+i\left(y_{1}-y_{2}\right)$, we obtain

$$
\left|z_{1}-z_{2}\right|=\sqrt{\left(x_{1}-x_{2}\right)^{2}+\left(y_{1}-y_{2}\right)^{2}}
$$

which is the familiar formula for distance between the points ( $x_{1}, y_{1}$ ) and $\left(x_{2}, y_{2}\right)$. Thus $\left|z_{1}-z_{2}\right|$ has a concrete geometric interpretation as the distance between (the points) $z_{1}$ and $z_{2}$ (see Figure 7).

The absolute value of complex numbers enjoys many interesting properties, including all those of the usual absolute value of real numbers.

> [!identity] Identities Involving the Absolute Value
> Let $z, z_{1}, z_{2}, \ldots$ be any complex numbers. We have
> 
> $$
> |z|=\sqrt{z \bar{z}}, \quad \text { and also } \quad|z|^{2}=z \bar{z} .
> $$
> 
> Furthermore
> 
> $$
> |z|=|\bar{z}| .
> $$
> 
> The absolute value of a product satisfies
> 
> $$
> \begin{gathered}
> \left|z_{1} z_{2}\right|=\left|z_{1}\right|\left|z_{2}\right| \\
> \left|z_{1} z_{2} \cdots z_{n}\right|=\left|z_{1}\right|\left|z_{2}\right| \cdots\left|z_{n}\right| \\
> \left|z^{n}\right|=|z|^{n} \quad(n=1,2, \ldots)
> \end{gathered}
> $$
> 
> The absolute value of a quotient satisfies
> 
> $$
> \left|\frac{z_{1}}{z_{2}}\right|=\frac{\left|z_{1}\right|}{\left|z_{2}\right|} \quad\left(z_{2} \neq 0\right)
> $$
> 


> [!NOTE]
> (10) states that the absolute value of a product is the product of the absolute values.
> (13) states that the absolute value of a quotient is the quotient of the absolute values.

In addition to the identities that we just proved, the absolute value satisfies fundamental inequalities, which are in some cases immediate consequences of elementary facts from geometry. Let us recall two facts and then list the inequalities that will be obtained as a consequence.

- In a right triangle, the hypotenuse is larger than either one of the other two sides.
- In any triangle, the sum of two sides is larger than the third side.



> [!identity] Inequalities Involving the Absolute Value
> Let $z, z_{1}, z_{2}, \ldots$ be complex numbers. We have
> 
> $$
> \begin{gathered}
> |\operatorname{Re} z| \leq|z|, \quad|\operatorname{Im} z| \leq|z| ; \\
> |z| \leq|\operatorname{Re} z|+|\operatorname{Im} z| .
> \end{gathered}
> $$
> 
> The absolute value of a sum satisfies the fundamental inequality
> 
> $$
> \left|z_{1}+z_{2}\right| \leq\left|z_{1}\right|+\left|z_{2}\right|
> $$
> 
> known as the triangle inequality. More generally,
> 
> $$
> \left|z_{1}+z_{2}+\cdots+z_{n}\right| \leq\left|z_{1}\right|+\left|z_{2}\right|+\cdots+\left|z_{n}\right| .
> $$
>

> 
> $$
> \left|z_{1}-z_{2}\right| \leq\left|z_{1}\right|+\left|z_{2}\right|

> 
> $$
> \left|z_{1} \pm z_{2}\right| \geq\left|\left|z_{1}\right|-\right| z_{2} \|

Putting these two together, we conclude $\left|z_{1}-z_{2}\right| \geq\left|\left|z_{1}\right|-\left|z_{2}\right|\right|$, which proves the "minus-sign" inequality in (19). To get the inequality with a plus sign, namely, $\left|z_{1}+z_{2}\right| \geq\left|\left|z_{1}\right|-\left|z_{2}\right|\right|$, replace $z_{2}$ by $-z_{2}$.

Because the triangle inequality is fundamental in the development of complex analysis, we will also offer an algebraic proof. This will afford us the opportunity to practice some of the properties of complex numbers that we have already derived. Start by observing that

$$
\overline{z_{1} \overline{z_{2}}}=\overline{z_{1}} \overline{\overline{z_{2}}}=\overline{z_{1}} z_{2}
$$

Since for any complex number $w, w+\bar{w}=2 \operatorname{Re} w$, we obtain

$$
z_{1} \overline{z_{2}}+\overline{z_{1}} z_{2}=z_{1} \overline{z_{2}}+\overline{z_{1}} \overline{z_{2}}=2 \operatorname{Re}\left(z_{1} \overline{z_{2}}\right)
$$

Using this interesting identity, along with (8) and basic properties of complex conjugation, we obtain

$$
\begin{aligned}
\left|z_{1}+z_{2}\right|^{2} & =\left(z_{1}+z_{2}\right) \overline{\left(z_{1}+z_{2}\right)}=\left(z_{1}+z_{2}\right)\left(\overline{z_{1}}+\overline{z_{2}}\right) \\
& =z_{1} \overline{z_{1}}+z_{2} \overline{z_{2}}+z_{1} \overline{z_{2}}+\overline{z_{1}} z_{2}=\left|z_{1}\right|^{2}+\left|z_{2}\right|^{2}+z_{1} \overline{z_{2}}+\overline{z_{1}} z_{2} \\
& =\left|z_{1}\right|^{2}+\left|z_{2}\right|^{2}+2 \operatorname{Re}\left(z_{1} \overline{z_{2}}\right) \\
& \leq\left|z_{1}\right|^{2}+\left|z_{2}\right|^{2}+2\left|z_{1} \overline{z_{2}}\right| \\
& =\left|z_{1}\right|^{2}+\left|z_{2}\right|^{2}+2\left|z_{1}\right| \overline{z_{2}} \mid \\
& =\left|z_{1}\right|^{2}+\left|z_{2}\right|^{2}+2\left|z_{1}\right|\left|z_{2}\right| \quad\left([\text { by }(9)],\left|\overline{z_{2}}\right|=\left|z_{2}\right|\right) \\
& =\left(\left|z_{1}\right|+\left|z_{2}\right|\right)^{2}
\end{aligned}
$$

and (16) follows now upon taking square roots on both sides.
The triangle inequality will be used extensively in proofs to provide estimates on the sizes of complex-valued expressions. We next illustrate this type of applications with examples.

##### problem 14.21

From problem 20, the ellipse is described by

$$
|2z|+|2z-1|=4 .
$$

Derivation Mode:

$$
\begin{aligned}
|2z|+|2z-1|
&=4 \\
2|z|+|2z-1|
&=4 \\
2|z|+|2z-2\cdot \tfrac{1}{2}|
&=4 \\
2|z|+|2(z-\tfrac{1}{2})|
&=4 \\
2|z|+2|z-\tfrac{1}{2}|
&=4 \\
|z|+|z-\tfrac{1}{2}|
&=2 .
\end{aligned}
$$

Now write $z=x+iy$ and center the equation at the midpoint $\frac{1}{4}$ of the foci:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}+\sqrt{\left(x-\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{2}+y^{2}}
&=2 \\
\sqrt{\left(\left(x-\tfrac{1}{4}\right)+\tfrac{1}{4}\right)^{2}+y^{2}}+\sqrt{\left(\left(x-\tfrac{1}{4}\right)-\tfrac{1}{4}\right)^{2}+y^{2}}
&=2 .
\end{aligned}
$$

Let

$$
u=x-\tfrac{1}{4} .
$$

Then

$$
\sqrt{\left(u+\tfrac{1}{4}\right)^{2}+y^{2}}+\sqrt{\left(u-\tfrac{1}{4}\right)^{2}+y^{2}}=2 .
$$

Continue in Derivation Mode:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\sqrt{\left(u+\tfrac{1}{4}\right)^{2}+y^{2}}
&=2-\sqrt{\left(u-\tfrac{1}{4}\right)^{2}+y^{2}} \\
\left(u+\tfrac{1}{4}\right)^{2}+y^{2}
&=4-4\sqrt{\left(u-\tfrac{1}{4}\right)^{2}+y^{2}}+\left(u-\tfrac{1}{4}\right)^{2}+y^{2} \\
u^{2}+\tfrac{u}{2}+\tfrac{1}{16}+y^{2}
&=4-4\sqrt{\left(u-\tfrac{1}{4}\right)^{2}+y^{2}}+u^{2}-\tfrac{u}{2}+\tfrac{1}{16}+y^{2} \\
\tfrac{u}{2}
&=4-4\sqrt{\left(u-\tfrac{1}{4}\right)^{2}+y^{2}}-\tfrac{u}{2} \\
u
&=4-4\sqrt{\left(u-\tfrac{1}{4}\right)^{2}+y^{2}} \\
4\sqrt{\left(u-\tfrac{1}{4}\right)^{2}+y^{2}}
&=4-u \\
\sqrt{\left(u-\tfrac{1}{4}\right)^{2}+y^{2}}
&=1-\tfrac{u}{4} \\
\left(u-\tfrac{1}{4}\right)^{2}+y^{2}
&=\left(1-\tfrac{u}{4}\right)^{2} \\
u^{2}-\tfrac{u}{2}+\tfrac{1}{16}+y^{2}
&=1-\tfrac{u}{2}+\tfrac{u^{2}}{16} \\
16u^{2}-8u+1+16y^{2}
&=16-8u+u^{2} \\
15u^{2}+16y^{2}
&=15 \\
u^{2}+\tfrac{16}{15}y^{2}
&=1 \\
\left(x-\tfrac{1}{4}\right)^{2}+\tfrac{16}{15}y^{2}
&=1 .
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore the Cartesian equation of the ellipse is

$$
\left(x-\tfrac{1}{4}\right)^{2}+\frac{16}{15}y^{2}=1,
$$

or equivalently

$$
\frac{\left(x-\tfrac{1}{4}\right)^{2}}{1}+\frac{y^{2}}{15/16}=1 .
$$

##### problem 15.23c

Write

$$
z_{1}=x_{1}+iy_{1}, \qquad z_{2}=x_{2}+iy_{2} .
$$

Then

$$
z_{2}-z_{1}=(x_{2}-x_{1})+i(y_{2}-y_{1}) .
$$

For real $t$,

$$
\begin{aligned}
z
&=z_{1}+t(z_{2}-z_{1}) \\
&=(x_{1}+iy_{1})+t\bigl((x_{2}-x_{1})+i(y_{2}-y_{1})\bigr) \\
&=\bigl(x_{1}+t(x_{2}-x_{1})\bigr)+i\bigl(y_{1}+t(y_{2}-y_{1})\bigr) .
\end{aligned}
$$

So the coordinates of $z$ are

$$
x=x_{1}+t(x_{2}-x_{1}), \qquad y=y_{1}+t(y_{2}-y_{1}) .
$$

These are precisely the parametric equations of the line through the two points $(x_{1},y_{1})$ and $(x_{2},y_{2})$. Also, when $t=0$ we get $z=z_{1}$, and when $t=1$ we get $z=z_{2}$. As $t$ ranges over all real numbers, the point $z$ runs through the entire line through $z_{1}$ and $z_{2}$.

For illustration, take

$$
z_{1}=1+i, \qquad z_{2}=3+2i .
$$

Then

$$
z_{1}+t(z_{2}-z_{1})=1+i+t(2+i),
$$

> 30. Equality in the triangle inequality. When do we have
> 
> $$
> \left|z_{1}+z_{2}\right|=\left|z_{1}\right|+\left|z_{2}\right| ?
> $$
> 
> To answer this question, review the algebraic proof of the triangle inequality and note that the string of equalities in the proof was broken when we replaced $2 \operatorname{Re}\left(z_{1} \overline{z_{2}}\right)$ by $2\left|z_{1}\right|\left|z_{2}\right|$. Show that this is the case if and only either $z_{1}$ or $z_{2}$ is zero or $z_{1}=\alpha z_{2}$, where $\alpha$ is a positive real number. Geometrically, this states that $z_{1}$ and $z_{2}$ are on the same side of the ray issued from the origin.
> 
> 


From the algebraic proof of the triangle inequality, we have equality

$$
\left|z_{1}+z_{2}\right|=\left|z_{1}\right|+\left|z_{2}\right|
$$

if and only if

$$
\operatorname{Re}\left(z_{1}\overline{z_{2}}\right)=\left|z_{1}\right|\left|z_{2}\right| .
$$

We now determine when this happens.

If either $z_{1}=0$ or $z_{2}=0$, then equality is immediate, since

$$
\left|z_{1}+z_{2}\right|=\left|z_{1}\right|+\left|z_{2}\right|
$$

reduces to a trivial identity.

Now assume that $z_{1}\neq 0$ and $z_{2}\neq 0$. Set

$$
w=z_{1}\overline{z_{2}} .
$$

Then

$$
|w|=\left|z_{1}\overline{z_{2}}\right|=\left|z_{1}\right|\left|z_{2}\right| .
$$

So the condition becomes

$$
\operatorname{Re}(w)=|w| .
$$

Write $w=u+iv$, where $u$ and $v$ are real. Then, in Derivation Mode,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\operatorname{Re}(w)=|w|
&\Longleftrightarrow u=\sqrt{u^{2}+v^{2}} \\
&\Longleftrightarrow u^{2}=u^{2}+v^{2} \quad \text{and } u\ge 0 \\
&\Longleftrightarrow v^{2}=0 \quad \text{and } u\ge 0 \\
&\Longleftrightarrow v=0 \quad \text{and } u\ge 0 \\
&\Longleftrightarrow w \text{ is a nonnegative real number.}
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence

$$
\operatorname{Re}\left(z_{1}\overline{z_{2}}\right)=\left|z_{1}\right|\left|z_{2}\right|
$$

if and only if

$$
z_{1}\overline{z_{2}}=\beta
$$

for some real number $\beta\ge 0$.

Since $z_{2}\neq 0$, we may divide by $\overline{z_{2}}$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
z_{1}\overline{z_{2}}=\beta
&\Longleftrightarrow z_{1}=\frac{\beta}{\overline{z_{2}}} \\
&\Longleftrightarrow z_{1}=\frac{\beta z_{2}}{z_{2}\overline{z_{2}}} \\
&\Longleftrightarrow z_{1}=\frac{\beta}{|z_{2}|^{2}}\,z_{2} .
\end{aligned}
$$

If we let

$$
\alpha=\frac{\beta}{|z_{2}|^{2}},
$$

then $\alpha\ge 0$ is real, and

$$
z_{1}=\alpha z_{2} .
$$

Because we are in the case $z_{1}\neq 0$ and $z_{2}\neq 0$, we must actually have $\alpha>0$.

Conversely, if

$$
z_{1}=\alpha z_{2}
$$

with $\alpha>0$ real, then

$$
\begin{aligned}
z_{1}\overline{z_{2}}
&=\alpha z_{2}\overline{z_{2}} \\
&=\alpha |z_{2}|^{2},
\end{aligned}
$$

which is a positive real number. Therefore

$$
\operatorname{Re}\left(z_{1}\overline{z_{2}}\right)=\alpha |z_{2}|^{2}=\left|z_{1}\right|\left|z_{2}\right|,

and equality holds in the triangle inequality.

$$
\left|z_{1}+z_{2}\right|=\left|z_{1}\right|+\left|z_{2}\right|
$$

if and only if either $z_{1}=0$, or $z_{2}=0$, or

$$
z_{1}=\alpha z_{2}
$$

for some positive real number $\alpha$.

Geometrically, this means that $z_{1}$ and $z_{2}$ point in exactly the same direction from the origin.

&=\left(a^{2}+b^{2}-2ab\cos\alpha\right)+\left(a^{2}+b^{2}+2ab\cos\alpha\right) \\
&=2a^{2}+2b^{2} \\
&=2\left(a^{2}+b^{2}\right) .
\end{aligned}
$$

This proves the parallelogram identity for the side lengths and diagonals of an arbitrary parallelogram.

##### problem 24.32b

If the parallelogram has adjacent sides represented by the vectors $u$ and $v$, then its four vertices are

$$
0, \qquad u, \qquad v, \qquad u+v .
$$

So one diagonal runs from $0$ to $u+v$, and therefore is represented by the vector

$$
u+v .
$$

The other diagonal runs from the vertex $v$ to the vertex $u$, so it is represented by the vector

$$
u-v .
$$

For illustration, take

$$
u=2+i, \qquad v=1+2i .
$$

Then the parallelogram has vertices

$$
0, \qquad 2+i, \qquad 1+2i, \qquad 3+3i,
$$

with diagonals $u+v=3+3i$ and $u-v=1-i$.

##### problem 24.32c

In the parallelogram determined by the vectors $u$ and $v$, the side lengths are

$$
|u| \qquad \text{and} \qquad |v|,
$$

and the diagonal lengths are

$$
|u+v| \qquad \text{and} \qquad |u-v| .
$$

Applying part (a) with

$$
a=|u|, \qquad b=|v|, \qquad c=|u+v|, \qquad d=|u-v|,
$$

we obtain

$$
|u+v|^{2}+|u-v|^{2}=2\left(|u|^{2}+|v|^{2}\right) .
$$

This is the parallelogram identity for complex numbers.

##### problem 24.32d

Using (8), namely $|w|^{2}=w\overline{w}$, we compute in Derivation Mode:

$$
\begin{aligned}
|u+v|^{2}+|u-v|^{2}
&=(u+v)\overline{(u+v)}+(u-v)\overline{(u-v)} \\
&=(u+v)(\bar{u}+\bar{v})+(u-v)(\bar{u}-\bar{v}) \\
&=u\bar{u}+u\bar{v}+v\bar{u}+v\bar{v}+u\bar{u}-u\bar{v}-v\bar{u}+v\bar{v} \\
&=2u\bar{u}+2v\bar{v} \\
&=2|u|^{2}+2|v|^{2} \\
&=2\left(|u|^{2}+|v|^{2}\right) .
\end{aligned}
$$

This completes the algebraic proof.

##### problem 28.41a

To prove (21), start with the triangle inequality:

$$
\left|\sum_{j=1}^{n}\overline{v_{j}}w_{j}\right|
\leq \sum_{j=1}^{n}\left|\overline{v_{j}}w_{j}\right| .
$$

Using the absolute value of a product and the fact that $|\overline{v_j}|=|v_j|$, we get

$$
\left|\sum_{j=1}^{n}\overline{v_{j}}w_{j}\right|
\leq \sum_{j=1}^{n}|v_{j}||w_{j}| .
$$

Therefore, if we can prove

$$
\sum_{j=1}^{n}|v_{j}||w_{j}|
\leq \sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^{n}|v_{j}|^{2}} \sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^{n}|w_{j}|^{2}},
$$

which is (22), then it immediately follows that

$$
\left|\sum_{j=1}^{n}\overline{v_{j}}w_{j}\right|
\leq \sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^{n}|v_{j}|^{2}} \sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^{n}|w_{j}|^{2}} .
$$

So proving (22) is enough to prove (21).

##### problem 28.41b

Assume

$$
\sum_{j=1}^{n}|v_{j}|^{2}=1, \qquad \sum_{j=1}^{n}|w_{j}|^{2}=1 .
$$

Now use the hint and work in Derivation Mode:

$$
\begin{aligned}
0
&\leq \sum_{j=1}^{n}\left(|v_{j}|-|w_{j}|\right)^{2} \\
&= \sum_{j=1}^{n}\left(|v_{j}|^{2}-2|v_{j}||w_{j}|+|w_{j}|^{2}\right) \\
&= \sum_{j=1}^{n}|v_{j}|^{2}-2\sum_{j=1}^{n}|v_{j}||w_{j}|+\sum_{j=1}^{n}|w_{j}|^{2} \\
&= 1-2\sum_{j=1}^{n}|v_{j}||w_{j}|+1 \\
&= 2-2\sum_{j=1}^{n}|v_{j}||w_{j}| .
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence

$$
2\sum_{j=1}^{n}|v_{j}||w_{j}| \leq 2,
$$

so

$$
\sum_{j=1}^{n}|v_{j}||w_{j}| \leq 1 .
$$

But under our normalization,

$$
\sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^{n}|v_{j}|^{2}} \sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^{n}|w_{j}|^{2}}
=\sqrt{1}\sqrt{1}
=1 .
$$

Therefore

$$
\sum_{j=1}^{n}|v_{j}||w_{j}| \leq \sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^{n}|v_{j}|^{2}} \sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^{n}|w_{j}|^{2}} .
$$

This proves (22) in the normalized case.

##### problem 28.41c

Now consider the general case. If all $v_j=0$, then

$$
\sum_{j=1}^{n}|v_{j}||w_{j}|=0,
$$

so (22) is trivial. Likewise, if all $w_j=0$, then (22) is also trivial. So we may assume that $v$ and $w$ are not identically zero.

Define

$$
\|v\|=\sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^{n}|v_{j}|^{2}}, \qquad \|w\|=\sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^{n}|w_{j}|^{2}} .
$$

Then $\|v\|>0$ and $\|w\|>0$. Consider the normalized vectors

$$
\widetilde{v}=\frac{1}{\|v\|}v, \qquad \widetilde{w}=\frac{1}{\|w\|}w .
$$

Their components are

$$
\widetilde{v}_{j}=\frac{v_{j}}{\|v\|}, \qquad \widetilde{w}_{j}=\frac{w_{j}}{\|w\|} .
$$

Now use Derivation Mode:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\sum_{j=1}^{n}|\widetilde{v}_{j}|^{2}
&= \sum_{j=1}^{n}\left|\frac{v_{j}}{\|v\|}\right|^{2} \\
&= \sum_{j=1}^{n}\frac{|v_{j}|^{2}}{\|v\|^{2}} \\
&= \frac{1}{\|v\|^{2}}\sum_{j=1}^{n}|v_{j}|^{2} \\
&= \frac{\|v\|^{2}}{\|v\|^{2}} \\
&= 1,
\end{aligned}
$$

and similarly

$$
\sum_{j=1}^{n}|\widetilde{w}_{j}|^{2}=1 .
$$

So part (b) applies to $\widetilde v$ and $\widetilde w$:

$$
\sum_{j=1}^{n}|\widetilde{v}_{j}||\widetilde{w}_{j}| \leq 1 .
$$

Substitute the definitions and simplify in Derivation Mode:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\sum_{j=1}^{n}\left|\frac{v_{j}}{\|v\|}\right|\left|\frac{w_{j}}{\|w\|}\right|
&\leq 1 \\
\sum_{j=1}^{n}\frac{|v_{j}||w_{j}|}{\|v\|\|w\|}
&\leq 1 \\
\frac{1}{\|v\|\|w\|}\sum_{j=1}^{n}|v_{j}||w_{j}|
&\leq 1 \\
\sum_{j=1}^{n}|v_{j}||w_{j}|
&\leq \|v\|\|w\| \\
&= \sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^{n}|v_{j}|^{2}} \sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^{n}|w_{j}|^{2}} .
\end{aligned}
$$

This proves (22) in general. By part (a), (21) follows:

$$
\left|\sum_{j=1}^{n}\overline{v_{j}}w_{j}\right|
\leq \sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^{n}|v_{j}|^{2}} \sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^{n}|w_{j}|^{2}} .
$$
