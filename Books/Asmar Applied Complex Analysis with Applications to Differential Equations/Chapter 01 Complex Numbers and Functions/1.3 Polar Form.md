##### review 1.1

A point $P=(x,y) \neq (0,0)$ is represented in polar coordinates as

$$
P=(r,\theta),
$$

where $r$ is the distance from $P$ to the origin and $\theta$ is the angle measured from the positive $x$-axis to the ray from the origin to $P$.


##### review 1.2

In polar coordinates, counterclockwise rotation is positive, while clockwise rotation is negative.


##### review 1.3

The coordinate-change formulas are

$$
x=r \cos \theta,
\qquad
y=r \sin \theta,
$$

with the restriction

$$
r \geq 0.
$$


##### review 1.4

If

$$
z=x+i y,
$$

then its polar form is

$$
z=r(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta).
$$

Here $r$ is the modulus of $z$, and $\theta$ is an argument of $z$.


##### review 1.5

The modulus can be written as

$$
r=\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}=|z|=\sqrt{z \bar{z}}.
$$

##### review 1.7

The argument identities are

$$
\cos \theta=\frac{x}{r},
\qquad
\sin \theta=\frac{y}{r},
$$

which are valid when

$$
r \neq 0,
$$

and

$$
\tan \theta=\frac{y}{x},
$$

which is valid when

$$
x \neq 0.
$$


##### review 1.8

In polar form,

$$
\operatorname{Re} z=r \cos \theta,
\qquad
\operatorname{Im} z=r \sin \theta.
$$


##### review 1.9

When

$$
z=0,
$$

we have

$$
r=0,
$$

and the argument is not defined.


##### review 1.10

We have

$$
|z|=1
\iff r=1.
$$

If $r=1$, then the polar form becomes

$$
z=r(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)=\cos \theta+i \sin \theta.
$$

Conversely, if

$$
z=\cos \theta+i \sin \theta,
$$

then it is in polar form with modulus

$$
r=1,
$$

so

$$
|z|=1.
$$

Therefore

$$
|z|=1
\iff
z=\cos \theta+i \sin \theta
\quad \text{for some real } \theta.
$$


##### review 1.11

A complex number is unimodular if its modulus is $1$. Equivalently, it is a complex number lying on the unit circle.

##### problem 1

Let

$$
z=x+iy\neq 0.
$$

An angle $\theta$ is called an argument of $z$ if it satisfies the argument identities

$$
\cos\theta=\frac{x}{|z|},
\qquad
\sin\theta=\frac{y}{|z|}.
$$

These identities do not determine a unique value of $\theta$ because the sine and cosine functions are $2\pi$-periodic. In derivation form,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\cos(\theta+2k\pi)
&=\cos\theta, \\
\sin(\theta+2k\pi)
&=\sin\theta,
\end{aligned}
\qquad
k\in\mathbb{Z}.
$$

So if one angle $\theta$ satisfies

$$
\cos\theta=\frac{x}{|z|},
\qquad
\sin\theta=\frac{y}{|z|},
$$

then every angle of the form

$$
\theta+2k\pi,
\qquad
k\in\mathbb{Z},
$$

also satisfies the same two identities. Thus the argument is not unique.

To obtain a unique value, we restrict $\theta$ to the interval

$$
-\pi<\theta\le \pi.
$$

This interval excludes the left endpoint

$$
-\pi
$$

and includes the right endpoint

$$
\pi.
$$

With this restriction, there is exactly one value of $\theta$ satisfying the argument identities. This unique value is called the principal value of the argument and is denoted by

$$
\operatorname{Arg} z.
$$

So the defining statement is

$$
-\pi<\operatorname{Arg} z\le \pi.
$$

##### problem 2

The symbols

$$
\arg z
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\operatorname{Arg} z
$$

do not mean the same thing.

The symbol

$$
\operatorname{Arg} z
$$

denotes the single principal value of the argument, namely the unique number in the interval

$$
(-\pi,\pi]
$$

that is an argument of $z$.

By contrast,

$$
\arg z
$$

denotes the full set of all arguments of $z$. Therefore,

$$
\arg z=\{\operatorname{Arg} z+2k\pi:k\in\mathbb{Z}\}.
$$

Equivalently, if $\theta$ is any one argument of $z$, then

$$
\arg z=\{\theta+2k\pi:k\in\mathbb{Z}\}.
$$

So

$$
\operatorname{Arg} z
$$

is a single real number, while

$$
\arg z
$$

is a set of real numbers. In other words,

$$
\operatorname{Arg} z
$$

is single-valued, whereas

$$
\arg z
$$

is multi-valued or set-valued.

Sometimes one abuses notation and writes

$$
\arg z=\theta+2k\pi
$$

or even simply

$$
\arg z=\theta
$$

to indicate that one specific value has been selected from the set

$$
\arg z.
$$

We now shift our attention to the more delicate subject of the argument. If $\theta$ is any angle that satisfies "the argument identities", then $\theta+2 k \pi(k=0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots)$ will also satisfy the argument identities, because the cosine and sine are $2 \pi$-periodic functions. Thus, the argument identities do not determine a unique value of argument $z$. If we restrict the choice of $\theta$ to the interval $-\pi<\theta \leq \pi$, then there is a unique value of $\theta$ that satisfies (4). We call this value ==the principal value of the argument== and denote it by $\operatorname{Arg} z$. Thus, by definition, if $z=x+i y$, then

##### problem 1a

For a complex number written in rectangular form as

$$
z=x+i y,
$$

the formulas we use are

$$
|z|=\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}},
$$

and

$$
z=r(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta),
\qquad
r=|z|,
\qquad
x=r \cos \theta,
\qquad
y=r \sin \theta .
$$

Also, the principal value of the argument is the unique angle

$$
\operatorname{Arg} z \in(-\pi, \pi] .
$$

Now apply these formulas to

$$
z_{1}=5=5+0 i .
$$

So

$$
x=5,
\qquad
y=0 .
$$

The modulus is

$$
\begin{aligned}
\left|z_{1}\right|
&=\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}} \\
&=\sqrt{5^{2}+0^{2}} \\
&=\sqrt{25+0} \\
&=\sqrt{25} \\
&=5 .
\end{aligned}
$$

To find the argument, use

$$
\begin{aligned}
x
&=r \cos \theta \\
5
&=5 \cos \theta \\
\frac{5}{5}
&=\cos \theta \\
1
&=\cos \theta,
\end{aligned}
\qquad
\begin{aligned}
y
&=r \sin \theta \\
0
&=5 \sin \theta \\
\frac{0}{5}
&=\sin \theta \\
0
&=\sin \theta .
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus we need

$$
\cos \theta=1
\qquad \text { and } \qquad
\sin \theta=0,
$$

which happens when

$$
\theta=2 k \pi,
\qquad
k \in \mathbb{Z} .
$$

Therefore the argument is

$$
\arg z_{1}=\{2 k \pi: k \in \mathbb{Z}\} .
$$

Among these values, the one in the interval $(-\pi, \pi]$ is

$$
\operatorname{Arg} z_{1}=0 .
$$

The polar form is

$$
z_{1}=r(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)=5(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta),
\qquad
\theta=2 k \pi,\ k \in \mathbb{Z} .
$$

In particular, using the principal argument $\theta=0$,

$$
\begin{aligned}
z_{1}
&=5(\cos 0+i \sin 0) \\
&=5(1+i \cdot 0) \\
&=5 .
\end{aligned}
$$

So the final answers are:

$$
\left|z_{1}\right|=5,
\qquad
\arg z_{1}=2 k \pi \text { for } k \in \mathbb{Z},
\qquad
\operatorname{Arg} z_{1}=0,
$$

and

$$
z_{1}=5(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta),
\qquad
\theta=2 k \pi,\ k \in \mathbb{Z} .
$$

##### problem 1

Assume $z\neq 0$, since the argument is undefined at $z=0$.

We use the polar form

$$
z=r(\cos \theta+i\sin \theta), \qquad r=|z|>0,
$$

the conjugation rule

$$
\overline{a+ib}=a-ib,
$$

and the angle identities

$$
\cos(-\theta)=\cos\theta, \qquad \sin(-\theta)=-\sin\theta,
$$

$$
\cos(\theta+\pi)=-\cos\theta, \qquad \sin(\theta+\pi)=-\sin\theta.
$$

Also,

$$
-\arg z=\{-\theta:\theta\in\arg z\}, \qquad \arg z+\pi=\{\theta+\pi:\theta\in\arg z\}.
$$

For (a), let $\theta\in\arg z$. Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
z
&=r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta), \\
\overline{z}
&=\overline{r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)}, \\
&=r(\cos\theta-i\sin\theta), \\
&=r(\cos(-\theta)+i\sin(-\theta)).
\end{aligned}
$$

So $-\theta\in\arg \overline{z}$. Therefore

$$
\arg \overline{z}\supseteq -\arg z.
$$

Conversely, let $\phi\in\arg \overline{z}$. Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
\overline{z}
&=r(\cos\phi+i\sin\phi), \\
z
&=\overline{r(\cos\phi+i\sin\phi)}, \\
&=r(\cos\phi-i\sin\phi), \\
&=r(\cos(-\phi)+i\sin(-\phi)).
\end{aligned}
$$

So $-\phi\in\arg z$, which means $\phi\in-\arg z$. Therefore

$$
\arg \overline{z}\subseteq -\arg z.
$$

Combining the two inclusions gives

$$
\arg \overline{z}=-\arg z.
$$

For (b), let $\theta\in\arg z$. Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
z
&=r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta), \\
-z
&=-r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta), \\
&=r(-\cos\theta-i\sin\theta), \\
&=r(\cos(\theta+\pi)+i\sin(\theta+\pi)).
\end{aligned}
$$

So $\theta+\pi\in\arg(-z)$. Therefore

$$
\arg(-z)\supseteq \arg z+\pi.
$$

Conversely, let $\phi\in\arg(-z)$. Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
-z
&=r(\cos\phi+i\sin\phi), \\
z
&=-r(\cos\phi+i\sin\phi), \\
&=r(-\cos\phi-i\sin\phi), \\
&=r(\cos(\phi-\pi)+i\sin(\phi-\pi)).
\end{aligned}
$$

So $\phi-\pi\in\arg z$, which means $\phi\in\arg z+\pi$. Therefore

$$
\arg(-z)\subseteq \arg z+\pi.
$$

Hence

$$
\arg(-z)=\arg z+\pi.
$$




##### problem 2

We use the formulas

$$
\tan\theta=\frac{y}{x}\qquad (x\neq 0),
$$

$$
\tan(\theta+k\pi)=\tan\theta \qquad (k\in\mathbb{Z}),
$$

and the principal range of the inverse tangent

$$
\tan^{-1}(u)\in\left(-\frac{\pi}{2},\frac{\pi}{2}\right).
$$

The difficulty is that the tangent function is not one-to-one on all of $\mathbb{R}$. Different angles can have the same tangent value. For example,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\theta
&=\frac{4\pi}{3}, \\
\tan\theta
&=\tan\left(\pi+\frac{\pi}{3}\right), \\
&=\tan\frac{\pi}{3}, \\
&=\sqrt{3}.
\end{aligned}
$$

But

$$
\tan^{-1}(\sqrt{3})=\frac{\pi}{3}\neq \frac{4\pi}{3}.
$$

So the formula

$$
\theta=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)
$$

does not determine $\theta$ in general. It returns only the representative in the interval

$$
\left(-\frac{\pi}{2},\frac{\pi}{2}\right).
$$

The property that fixes the problem is the $\pi$-periodicity of the tangent:

$$
\tan(\theta+k\pi)=\tan\theta.
$$

Therefore, if

$$
\tan\theta=\frac{y}{x},
$$

then the full family of solutions is

$$
\theta=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)+k\pi, \qquad k\in\mathbb{Z}.
$$

The correct value of $k$ is chosen so that $\theta$ lands in the correct quadrant. If we want the principal value, we also require

$$
-\pi<\theta\leq\pi.
$$

Since also

$$
\theta=\alpha+k\pi, \qquad k\in\mathbb{Z}.

$$

$$
\frac{y}{x}\leq 0,

so

$$
-\frac{\pi}{2}<\alpha\leq 0.
$$

Adding $\pi$ gives

$$
\frac{\pi}{2}<\alpha+\pi\leq\pi.
$$

**That is exactly the principal-value interval for quadrant II together with the point on the negative real axis.** Hence we choose $k=1$, and

$$
\operatorname{Arg} z=\alpha+\pi=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)+\pi.

If $x<0$ and $y<0$, then

$$
\frac{y}{x}>0,

so

$$
0<\alpha<\frac{\pi}{2}.

Subtracting $\pi$ gives

$$
-\pi<\alpha-\pi<-\frac{\pi}{2}.

We use the principal-value restriction

$$
-\pi<\operatorname{Arg} z\leq\pi,
$$

and the polar-coordinate identities

$$
\cos\theta=\frac{x}{r}, \qquad \sin\theta=\frac{y}{r}.

z=iy

r=|z|=|y|.

\frac{y}{|y|}=1,
$$

\cos\theta=0, \qquad \sin\theta=1,
$$

which gives the principal angle

$$
\theta=\frac{\pi}{2}.

$$
\frac{y}{|y|}=-1,
$$

so

$$
\cos\theta=0, \qquad \sin\theta=-1,
$$

which gives the principal angle

$$
\theta=-\frac{\pi}{2}.
$$

Therefore, when $x=0$ and $z\neq 0$,

$$
\operatorname{Arg} z=\operatorname{Arg}(iy)=
\begin{cases}
\frac{\pi}{2}, & \text{if } y>0, \\[6pt]
-\frac{\pi}{2}, & \text{if } y<0.
\end{cases}
$$

$$
\begin{gathered}

\arg (-z)=\arg z+\pi

It is tempting to compute the argument $\theta$ of a complex number $z=x+i y$ by taking the inverse tangent on both sides of (6) and writing $\theta=\tan ^{-1}\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)$. This formula is true only if $\theta$ is in the interval $\left(-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right)$, because the inverse tangent takes its values in the interval $\left(-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right)$ (see Figure 3); and so it will not yield values of $\theta$ that are outside this interval. For example, if $\theta=\frac{4 \pi}{3}$, from Table 1 we have $\tan \frac{4 \pi}{3}=\sqrt{3}$. However, $\tan ^{-1} \sqrt{3}=\frac{\pi}{3}$ and not $\frac{4 \pi}{3}$. To overcome this problem, recall that the tangent is $\pi$-periodic. That means, for all $\theta$,

$$
\tan \theta=\tan (\theta+k \pi) \quad(k=0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots) .
$$

##### problem 1

Let

$$
z_{1}=r_{1}\left(\cos \theta_{1}+i \sin \theta_{1}\right),
\qquad
z_{2}=r_{2}\left(\cos \theta_{2}+i \sin \theta_{2}\right),
\qquad
r_{1}>0,\ r_{2}>0,
$$

with $\theta_{1}\in\arg z_{1}$ and $\theta_{2}\in\arg z_{2}$.

To derive the polar form of the product, expand directly and then use the angle-addition identities.

$$
\begin{aligned}
z_{1} z_{2}
&=\left[r_{1}\left(\cos \theta_{1}+i \sin \theta_{1}\right)\right]\left[r_{2}\left(\cos \theta_{2}+i \sin \theta_{2}\right)\right] \\
&=r_{1} r_{2}\left(\cos \theta_{1}+i \sin \theta_{1}\right)\left(\cos \theta_{2}+i \sin \theta_{2}\right) \\
&=r_{1} r_{2}\left(\cos \theta_{1} \cos \theta_{2}+i \cos \theta_{1} \sin \theta_{2}+i \sin \theta_{1} \cos \theta_{2}+i^{2} \sin \theta_{1} \sin \theta_{2}\right) \\
&=r_{1} r_{2}\left(\cos \theta_{1} \cos \theta_{2}+i \cos \theta_{1} \sin \theta_{2}+i \sin \theta_{1} \cos \theta_{2}-\sin \theta_{1} \sin \theta_{2}\right) \\
&=r_{1} r_{2}\left[\left(\cos \theta_{1} \cos \theta_{2}-\sin \theta_{1} \sin \theta_{2}\right)+i\left(\sin \theta_{1} \cos \theta_{2}+\cos \theta_{1} \sin \theta_{2}\right)\right] \\
&=r_{1} r_{2}\left[\cos \left(\theta_{1}+\theta_{2}\right)+i \sin \left(\theta_{1}+\theta_{2}\right)\right].
\end{aligned}
$$

So the polar form of the product is

$$
z_{1} z_{2}=r_{1} r_{2}\left[\cos \left(\theta_{1}+\theta_{2}\right)+i \sin \left(\theta_{1}+\theta_{2}\right)\right].
$$

This immediately gives the modulus:

$$
\left|z_{1} z_{2}\right|=r_{1} r_{2}=\left|z_{1}\right|\left|z_{2}\right|.
$$

For the argument, write the full argument sets explicitly:

$$
\arg z_{1}=\left\{\theta_{1}+2 m \pi: m \in \mathbb{Z}\right\},
\qquad
\arg z_{2}=\left\{\theta_{2}+2 n \pi: n \in \mathbb{Z}\right\}.
$$

Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
\arg \left(z_{1} z_{2}\right)
&=\left\{\theta_{1}+\theta_{2}+2 k \pi: k \in \mathbb{Z}\right\} \\
&=\left\{\theta_{1}+\theta_{2}+2(m+n)\pi: m,n \in \mathbb{Z}\right\} \\
&=\left\{\left(\theta_{1}+2 m \pi\right)+\left(\theta_{2}+2 n \pi\right): m,n \in \mathbb{Z}\right\} \\
&=\arg z_{1}+\arg z_{2}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus multiplication in polar form does two things at once: it multiplies the moduli and adds the arguments. If one uses principal arguments instead of full argument sets, then

$$
\operatorname{Arg}\left(z_{1} z_{2}\right)
$$

is the unique number in $(-\pi,\pi]$ congruent to

$$
\operatorname{Arg} z_{1}+\operatorname{Arg} z_{2}
$$

modulo $2\pi$.

The following plot illustrates this with

$$
z_{1}=2\left(\cos \frac{\pi}{6}+i \sin \frac{\pi}{6}\right),
\qquad
z_{2}=\frac{3}{2}\left(\cos \frac{\pi}{3}+i \sin \frac{\pi}{3}\right),
$$

for which

$$
z_{1} z_{2}=3\left(\cos \frac{\pi}{2}+i \sin \frac{\pi}{2}\right)=3 i .
$$

##### problem 2

Assume $z \neq 0$, and write

$$
z=r(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta),
\qquad
r=|z|>0,
\qquad
\theta\in\arg z.
$$

Now compute the reciprocal directly.

$$
\begin{aligned}
z^{-1}
&=\frac{1}{z} \\
&=\frac{1}{r(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)} \\
&=\frac{1}{r} \cdot \frac{1}{\cos \theta+i \sin \theta} \\
&=\frac{1}{r} \cdot \frac{\cos \theta-i \sin \theta}{(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)(\cos \theta-i \sin \theta)} \\
&=\frac{1}{r} \cdot \frac{\cos \theta-i \sin \theta}{\cos ^{2} \theta-i \cos \theta \sin \theta+i \sin \theta \cos \theta-i^{2} \sin ^{2} \theta} \\
&=\frac{1}{r} \cdot \frac{\cos \theta-i \sin \theta}{\cos ^{2} \theta+\sin ^{2} \theta} \\
&=\frac{1}{r}\left(\cos \theta-i \sin \theta\right) \\
&=\frac{1}{r}\left(\cos (-\theta)+i \sin (-\theta)\right).
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore the polar form of the inverse is

$$
z^{-1}=\frac{1}{r}\left(\cos (-\theta)+i \sin (-\theta)\right).
$$

So the modulus and argument behave as follows:

$$
\left|z^{-1}\right|=\frac{1}{r}=\frac{1}{|z|},
$$

and

$$
\arg \left(z^{-1}\right)=-\arg z.
$$

More explicitly, if

$$
\arg z=\left\{\theta+2 k \pi: k \in \mathbb{Z}\right\},
$$

then

$$
\begin{aligned}
\arg \left(z^{-1}\right)
&=\left\{-\theta+2 k \pi: k \in \mathbb{Z}\right\} \\
&=\left\{-(\theta+2 m \pi): m \in \mathbb{Z}\right\} \\
&=-\arg z.
\end{aligned}
$$

For principal arguments, the clean statement is that

$$
\operatorname{Arg}\left(z^{-1}\right)
$$

is the unique number in $(-\pi,\pi]$ congruent to

$$
-\operatorname{Arg} z
$$

modulo $2\pi$.

Let

\qquad

z_{2}^{-1}=\frac{1}{r_{2}}\left(\cos (-\theta_{2})+i \sin (-\theta_{2})\right).
$$

Now use

$$
\frac{z_{1}}{z_{2}}=z_{1} z_{2}^{-1},
$$

and then apply the product formula from Problem 1.

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{z_{1}}{z_{2}}
&=z_{1} z_{2}^{-1} \\
&=\left[r_{1}\left(\cos \theta_{1}+i \sin \theta_{1}\right)\right]\left[\frac{1}{r_{2}}\left(\cos (-\theta_{2})+i \sin (-\theta_{2})\right)\right] \\
&=\frac{r_{1}}{r_{2}}\left(\cos \theta_{1}+i \sin \theta_{1}\right)\left(\cos (-\theta_{2})+i \sin (-\theta_{2})\right) \\
&=\frac{r_{1}}{r_{2}}\left[\cos \left(\theta_{1}+(-\theta_{2})\right)+i \sin \left(\theta_{1}+(-\theta_{2})\right)\right] \\
&=\frac{r_{1}}{r_{2}}\left[\cos \left(\theta_{1}-\theta_{2}\right)+i \sin \left(\theta_{1}-\theta_{2}\right)\right].
\end{aligned}
$$

So the polar form of the quotient is

$$
\frac{z_{1}}{z_{2}}=\frac{r_{1}}{r_{2}}\left[\cos \left(\theta_{1}-\theta_{2}\right)+i \sin \left(\theta_{1}-\theta_{2}\right)\right].
$$

Hence

$$
\left|\frac{z_{1}}{z_{2}}\right|=\frac{r_{1}}{r_{2}}=\frac{|z_{1}|}{|z_{2}|}.
$$

For the arguments,

$$
\arg z_{1}=\left\{\theta_{1}+2 m \pi: m \in \mathbb{Z}\right\},
\qquad
\arg z_{2}=\left\{\theta_{2}+2 n \pi: n \in \mathbb{Z}\right\},
$$

so

$$
\begin{aligned}
\arg \left(\frac{z_{1}}{z_{2}}\right)
&=\left\{\theta_{1}-\theta_{2}+2 k \pi: k \in \mathbb{Z}\right\} \\
&=\left\{\theta_{1}-\theta_{2}+2(m-n)\pi: m,n \in \mathbb{Z}\right\} \\
&=\left\{\left(\theta_{1}+2 m \pi\right)-\left(\theta_{2}+2 n \pi\right): m,n \in \mathbb{Z}\right\} \\
&=\arg z_{1}-\arg z_{2}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus division in polar form divides the moduli and subtracts the arguments. If one uses principal arguments instead of full argument sets, then

$$
\operatorname{Arg}\left(\frac{z_{1}}{z_{2}}\right)
$$

is the unique number in $(-\pi,\pi]$ congruent to

$$
\operatorname{Arg} z_{1}-\operatorname{Arg} z_{2}
$$

modulo $2\pi$.

$$

The polar form of complex numbers gives us a new insight into multiplication and related operations on complex numbers. Let $z_{1}=r_{1}\left(\cos \theta_{1}+i \sin \theta_{1}\right)$ and $z_{2}=r_{2}\left(\cos \theta_{2}+i \sin \theta_{2}\right)$ be two nonzero complex numbers. We compute their product directly, using $i^{2}=-1$ :

$$
\begin{aligned}
z_{1} z_{2} & =r_{1}\left(\cos \theta_{1}+i \sin \theta_{1}\right) r_{2}\left(\cos \theta_{2}+i \sin \theta_{2}\right) \\
& =r_{1} r_{2}\left[\left(\cos \theta_{1} \cos \theta_{2}-\sin \theta_{1} \sin \theta_{2}\right)+i\left(\sin \theta_{1} \cos \theta_{2}+\cos \theta_{1} \sin \theta_{2}\right)\right]
\end{aligned}
$$

We recognize the trigonometric expressions as the sum angle formulas for the cosine and sine:

$$
\cos \left(\theta_{1}+\theta_{2}\right)=\cos \theta_{1} \cos \theta_{2}-\sin \theta_{1} \sin \theta_{2}
$$

and

$$
\sin \left(\theta_{1}+\theta_{2}\right)=\sin \theta_{1} \cos \theta_{2}+\cos \theta_{1} \sin \theta_{2} .
$$

Therefore, the polar form of the product

$$
z_{1} z_{2}=r_{1} r_{2}\left[\cos \left(\theta_{1}+\theta_{2}\right)+i \sin \left(\theta_{1}+\theta_{2}\right)\right] .
$$

By taking the modulus of both sides of (16), we have

$$
\left|z_{1} z_{2}\right|=\left|z_{1}\right|\left|z_{2}\right|
$$

which is a fact we already know from Section 1.2. By taking the argument of both sides of (16), we obtain

$$
\arg \left(z_{1} z_{2}\right)=\theta_{1}+\theta_{2}=\arg z_{1}+\arg z_{2} .
$$

More precisely,

$$
\arg \left(z_{1} z_{2}\right)=\left\{\theta_{1}+\theta_{2}+2 k \pi: k=0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots\right\} .
$$

Identities (17) and (18) tell us that when we multiply two complex numbers in polar form, we multiply their moduli and add their arguments. See Figure 5.

> Figure 5 To multiply in polar form, add the arguments and multiply the moduli.



Suppose that $z_{1} \neq 0$, and $z_{2}=z_{1}^{-1}$ in (16). Since $z_{1} z_{2}=z_{1} z_{1}^{-1}=1$, we obtain

$$
1=r_{1} r_{2}\left[\cos \left(\theta_{1}+\theta_{2}\right)+i \sin \left(\theta_{1}+\theta_{2}\right)\right] .
$$

Hence from (8), $r_{1} r_{2}=1$ and $\theta_{1}+\theta_{2}=0$, because the modulus of 1 is 1 and its argument is 0 . Hence $r_{2}=\frac{1}{r_{1}}$ and $\theta_{2}=-\theta_{1}$. So the polar form of the inverse of a complex number $z$ is given by

$$
z^{-1}=\frac{1}{r}(\cos (-\theta)+i \sin (-\theta))=\frac{1}{r}(\cos \theta-i \sin \theta) .
$$

Consider now arbitrary $z_{1}$ and $z_{2} \neq 0$. Since $\frac{z_{1}}{z_{2}}=z_{1} z_{2}^{-1}$, then by (16) and (19), we obtain the polar form of a quotient

$$
\frac{z_{1}}{z_{2}}=\frac{r_{1}}{r_{2}}\left(\cos \left(\theta_{1}-\theta_{2}\right)+i \sin \left(\theta_{1}-\theta_{2}\right)\right)
$$

Thus to divide two complex numbers in polar form, we divide their moduli and subtract their arguments (Figure 6).

## De Moivre's Identity

> [!review] Review 5
> Describe the pattern that leads to De Moivre's identity. State and prove the identity. Then extend it to compute $z^n$ for a complex number $z=r(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)$ of arbitrary modulus.




The pattern comes directly from the multiplication rule in polar form. If

$$
z=\cos \theta+i \sin \theta,
$$

then $|z|=1$, so each time we multiply by another copy of $z$, the modulus stays equal to $1$ while the argument increases by another $\theta$. Writing out the first few powers makes the pattern visible:

$$
\begin{aligned}
z
&=\cos \theta+i \sin \theta, \\
z^{2}
&=z \cdot z, \\
&=(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta), \\
&=\cos 2 \theta+i \sin 2 \theta, \\
z^{3}
&=z^{2} \cdot z, \\
&=(\cos 2 \theta+i \sin 2 \theta)(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta), \\
&=\cos 3 \theta+i \sin 3 \theta, \\
z^{4}
&=z^{3} \cdot z, \\
&=(\cos 3 \theta+i \sin 3 \theta)(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta), \\
&=\cos 4 \theta+i \sin 4 \theta.
\end{aligned}
$$

So the pattern is

$$
(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)^{n}=\cos n \theta+i \sin n \theta.
$$

This is De Moivre's identity: For every positive integer $n$ and every real number $\theta$,

$$\boxed{(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)^n=\cos n \theta+i \sin n \theta.}$$

We now prove the identity by mathematical induction on $n$.

For the base case $n=1$,

$$
\begin{aligned}
(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)^{1}
&=\cos \theta+i \sin \theta \\
&=\cos (1 \cdot \theta)+i \sin (1 \cdot \theta).
\end{aligned}
$$

So the formula is true when $n=1$.

Now assume that for some positive integer $n$,

$$
(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)^{n}=\cos n \theta+i \sin n \theta.
$$

We must prove that

$$
(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)^{n+1}=\cos (n+1)\theta+i \sin (n+1)\theta.
$$

Using the induction hypothesis and then multiplying explicitly, we get

$$
\begin{aligned}
(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)^{n+1}
&=(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)^{n}(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta) \\
&=(\cos n \theta+i \sin n \theta)(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta) \\
&=\cos n \theta \cos \theta+i \cos n \theta \sin \theta+i \sin n \theta \cos \theta+i^{2} \sin n \theta \sin \theta \\
&=\cos n \theta \cos \theta+i \cos n \theta \sin \theta+i \sin n \theta \cos \theta-\sin n \theta \sin \theta \\
&=\left(\cos n \theta \cos \theta-\sin n \theta \sin \theta\right)+i\left(\sin n \theta \cos \theta+\cos n \theta \sin \theta\right) \\
&=\cos (n \theta+\theta)+i \sin (n \theta+\theta) \\
&=\cos ((n+1)\theta)+i \sin ((n+1)\theta).
\end{aligned}
$$

This is exactly the desired statement for $n+1$. Therefore, by induction, De Moivre's identity holds for every positive integer $n$.

We can now extend the identity to a complex number with modulus $r$. Let

$$
z=r(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta),
\qquad r \geq 0.
$$

If $r>0$, then

$$
\begin{aligned}
z^{n}
&=\left[r(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)\right]^{n} \\
&=r^{n}(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)^{n} \\
&=r^{n}\left(\cos n \theta+i \sin n \theta\right).
\end{aligned}
$$

So for a nonzero complex number in polar form, the $n$th power is obtained by raising the modulus to the $n$th power and multiplying the argument by $n$:

$$
z^{n}=r^{n}\left(\cos n \theta+i \sin n \theta\right).
$$

If $r=0$, then $z=0$, and for every positive integer $n$,

$$
z^{n}=0.
$$

Thus the complete power rule is:

$$
z^{n}=
\begin{cases}
r^{n}\left(\cos n \theta+i \sin n \theta\right), & r>0, \\
0, & r=0.
\end{cases}
$$

In words: powers of complex numbers in polar form are easy to compute because the modulus is raised to the power and the argument is multiplied by that power.

\end{aligned}

We have the following useful identity.



> [!equation] De Moivre's Identity
> For any positive integer $n$ and real number $\theta$,
> 
> $$
> (\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)^{n}=\cos n \theta+i \sin n \theta .
> $$
> 

Proof We prove the statement by mathematical induction. For $n=1$, the statement is true, since we have trivially $(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)^{1}=\cos 1 \cdot \theta+i \sin 1 \cdot \theta$. Now for the inductive step: We assume the statement is true for $n$, and prove that it is true for $n+1$. Let us compute

$$
\begin{aligned}
(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)^{n+1} & =(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)^{n}(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta) \\
& =(\cos n \theta+i \sin n \theta)(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)
\end{aligned}
$$

**(by induction hypothesis)**

$$
=\cos (n+1) \theta+i \sin (n+1) \theta \quad[\text { by }(16)] .
$$

By induction, the statement holds for all positive integers $n$.
We can also use De Moivre's identity to calculate powers of complex numbers of arbitrary modulus $r \neq 0$. For if $z=r(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)$, then

$$
\begin{aligned}
z^{n} & =[r(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)]^{n} \\
& =r^{n}(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)^{n} \\
& =r^{n}(\cos n \theta+i \sin n \theta)
\end{aligned}
$$

$$

When an angle is written in the form

$$

$$

it is often fastest to rewrite $2\pi$ with denominator $n$ first. Since

$$
2\pi=\frac{2n\pi}{n}.
$$

So for an angle of the form $\frac{m\pi}{n}$, one full revolution has the same denominator and becomes $\frac{2n\pi}{n}$. Then we divide $m$ by $2n$:

$$
m=(2n)q+r,
\qquad
0\le r<2n.
$$

This gives

$$
\frac{m\pi}{n}
=\frac{(2n)q\pi+r\pi}{n}
=2q\pi+\frac{r\pi}{n}.
$$

The quotient $q$ tells us how many full copies of $2\pi$ to remove, and the remainder $r$ gives the reduced angle in $[0,2\pi)$.

r>0
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\rho>0,
$$

we take the positive real $n$th root and get

$$
r=\rho^{1/n}.
$$

Now compare arguments. If two nonzero complex numbers are equal, then their arguments differ by an integer multiple of $2\pi$. Therefore

$$
n\theta=\phi+2k\pi,
\qquad
k\in\mathbb{Z}.
$$

Solving for $\theta$ gives

$$
\theta=\frac{\phi}{n}+\frac{2k\pi}{n},
\qquad
k\in\mathbb{Z}.
$$

Thus every root has modulus

$$
r=\rho^{1/n}
$$

and argument

$$
\theta=\frac{\phi}{n}+\frac{2k\pi}{n},
\qquad
k\in\mathbb{Z}.
$$

It remains to explain why only

$$
k=0,1,\ldots,n-1
$$

produce distinct roots.

First, if $k$ is replaced by $k+n$, then

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\phi}{n}+\frac{2(k+n)\pi}{n}
&=\frac{\phi}{n}+\frac{2k\pi}{n}+\frac{2n\pi}{n} \\
&=\frac{\phi}{n}+\frac{2k\pi}{n}+2\pi.
\end{aligned}
$$

But adding $2\pi$ to an argument does not change the complex number. Hence the values $k$ and $k+n$ produce the same root.

More generally, if

$$
k_{2}=k_{1}+mn
\qquad
(m\in\mathbb{Z}),
$$

then

$$
\frac{\phi}{n}+\frac{2k_{2}\pi}{n}
=\frac{\phi}{n}+\frac{2k_{1}\pi}{n}+2m\pi,
$$

so $k_{1}$ and $k_{2}$ give the same root.

Now we show that the roots coming from

$$
k=0,1,\ldots,n-1
$$

are all different. Suppose two of them were equal, say the roots corresponding to $k_{1}$ and $k_{2}$ with

$$
0\le k_{1},k_{2}\le n-1.
$$

Then their arguments would differ by a multiple of $2\pi$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\left(\frac{\phi}{n}+\frac{2k_{1}\pi}{n}\right)-\left(\frac{\phi}{n}+\frac{2k_{2}\pi}{n}\right)
&=2m\pi
\qquad (m\in\mathbb{Z}) \\
\frac{2(k_{1}-k_{2})\pi}{n}
&=2m\pi \\
\frac{k_{1}-k_{2}}{n}
&=m \\
k_{1}-k_{2}
&=mn.
\end{aligned}
$$

But

$$
-(n-1)\le k_{1}-k_{2}\le n-1,
$$

and the only multiple of $n$ in that interval is $0$. Therefore

$$
k_{1}-k_{2}=0,
$$

so

$$
k_{1}=k_{2}.
$$

Thus the $n$ values

$$
k=0,1,\ldots,n-1
$$

give exactly the $n$ distinct roots.

##### problem 3

Combining the modulus and argument from Problem 2, the $n$th roots of

$$
w=\rho(\cos \phi+i\sin \phi),
\qquad
\rho>0,
$$

are

$$
z_{k+1}
=\rho^{1/n}\left[\cos\left(\frac{\phi}{n}+\frac{2k\pi}{n}\right)+i\sin\left(\frac{\phi}{n}+\frac{2k\pi}{n}\right)\right],
\qquad
k=0,1,\ldots,n-1.
$$

The principal $n$th root is defined to be the unique root whose principal argument is

$$
\operatorname{Arg} z=\frac{\operatorname{Arg} w}{n}.
$$

To obtain it from the general formula, choose

$$
\phi=\operatorname{Arg} w
$$

and then take

$$
k=0.
$$

Thus the principal $n$th root is

$$
\rho^{1/n}\left[\cos\left(\frac{\operatorname{Arg} w}{n}\right)+i\sin\left(\frac{\operatorname{Arg} w}{n}\right)\right].
$$

In words, to find the principal root, take the principal argument of $w$, divide that argument by $n$, and use the positive real $n$th root of the modulus.


---

We first define an $n$th root of a complex number.



> [!definition] nth Root of a Complex Number
> Let $w \neq 0$ be a given complex number and $n$ a positive integer. A number $z$ is called an $n$th root of $w$ if $z^{n}=w$.

Our goal is to find the roots of the given number $w$. De Moivre's formula for calculating powers can in essence be worked "backward" to find roots of complex numbers.

Write $w=\rho(\cos \phi+i \sin \phi)$ and $z=r(\cos \theta+i \sin \theta)$. The equation $z^{n}=w$ tells us (using (21)) that

$$
r^{n}(\cos n \theta+i \sin n \theta)=\rho(\cos \phi+i \sin \phi)
$$

Two complex numbers are equal only if their moduli are equal, so $r^{n}=\rho$. Thus, take $r=\rho^{1 / n}$ (meaning the real root of a real number). Also, when two complex numbers are equal, their arguments must differ by $2 k \pi$, where $k$ is an integer. So

$$
n \theta=\phi+2 k \pi, \quad \text { or } \quad \theta=\frac{\phi}{n}+\frac{2 k \pi}{n} .
$$

If we take the values $k=0,1, \ldots, n-1$, we will get $n$ values of $\theta$ that yield $n$ distinct roots of $w$. Any other value of $k$ will produce a root identical to one of these, since when $k$ increases by $n$, the argument of $z$ increases by $2 \pi$. Thus we have a formula for the $n$ roots of a complex number $w$.

### FORMULA FOR THE $\boldsymbol{n}$ th ROOTS

Let $w=\rho(\cos \phi+i \sin \phi) \neq 0$. The $n$th roots of $w$ are the solutions of the equation $z^{n}=w$. They are given by

> [!equation] Formula for the nth Roots of a Complex Number $w$
> 
> $$
> z_{k+1}=\rho^{1 / n}\left[\cos \left(\frac{\phi}{n}+\frac{2 k \pi}{n}\right)+i \sin \left(\frac{\phi}{n}+\frac{2 k \pi}{n}\right)\right]
> $$
> 

$k=0,1, \ldots, n-1$.
The unique number $z$ such that $z^{n}=w$ and $\operatorname{Arg} z=\frac{\operatorname{Arg} w}{n}$ is called the principal $n$th root of $w$. The principal root is obtained from (23) by taking $\phi=\operatorname{Arg} w$ and $k=0$.

> [!review] Review 7
> 1. The quadratic formula was derived in Section 1.1 for real coefficients. Does it still hold when $a, b, c$ are complex? What changes in its interpretation?
> 2. What does $\pm \sqrt{b^2-4 a c}$ mean when $b^2-4 a c$ is complex, and how do you compute it?




##### problem 1

Yes. The quadratic formula still holds when

$$
a,b,c\in\mathbb{C},
\qquad
a\neq 0.
$$

The reason is that the derivation uses only algebraic operations that are valid in the complex numbers: addition, subtraction, multiplication, division by a nonzero number, and taking square roots at the final step.

Let

$$
az^2+bz+c=0,
\qquad
a\neq 0.
$$

Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
az^2+bz+c
&=0, \\
az^2+bz
&=-c, \\
z^2+\frac{b}{a}z
&=-\frac{c}{a}, \\
z^2+\frac{b}{a}z+\left(\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2
&=-\frac{c}{a}+\left(\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2, \\
\left(z+\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2
&=-\frac{c}{a}+\frac{b^2}{4a^2}, \\
\left(z+\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2
&=\frac{-4ac+b^2}{4a^2}, \\
\left(z+\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2
&=\frac{b^2-4ac}{4a^2}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Now take square roots:

$$
z+\frac{b}{2a}=\pm \sqrt{\frac{b^2-4ac}{4a^2}}.
$$

Since

$$
\sqrt{4a^2}=2a
$$

up to the usual choice of sign already absorbed by the symbol $\pm$, this becomes

$$
z+\frac{b}{2a}=\pm \frac{\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
z
&=-\frac{b}{2a}\pm \frac{\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} \\
&=\frac{-b\pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}.
\end{aligned}
$$

So the formula itself does not change:

$$
z=\frac{-b\pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}.
$$

What changes is the interpretation of the square root. Over the real numbers, a negative discriminant has no real square root. Over the complex numbers, every nonzero complex number has two complex square roots, and $0$ has one square root, namely $0$. Thus the quadratic formula always makes sense in $\mathbb{C}$.

##### problem 2

Let

$$
\Delta=b^2-4ac.
$$

When $\Delta$ is complex, the symbol

$$
\pm\sqrt{\Delta}
$$

means the two complex numbers whose squares equal $\Delta$.

More precisely:

1. If
   $$
   \Delta=0,
   $$
   then
   $$
   \sqrt{\Delta}=0,
   $$
   so the two signs give the same value, and the quadratic has the repeated root
   $$
   z=-\frac{b}{2a}.
   $$

2. If
   $$
   \Delta\neq 0,
   $$
   write $\Delta$ in polar form:
   $$
   \Delta=\rho(\cos\phi+i\sin\phi),
   \qquad
   \rho=|\Delta|>0.
   $$

To compute the square roots, solve

$$
w^2=\Delta.
$$

Write

$$
w=r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta).
$$

Then by De Moivre's identity,

$$
\begin{aligned}
w^2
&=\left[r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)\right]^2 \\
&=r^2(\cos 2\theta+i\sin 2\theta).
\end{aligned}
$$

Since

$$
w^2=\Delta=\rho(\cos\phi+i\sin\phi),
$$

we must have

$$
r^2=\rho
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
2\theta=\phi+2k\pi,
\quad
k\in\mathbb{Z}.
$$

Thus

$$
r=\rho^{1/2}
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\theta=\frac{\phi}{2}+k\pi.
$$

So the two square roots are obtained by taking

$$
k=0
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
k=1:
$$

$$
\begin{aligned}
w_1
&=\rho^{1/2}\left(\cos\frac{\phi}{2}+i\sin\frac{\phi}{2}\right), \\
w_2
&=\rho^{1/2}\left(\cos\left(\frac{\phi}{2}+\pi\right)+i\sin\left(\frac{\phi}{2}+\pi\right)\right).
\end{aligned}
$$

Using

$$
\cos(\alpha+\pi)=-\cos\alpha,
\qquad
\sin(\alpha+\pi)=-\sin\alpha,
$$

we get

$$
\begin{aligned}
w_2
&=\rho^{1/2}\left(-\cos\frac{\phi}{2}-i\sin\frac{\phi}{2}\right) \\
&=-\rho^{1/2}\left(\cos\frac{\phi}{2}+i\sin\frac{\phi}{2}\right) \\
&=-w_1.
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore

$$
\pm\sqrt{\Delta}
=
\pm \rho^{1/2}\left(\cos\frac{\phi}{2}+i\sin\frac{\phi}{2}\right).
$$

Substituting into the quadratic formula gives the two solutions

$$
z=\frac{-b\pm \rho^{1/2}\left(\cos\frac{\phi}{2}+i\sin\frac{\phi}{2}\right)}{2a}.
$$

So when $b^2-4ac$ is complex, you compute $\pm\sqrt{b^2-4ac}$ by:

$$
\text{(i) finding the polar form of } \Delta,
\qquad
\text{(ii) taking the square root of the modulus,}
\qquad
\text{(iii) halving the argument.}
$$

The second square root is just the negative of the first.

$$
\frac{64 \pi}{3}=\frac{60 \pi}{3}+\frac{4 \pi}{3}=20 \pi+\frac{4 \pi}{3},
$$

$$
z=\frac{1}{2}\left(\cos \frac{4 \pi}{3}+i \sin \frac{4 \pi}{3}\right)=\frac{1}{2}\left(-\frac{1}{2}-i \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right)=-\frac{1}{4}-i \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}.
$$

Thus the point is

$$
\left(-\frac{1}{4},-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}\right),
$$

which lies in the third quadrant.

$$
\arg \bar{z}=-\arg z.

$$

& =r(-\cos \theta-i \sin \theta) \\

\end{aligned}

\arg (-z)=\{\theta+\pi+2 k \pi: k \in \mathbb{Z}\}.

$$

$$

$$

> 27. Show that
> 
> $$
> \left(\frac{1+i \tan \theta}{1-i \tan \theta}\right)^{n}=\frac{1+i \tan n \theta}{1-i \tan n \theta}
> $$
> 

Assume both sides are defined.

Start with the left-hand fraction:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{1+i \tan \theta}{1-i \tan \theta}
& =\frac{1+i \frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta}}{1-i \frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta}} \\
& =\frac{\frac{\cos \theta+i \sin \theta}{\cos \theta}}{\frac{\cos \theta-i \sin \theta}{\cos \theta}} \\
& =\frac{\cos \theta+i \sin \theta}{\cos \theta-i \sin \theta} \\
& =\frac{\cos \theta+i \sin \theta}{\cos (-\theta)+i \sin (-\theta)} \\
& =\cos (\theta-(-\theta))+i \sin (\theta-(-\theta)) \\
& =\cos 2 \theta+i \sin 2 \theta.
\end{aligned}
$$

Raise both sides to the $n$th power:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\left(\frac{1+i \tan \theta}{1-i \tan \theta}\right)^{n}
& =\left(\cos 2 \theta+i \sin 2 \theta\right)^{n} \\
& =\cos 2 n \theta+i \sin 2 n \theta.
\end{aligned}
$$

Now simplify the right-hand fraction:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{1+i \tan n \theta}{1-i \tan n \theta}
& =\frac{1+i \frac{\sin n \theta}{\cos n \theta}}{1-i \frac{\sin n \theta}{\cos n \theta}} \\
& =\frac{\frac{\cos n \theta+i \sin n \theta}{\cos n \theta}}{\frac{\cos n \theta-i \sin n \theta}{\cos n \theta}} \\
& =\frac{\cos n \theta+i \sin n \theta}{\cos n \theta-i \sin n \theta} \\
& =\frac{\cos n \theta+i \sin n \theta}{\cos (-n \theta)+i \sin (-n \theta)} \\
& =\cos (n \theta-(-n \theta))+i \sin (n \theta-(-n \theta)) \\
& =\cos 2 n \theta+i \sin 2 n \theta.
\end{aligned}
$$

Both sides are equal to $\cos 2 n \theta+i \sin 2 n \theta$, so

$$
\left(\frac{1+i \tan \theta}{1-i \tan \theta}\right)^{n}=\frac{1+i \tan n \theta}{1-i \tan n \theta}.
$$

> [!exercise] Exercise 18
> 30. (a) Show that if $z_{1}, z_{2}, \ldots, z_{n}$ are all unimodular, then so is $z_{1} z_{2} \cdots z_{n}$.
> (b) Let $n$ be a positive integer and $z$ a nonzero complex number. Show that $z$ is unimodular if and only if all its $n$th roots are unimodular.

##### part (a)

If each $z_{j}$ is unimodular, then $|z_{j}|=1$ for $j=1,2, \ldots, n$. By the product formula for moduli,

$$
\left|z_{1} z_{2} \cdots z_{n}\right|=\left|z_{1}\right|\left|z_{2}\right| \cdots\left|z_{n}\right|=1 \cdot 1 \cdots 1=1.
$$

Hence $z_{1} z_{2} \cdots z_{n}$ is unimodular.

##### part (b)

First suppose that $z$ is unimodular, so $|z|=1$. Let $w$ be any $n$th root of $z$, so

$$
w^{n}=z.
$$

Taking moduli,

$$
\left|w^{n}\right|=|z|=1.
$$

Since $\left|w^{n}\right|=|w|^{n}$, we get

$$
|w|^{n}=1.
$$

Because $|w| \geq 0$, it follows that

$$
|w|=1.
$$

So every $n$th root of $z$ is unimodular.

Conversely, suppose that all $n$th roots of $z$ are unimodular. Let $w$ be one of these roots. Then

$$
w^{n}=z
$$

and

$$
|w|=1.
$$

Therefore

$$
|z|=\left|w^{n}\right|=|w|^{n}=1^{n}=1.
$$

Hence $z$ is unimodular.

Thus $z$ is unimodular if and only if all its $n$th roots are unimodular.

> 53. Summing roots of unity. Let $\omega_{1}, \omega_{2}, \ldots, \omega_{n}$ denote the $n$th roots of unity where $n \geq 2$; that is $\omega_{j}^{n}=1$ for $j=1,2, \ldots, n$. Pick and fix any root $\omega_{0} \neq 1$ from the set $\left(\omega_{j}\right)_{j=1}^{n}$.
> (a) Show that $\omega_{0} \omega_{j}$ is an $n$th root of unity for $j=1,2, \ldots, n$. **Hint: Verify the equation $z^{n}=1$.**
> (b) Show that $\omega_{0} \omega_{j} \neq \omega_{0} \omega_{k}$ if $j \neq k$. Conclude that the set $\left(\omega_{0} \omega_{j}\right)_{j=1}^{n}$ is the same as the set of all $n$ roots of unity.
> (c) Show that the sum of the $n$ roots of unity is zero. That is, show that
> 
> $$
> \omega_{1}+\omega_{2}+\cdots+\omega_{n}=0 .
> $$
> 
> (**Hint: $\omega_{1}+\omega_{2}+\cdots+\omega_{n}=\omega_{0} \omega_{1}+\omega_{0} \omega_{2}+\cdots+\omega_{0} \omega_{n}$; why? Factor $\omega_{0}$ and conclude that the sum has to be 0** .)

>

> $$

##### part (a)

For each $j=1,2,\ldots,n$, we have

$$
\omega_{j}^{n}=1
$$

and, because $\omega_{0}$ is also an $n$th root of unity,

$$
\omega_{0}^{n}=1.
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
\left(\omega_{0}\omega_{j}\right)^{n}
& =\omega_{0}^{n}\omega_{j}^{n} \\
& =1 \cdot 1 \\
& =1.
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence $\omega_{0}\omega_{j}$ is an $n$th root of unity.

##### part (b)

Suppose

$$
\omega_{0}\omega_{j}=\omega_{0}\omega_{k}.
$$

Since $\omega_{0}\neq 0$, we may divide by $\omega_{0}$ and obtain

$$
\omega_{j}=\omega_{k}.
$$

Thus if $j\neq k$, then

$$
\omega_{0}\omega_{j}\neq \omega_{0}\omega_{k}.
$$

So the numbers

$$
\omega_{0}\omega_{1},\ \omega_{0}\omega_{2},\ \ldots,\ \omega_{0}\omega_{n}
$$

are $n$ distinct $n$th roots of unity. Since there are exactly $n$ roots of $z^{n}=1$, the set

$$
\left(\omega_{0}\omega_{j}\right)_{j=1}^{n}
$$

must be the same as the set

$$
\left(\omega_{j}\right)_{j=1}^{n}.
$$

##### part (c)

Let

$$
S=\omega_{1}+\omega_{2}+\cdots+\omega_{n}.
$$

By part (b), multiplication by $\omega_{0}$ only permutes the set of roots, so

$$
S=\omega_{0}\omega_{1}+\omega_{0}\omega_{2}+\cdots+\omega_{0}\omega_{n}.
$$

Factor out $\omega_{0}$:

$$
S=\omega_{0}\left(\omega_{1}+\omega_{2}+\cdots+\omega_{n}\right)=\omega_{0}S.
$$

Hence

$$
(1-\omega_{0})S=0.
$$

Since $\omega_{0}\neq 1$, we have $1-\omega_{0}\neq 0$, so it follows that

$$
S=0.
$$

Therefore

$$
\omega_{1}+\omega_{2}+\cdots+\omega_{n}=0.
$$

##### part (d)

$$

\begin{aligned}
(1-\omega_{0})T

& =1-1 \\
& =0.
\end{aligned}

##### part (a)

Starting from

$$
z^{n}=(z+1)^{n},
$$

note first that $z+1 \neq 0$, for otherwise $z=-1$ and the equation would give

$$
(-1)^{n}=0,
$$

which is impossible. Hence we may divide by $(z+1)^{n}$:

$$
\left(\frac{z}{z+1}\right)^{n}=1.
$$

Therefore

$$
\frac{z}{z+1}
$$

must be an $n$th root of unity. So

$$
\frac{z}{z+1}=\omega_{k}=\cos \left(\frac{2 k \pi}{n}\right)+i \sin \left(\frac{2 k \pi}{n}\right),
\qquad k=0,1,\ldots,n-1.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
z=(z+1)\omega_{k}.
$$

We must exclude $k=0$, because $\omega_{0}=1$ would give

$$
z=z+1,
$$

which is impossible. Thus

$$
k=1,2,\ldots,n-1.
$$

##### part (b)

Write

$$
z=x+i y
$$

and let

$$
\alpha=\frac{2 k \pi}{n}.
$$

Then

$$
\omega_{k}=\cos \alpha+i \sin \alpha,
$$

so the equation $z=(z+1)\omega_k$ becomes

$$
x+i y=(x+1+i y)(\cos \alpha+i \sin \alpha).
$$

Expand the right-hand side:

$$
\begin{aligned}
x+i y
& =(x+1)\cos \alpha+i y \cos \alpha+i(x+1)\sin \alpha+i^{2} y \sin \alpha \\
& =\bigl((x+1)\cos \alpha-y \sin \alpha\bigr)+i\bigl(y \cos \alpha+(x+1)\sin \alpha\bigr).
\end{aligned}
$$

Equating real and imaginary parts gives

$$
x=(x+1)\cos \alpha-y \sin \alpha
$$

and

$$
y=y \cos \alpha+(x+1)\sin \alpha.
$$

From the second equation,

$$
\begin{aligned}
y-y \cos \alpha
& =(x+1)\sin \alpha \\
y(1-\cos \alpha)
& =(x+1)\sin \alpha.
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence

$$
y=\frac{(x+1)\sin \alpha}{1-\cos \alpha}.
$$

Substitute this into the first equation:

$$
\begin{aligned}
x
& =(x+1)\cos \alpha-\frac{(x+1)\sin ^{2} \alpha}{1-\cos \alpha}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Use

$$
\sin ^{2} \alpha=(1-\cos \alpha)(1+\cos \alpha)
$$

to obtain

$$
\begin{aligned}
x
& =(x+1)\cos \alpha-(x+1)(1+\cos \alpha) \\
& =-x-1.
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore

$$
2x=-1,
$$

so

$$
x=-\frac{1}{2}.
$$

Now return to

$$
y(1-\cos \alpha)=(x+1)\sin \alpha
$$

and substitute $x=-\frac{1}{2}$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
y(1-\cos \alpha)
& =\frac{1}{2}\sin \alpha,
\end{aligned}
$$

which gives

$$
y=\frac{\sin \alpha}{2(1-\cos \alpha)}.
$$

Since $\alpha=\frac{2k\pi}{n}$,

$$
y_{k}=\frac{\sin (2 k \pi / n)}{2(1-\cos (2 k \pi / n))}.
$$

Now let $\beta=\frac{k\pi}{n}$. Then $\alpha=2\beta$, so

$$
\begin{aligned}
y_{k}
& =\frac{\sin 2\beta}{2(1-\cos 2\beta)} \\
& =\frac{2 \sin \beta \cos \beta}{2\cdot 2 \sin ^{2} \beta} \\
& =\frac{\cos \beta}{2 \sin \beta} \\
& =\frac{1}{2}\cot \beta \\
& =\frac{1}{2}\cot \left(\frac{k\pi}{n}\right).
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus

$$
x=-\frac{1}{2}, \qquad y_{k}=\frac{\sin (2 k \pi / n)}{2(1-\cos (2 k \pi / n))}=\frac{1}{2}\cot \left(\frac{k\pi}{n}\right).
$$

> [!exercise] Exercise 29
> 55. The binomial formula. Use mathematical induction to prove that for any complex numbers $a$ and $b$ and any positive integer $n$
> 
> $$
> (a+b)^{n}=a^{n}+\binom{n}{1} a^{n-1} b^{1}+\binom{n}{2} a^{n-2} b^{2}+\cdots+\binom{n}{n-1} a^{1} b^{n-1}+b^{n},
> $$
> 
> where for $0 \leq m \leq n$, the binomial coefficient $\binom{n}{m}$ (read it " $n$ choose $m$ ") is defined by
> 
> $$
> \binom{n}{m}=\frac{n!}{(n-m)!m!},
> $$
> 
> with $0!=1$ as a convention. Using the summation notation, the binomial formula becomes
> 
> $$
> (a+b)^{n}=\sum_{m=0}^{n}\binom{n}{m} a^{n-m} b^{m}
> $$
> 
> **Hint: You should come up with two sums. Shift the index on one summation so the summand looks like the other. Pull off the $a^{n+1}$ and $b^{n+1}$ terms. Then use the identity of Pascal's triangle:**
> 
> $$
> \binom{n}{m-1}+\binom{n}{m}=\binom{n+1}{m} \cdot
> $$
> 
> 

We prove the binomial formula by induction on $n$.

For $n=1$,

$$
(a+b)^{1}=a+b=\binom{1}{0} a^{1}+\binom{1}{1} b^{1},
$$

so the formula is true in the base case.

Now assume that for some positive integer $n$,

$$
(a+b)^{n}=\sum_{m=0}^{n}\binom{n}{m} a^{n-m} b^{m}.
$$

Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
(a+b)^{n+1}
& =(a+b)(a+b)^{n} \\
& =(a+b)\sum_{m=0}^{n}\binom{n}{m} a^{n-m} b^{m} \\
& =a\sum_{m=0}^{n}\binom{n}{m} a^{n-m} b^{m}+b\sum_{m=0}^{n}\binom{n}{m} a^{n-m} b^{m} \\
& =\sum_{m=0}^{n}\binom{n}{m} a^{n+1-m} b^{m}+\sum_{m=0}^{n}\binom{n}{m} a^{n-m} b^{m+1}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Shift the index in the second sum by letting $j=m+1$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\sum_{m=0}^{n}\binom{n}{m} a^{n-m} b^{m+1}
& =\sum_{j=1}^{n+1}\binom{n}{j-1} a^{n+1-j} b^{j}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
(a+b)^{n+1}
& =\sum_{m=0}^{n}\binom{n}{m} a^{n+1-m} b^{m}+\sum_{m=1}^{n+1}\binom{n}{m-1} a^{n+1-m} b^{m} \\
& =a^{n+1}+\sum_{m=1}^{n}\binom{n}{m} a^{n+1-m} b^{m}+b^{n+1}+\sum_{m=1}^{n}\binom{n}{m-1} a^{n+1-m} b^{m} \\
& =a^{n+1}+\sum_{m=1}^{n}\left[\binom{n}{m}+\binom{n}{m-1}\right] a^{n+1-m} b^{m}+b^{n+1} \\
& =a^{n+1}+\sum_{m=1}^{n}\binom{n+1}{m} a^{n+1-m} b^{m}+b^{n+1} \\
& =\sum_{m=0}^{n+1}\binom{n+1}{m} a^{n+1-m} b^{m}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus the formula holds for $n+1$. By mathematical induction,

$$
(a+b)^{n}=\sum_{m=0}^{n}\binom{n}{m} a^{n-m} b^{m}
$$

for every positive integer $n$.

##### part (i)

Let

$$
I_{mn}=\int_{-1}^{1}T_m(x)T_n(x)\frac{dx}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}, \qquad m\ne n.
$$

Use the substitution

$$
x=\cos\theta, \qquad dx=-\sin\theta\,d\theta.
$$

As $x$ goes from $-1$ to $1$, $\theta$ goes from $\pi$ to $0$, and on $[0,\pi]$,

$$
\sqrt{1-x^2}=\sqrt{1-\cos^2\theta}=\sin\theta.
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
I_{mn}
& =\int_{\pi}^{0}T_m(\cos\theta)T_n(\cos\theta)\frac{-\sin\theta\,d\theta}{\sin\theta} \\
& =\int_{0}^{\pi}\cos(m\theta)\cos(n\theta)\,d\theta.
\end{aligned}
$$

Now use

$$
\cos(m\theta)\cos(n\theta)=\frac{1}{2}\left[\cos((m-n)\theta)+\cos((m+n)\theta)\right].
$$

Hence

$$
\begin{aligned}
I_{mn}
& =\frac{1}{2}\int_{0}^{\pi}\cos((m-n)\theta)\,d\theta+\frac{1}{2}\int_{0}^{\pi}\cos((m+n)\theta)\,d\theta \\
& =\frac{1}{2}\left[\frac{\sin((m-n)\theta)}{m-n}\right]_{0}^{\pi}
 +\frac{1}{2}\left[\frac{\sin((m+n)\theta)}{m+n}\right]_{0}^{\pi} \\
& =0,
\end{aligned}
$$

since $m\pm n$ are nonzero integers. Therefore

$$
\int_{-1}^{1}T_m(x)T_n(x)\frac{dx}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}=0 \qquad (m\ne n).
$$

##### part (j)

Let

$$
I_n=\int_{-1}^{1}[T_n(x)]^2\frac{dx}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}.
$$

Using the same substitution $x=\cos\theta$, we get

$$
\begin{aligned}
I_n
& =\int_{0}^{\pi}\cos^2(n\theta)\,d\theta.
\end{aligned}
$$

If $n=0$, then

$$
I_0=\int_0^\pi 1\,d\theta=\pi.
$$

If $n\ge1$, use

$$
\cos^2(n\theta)=\frac{1+\cos(2n\theta)}{2}.
$$

Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
I_n
& =\int_0^\pi \frac{1+\cos(2n\theta)}{2}\,d\theta \\
& =\frac{1}{2}\int_0^\pi 1\,d\theta+\frac{1}{2}\int_0^\pi \cos(2n\theta)\,d\theta \\
& =\frac{\pi}{2}+\frac{1}{2}\left[\frac{\sin(2n\theta)}{2n}\right]_0^\pi \\
& =\frac{\pi}{2}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore

$$
\int_{-1}^{1}[T_n(x)]^2\frac{dx}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}
=\begin{cases}
\pi & \text{if } n=0,\\
\frac{\pi}{2} & \text{if } n\ge1.
\end{cases}
$$
