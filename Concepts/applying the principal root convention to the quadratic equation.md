
The quadratic equation is $ax^{2} + bx + c = 0$, where $a \ne 0$. To obtain the quadratic formula, we start by normalizing the expression: Take the quadratic equation with real (or complex) coefficients and divide both sides by $a$ to make the leading coefficient 1. Isolate the quadratic and linear terms by moving the constant term to the right. Complete the square: find the quantity that turns the LHS into a perfect square $(\frac{b}{2a})$ and add it to both sides. Finally, to obtain the quadratic formula, take the square roots of both sides and solve for x.



**Derivation:**
1. Start and normalize.

Take the quadratic equation with real (or complex) coefficients

$$
a x^2+b x+c=0, \quad a \neq 0 .
$$


Divide both sides by $a$ to make the leading coefficient 1 :

$$
x^2+\frac{b}{a} x+\frac{c}{a}=0 .
$$
[[applying the principal root convention to the quadratic equation]]
2. Isolate the quadratic and linear terms.

Move the constant term to the right:

$$
x^2+\frac{b}{a} x=-\frac{c}{a} .
$$

3. Complete the square.

The quantity that completes the square is $\left(\frac{b}{2 a}\right)^2$. Add this to both sides:

$$
x^2+\frac{b}{a} x+\left(\frac{b}{2 a}\right)^2=-\frac{c}{a}+\left(\frac{b}{2 a}\right)^2 .
$$


The left-hand side is now a perfect square:

$$
\left(x+\frac{b}{2 a}\right)^2=\frac{b^2}{4 a^2}-\frac{c}{a}=\frac{b^2-4 a c}{4 a^2} .
$$

4. Take square roots.

$$
x+\frac{b}{2 a}= \pm \frac{\sqrt{b^2-4 a c}}{2 a} .
$$

(If $b^2-4 a c<0$, interpret $\sqrt{b^2-4 a c}$ as the principal square root; the " $\pm$ " supplies both conjugate solutions.)
5. Solve for $x$.

$$
x=-\frac{b}{2 a} \pm \frac{\sqrt{b^2-4 a c}}{2 a}=\frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4 a c}}{2 a} .
$$

5. Solve for $x$.

$$
x=-\frac{b}{2 a} \pm \frac{\sqrt{b^2-4 a c}}{2 a}=\frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4 a c}}{2 a} .
$$


That is the quadratic formula. The expression $\Delta=b^2-4 a c$ is the discriminant, which classifies the roots: $\Delta>0$ gives two distinct real roots, $\Delta=0$ gives one double root, and $\Delta<0$ gives a conjugate pair of complex roots $\frac{-b \pm i \sqrt{4 a c-b^2}}{2 a}$.



# Example


Solution Applying the quadratic formula for the first root, we find

$$
x_1=\frac{-1+\sqrt{-3}}{2}=\frac{-1+i \sqrt{3}}{2}
$$


Since the roots are conjugate, we immediately have the other root,

$$
x_2=\frac{-1-i \sqrt{3}}{2}
$$
